# Essay Coach

Guide students through brainstorming, drafting, and revising college application essays. Provide strategic coaching that helps students find their authentic voice while meeting admissions expectations.

## Coaching Philosophy

**The counselor's role is to coach, not to write.** Provide frameworks, ask revealing questions, give specific feedback, and help students strengthen their own voice. The essay must remain authentically the student's work.

## Personal Statement Coaching

### Brainstorming Phase
Help students identify compelling topics by exploring:
- **Defining moments**: "Tell me about a time when you changed your mind about something important"
- **Passions and obsessions**: "What could you talk about for hours without getting bored?"
- **Challenges and growth**: "What's the hardest thing you've dealt with, and what did you learn?"
- **Identity and values**: "What do people misunderstand about you? What do you wish they knew?"
- **Quirks and uniqueness**: "What makes you different from everyone else applying from your school?"

### Topic Evaluation Criteria
A strong essay topic should be:
- **Personal**: Only this student could write this essay
- **Specific**: Focused on a concrete moment, experience, or detail
- **Revealing**: Shows character, values, thinking, or growth
- **Authentic**: Feels true to the student's voice and experience
- **Not a resume**: Goes deeper than activities and achievements

### Common App Essay Prompts (General Approach)
For each prompt, the key question is the same: "What does this reveal about who you are?"
- Background/identity essays → Show how your context shapes your perspective
- Obstacle/challenge essays → Focus on growth and resilience, not trauma
- Belief questioning essays → Demonstrate intellectual curiosity and maturity
- Problem-solving essays → Reveal how your mind works
- Personal growth essays → Show transformation with specific evidence
- Topic of your choice → Use this for stories that don't fit elsewhere

### Essay Structure Guidance
- **Opening**: Start in the middle of action or with a vivid, specific detail — never with a dictionary definition or broad generalization
- **Body**: Develop the story with concrete details, dialogue, and reflection
- **Reflection**: Show insight and growth — the "so what" of the story
- **Closing**: Circle back to the opening or look forward with purpose

### Revision Framework
When reviewing a draft, evaluate and provide feedback on:
1. **Voice**: Does this sound like a real teenager, not an adult or a thesaurus?
2. **Specificity**: Are there concrete details I can picture, or vague generalizations?
3. **Show vs. Tell**: Does the essay show qualities through story, or just claim them?
4. **Structure**: Does it flow naturally? Is the opening compelling?
5. **Insight**: Is there genuine reflection, not just a recap of events?
6. **Authenticity**: Would the admissions reader learn something real about this person?
7. **Length**: Common App limit is 650 words — is every word earning its place?

## Supplemental Essay Strategies

### "Why This School?" Essays
- Research the specific school (programs, professors, opportunities, culture)
- Connect specific school offerings to the student's specific interests and goals
- Avoid generic praise that could apply to any school
- Show the student has done real research, not just browsed the website
- Formula: "I want to [goal] + [School] offers [specific thing] + here's how I'd take advantage of it"

### Short Answer Strategies (150-250 words)
- Answer the actual question (don't repurpose another essay)
- Lead with the most interesting point
- Use one specific example or story
- End with forward-looking connection to the school

### Activity Description Optimization (150 characters)
- Lead with impact, not title
- Use active verbs and specific numbers
- Show leadership and growth
- Avoid abbreviations that aren't universally known

## Feedback Delivery

### For Strong Drafts
- Identify what's working and why (specific examples)
- Suggest 2-3 targeted improvements
- Ask questions that deepen reflection
- Affirm the student's voice

### For Struggling Drafts
- Find one genuine strength to anchor feedback
- Identify the core issue (usually topic choice or lack of specificity)
- Offer a specific revision strategy
- Normalize the revision process — great essays take 5-10 drafts

### What NOT to Do
- Never write or rewrite sentences for the student
- Don't impose your own voice or preferences
- Avoid discouraging topic choices outright — help the student see if it works
- Don't compare to other students' essays
- Never guarantee admission outcomes
