# Recommendation Letters and Recommender Relationships

Strong recommendation letters provide insight into who you are beyond test scores and grades. Strategic selection of recommenders and thoughtful preparation amplifies the impact of these critical documents.

## Choosing Your Recommenders

**Ideal Recommenders**
Select teachers or counselors who:
- Know you well in an academic context (at least one semester in class)
- Can speak to your intellectual curiosity and growth
- Have directly observed your character and interpersonal skills
- Are invested in your success and willing to write substantively
- Can address specific examples and stories about you

**Strong Recommender Selection Strategies**

**Core Teachers:** Select from different subject areas if possible. A math teacher and English teacher offers diverse perspectives on your abilities. Choose teachers who taught you in:
- Recent years (junior or senior preferred)
- Challenging courses where you excelled or demonstrated growth
- Classes involving intellectual engagement, participation, or projects

**The "Deep Knowledge" Teacher:** Choose at least one teacher who knows you exceptionally well. This might be a teacher for whom you took multiple courses, who advised a club or team you led, or who supervised independent research.

**School Counselor:** Most applications require a counselor recommendation. Your school counselor documents overall character, academic trajectory, obstacles overcome, and unique contributions.

**Optional Academic Recommenders:** Some schools accept additional recommendations beyond required ones. Use optional slots for:
- Teachers in your intended major field
- Teachers who observed specific talents
- Research mentors or lab supervisors

**When NOT to Request from Someone:**
- Teachers who taught you more than 3 years ago (memory fades)
- Teachers who gave you a low grade (unless major improvement story)
- Teachers who don't know you well
- Adults who might overstate or understate your abilities

## Requesting Recommendations Strategically

**Timing: Ask 4-6 Weeks Before Deadline**

Send requests in the summer before senior year or early fall for November/January deadlines. This provides adequate time without rush. For February/March deadlines, ask by December.

**In-Person Request vs. Email**
- **Best:** Ask in person. Shows respect and allows real conversation
- **Acceptable:** Email if in-person meeting impossible, but follow with personal conversation
- **Avoid:** Text, vague requests, or requesting anonymously through portal

**What to Include in Your Request**

"I'm applying to college this fall and was wondering if you'd be willing to write a recommendation letter for me. I really valued [specific class/experience], especially when we [specific example]. Would you be comfortable doing this? I can provide more information about my colleges and goals if helpful."

**Give Them a Choice**
Always frame as a question, not a demand: "Would you be willing...?" If they hesitate, respect it. A reluctant recommender won't write a strong letter.

**Make It Easy: Provide a Brag Sheet**

Create a one-page document with:
- Your name and graduation year
- Brief statement of your college goals and intended major
- 4-6 specific examples of your work/character in their class:
  - Specific assignment or project you completed well
  - Example of intellectual curiosity you demonstrated
  - Leadership or collaboration you showed
  - Challenge you overcame
  - Personal quality they observed
- List of colleges and application deadlines
- Instructions for electronic submission (if applicable)

**Brag Sheet Template**

```
RECOMMENDATION LETTER INFORMATION
Student Name: [Your Name]
Graduation Year: 20XX
Class: [Teacher's Class Name, Year Taken]

COLLEGE GOALS

   Example: "When we wrote the research paper on [topic], I spent extra time investigating [specific aspect] and discovered [insight]."

   Example: "During discussions about [topic], I always asked thoughtful follow-up questions and connected ideas to [related concept]."
```

## Teacher Letter vs. Counselor Letter: Key Differences

**Teacher Recommendation (Academic)**
- Addresses academic abilities, classroom performance, intellectual engagement
- Provides specific examples from class
- Discusses critical thinking, collaboration, growth
- Best coming from recent classes
- Usually 1-2 letters required

**Counselor Recommendation (Holistic)**
- Addresses overall character, maturity, interpersonal skills
- Documents personal challenges, resilience, leadership
- Places you in broader school context
- Synthesizes four years of observations
- Almost universally required
- Usually 1 letter required

Both matter equally. A strong academic recommendation and a strong counselor recommendation together create comprehensive picture.

## Managing the Recommendation Process

**Create a Tracking Sheet**

| Recommender | Subject | Deadline | Portal Used | Submitted? | Reminder Date |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ms. Chen (English) | Common App | Jan 1 | Common App | [ ] | Dec 10 |
| Mr. Lee (Science) | Cornell/Princeton | Jan 1 | Coalition | [ ] | Dec 8 |
| Counselor Jones | All schools | Varies | Various | [ ] | Nov 15 |

**Gentle Reminders**
- First reminder: 2 weeks before deadline (casual email or in-person: "Just checking in—still on track?")
- Second reminder: 1 week before deadline (more specific: "The Common App deadline is January 1st")
- Final reminder: 2-3 days before (time-sensitive and specific to portal)

**Follow-Up Etiquette**

After letters are submitted:
- Thank recommenders sincerely in person or with written note
- Update them on your college acceptances (they're invested in you)
- Mention if you're attending their recommended school
- If you see them in college, visit and share your experience

## Optional Recommendations: When to Use Them

Consider submitting optional recommendations for:
- **Demonstrated expertise:** A music teacher if you're an accomplished musician
- **Research or project mentor:** Someone who supervised independent work
- **Coach or extracurricular advisor:** If sport/activity is central to your profile
- **Non-teacher who knows you well:** Employer, volunteer supervisor, club leader (if schools allow)

**When NOT to submit optional letters:**
- You don't have a compelling reason to add it
- The recommender doesn't know you well
- It might duplicate what your required letters say
- Schools specify a maximum number of letters

Colleges prefer quality over quantity. One extra strong letter beats two mediocre ones.

## Red Flags and How to Avoid Them

**Generic Letters**
Problem: Letter sounds like it could be about anyone
Solution: Provide specific examples in your brag sheet; mention personal interactions unique to you

**Late Submissions**
Problem: Missing deadline damages your application
Solution: Remind recommenders 2 weeks, 1 week, and 2 days before deadline

**Weak Recommender Choice**
Problem: Teacher who doesn't know you well writes cursory letter
Solution: Choose recommenders you've invested in relationships with; take their classes seriously

**Over-Hyped Expectations**
Problem: Expecting letter to "fix" weak grades or test scores
Solution: Recommendations add dimension but won't overcome major weaknesses; focus on overall profile

**Lack of Communication**
Problem: Recommender doesn't know anything about your colleges or goals
Solution: Provide brag sheet with specific college list and your academic interests

## Final Recommendations Checklist

**Before Requesting Letters:**
- [ ] Identified 2-3 strong teacher recommenders
- [ ] Confirmed your school counselor will write a letter
- [ ] Decided on any optional recommenders

**In Your Requests:**
- [ ] Asked in person, 4-6 weeks before deadline
- [ ] Provided brag sheet with specific examples
- [ ] Clearly communicated college list and deadlines
- [ ] Made it easy for them to submit (portal instructions)

**After Requests:**
- [ ] Created tracking sheet for all recommenders
- [ ] Set reminder dates (2 weeks, 1 week, 2 days before deadline)
- [ ] Verified letters submitted to all colleges
- [ ] Thanked recommenders appropriately

**For Strongest Letters:**
- [ ] Choose recommenders who know you deeply
- [ ] Provide specific examples they can reference
- [ ] Build relationships in their classes before senior year
- [ ] Show genuine interest in their subject
