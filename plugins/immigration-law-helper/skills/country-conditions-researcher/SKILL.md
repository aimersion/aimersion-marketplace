# Country Conditions Researcher Skill

Asylum cases require comprehensive documentation of country conditions and credible evidence that applicant faces persecution based on protected ground. The Country Conditions Researcher skill provides asylum practitioners with systematic source identification, research methodology, and legal framework for connecting general country conditions to applicant's specific claim.

## Core Capabilities

### Authoritative Research Sources

Immigration courts require evidence from authoritative, independent sources. Country conditions research should draw from:

**United States Government Sources:**

- **State Department Country Reports on Human Rights Practices:** Annual reports published February each year; comprehensive review of human rights situation in every country
  - Coverage: All countries with U.S. diplomatic relations
  - Format: Detailed narrative sections covering civil/political rights, freedoms, worker rights, specific abuses
  - Reliability: High (U.S. government official source)
  - Link: www.state.gov/reports-bureau-of-democracy-human-rights-and-labor/country-reports-human-rights-practices

- **State Department International Religious Freedom Reports:** Annual reports on religious freedom and persecution by country
  - Coverage: Focus on religious minorities, persecution, government discrimination
  - Format: Detailed sections on religious freedom status, persecution patterns, government responsibility
  - Reliability: High
  - Link: www.state.gov/religiousfreedomreports

- **Congressional Research Service (CRS) Reports:** Research reports prepared for Congress
  - Coverage: Specialized country analyses, human rights issues, political situations
  - Format: Focused analysis on specific topics or countries
  - Reliability: High

- **Bureau of Consular Affairs Immigrant Visa Country-Specific Information:** Consular guidance on country conditions affecting visa processing
  - Coverage: Information on civil unrest, security concerns, document availability
  - Format: Narrative descriptions
  - Reliability: High (identifies documented issues affecting visa processing)

**International Intergovernmental Organizations:**

- **UNHCR (United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees):**
  - UNHCR Eligibility Guidelines: Detailed guidance on asylum claims by country and persecution type
  - UNHCR Position Papers: Analysis of whether claims warrant protection in specific countries
  - UNHCR Fact-Finding Missions: Reports on country conditions in crisis situations
  - Reliability: Very high (international protection agency)
  - Link: www.unhcr.org

- **EASO/EUAA (European Asylum Support Office/European Union Agency for Asylum):**
  - Country of Origin Information (COI) reports: Comprehensive country condition reports
  - Country Guidance: Asylum guidance for specific countries and issues
  - Reliability: Very high (European asylum authority; highly respected methodology)
  - Link: www.euaa.europa.eu

**Independent International Human Rights Organizations:**

- **Human Rights Watch:** Highly respected international NGO
  - Coverage: Detailed country reports on human rights violations
  - Methodology: Investigative reporting, interviews, documentation
  - Reliability: Very high
  - Link: www.hrw.org

- **Amnesty International:** Major international human rights organization
  - Coverage: Annual reports on human rights; specialized country analyses
  - Methodology: Field investigations, documentation
  - Reliability: Very high
  - Link: www.amnesty.org

- **Freedom House:** U.S.-based NGO focused on freedom and democracy
  - Coverage: Annual freedom assessments by country
  - Methodology: Detailed analysis of political and civil rights
  - Reliability: High
  - Link: www.freedomhouse.org

- **International Federation of Human Rights (FIDH):** Global human rights network
  - Coverage: Specialized reports on specific human rights issues
  - Reliability: High

**Other Relevant Sources:**

- **Refugee Documentation Network:** Maintains library of country conditions documentation
- **American Immigration Council:** Provides resources on country conditions for immigration practitioners
- **Academic Journals:** Journal of Refugee Studies, Refugee Survey Quarterly
- **News Archives:** Major news sources (New York Times, BBC, Reuters, Associated Press, local news)

## Asylum Legal Framework

Asylum cases require meeting five distinct legal elements:

### 1. Persecution - Level and Nature of Harm

**Legal Standard:**
Persecution is serious harm imposed because of protected ground characteristic. Not all hardship constitutes persecution; must be severe.

**Harm Levels:**

**Clear Persecution:**
- Physical violence (beatings, torture, sexual assault, attempted murder)
- Imprisonment or detention with harsh conditions
- Death threats with credible likelihood of execution
- Rape or other sexual violence
- Forced military service with threat of execution for desertion
- Forced labor or human trafficking
- Destruction of home/property by government or government-tolerated actors
- Confiscation of business/livelihood because of protected ground

**Arguable Persecution:**
- Arrest and detention (duration and conditions matter)
- Threats of serious harm
- Harassment by government causing difficulty leaving home
- Denial of government services/documents because of protected ground
- Denial of employment or business license because of protected ground
- Discrimination combined with other hardship

**Not Persecution (Generally):**
- General crime or gang violence (unless targeting specific group)
- General economic hardship
- Domestic violence (unless government-tolerated/acquiescing)
- Generalized poverty or lack of services
- Private discrimination not involving government
- Failure to provide medical care (unless persecution)

**Government Responsibility:**
Persecution must be by government OR by private actors with government acquiescence/tolerance:
- Direct government action (police, military, officials)
- Private actor (gang, vigilante) tolerated by government's unwillingness to protect
- Private actor carrying out implicit government policy

---

### 2. Protected Ground - Five Categories

Persecution must be based on one of five protected grounds:

**A. Race:**
- Persecution based on ethnic background
- Persecution of minority racial groups
- Persecution of mixed-race individuals
- Examples: Rohingya in Myanmar (persecution based on ethnicity); Kurds in Turkey (ethnic persecution)

**B. Religion:**
- Persecution based on religious belief or practice
- Persecution of religious minorities
- Persecution for conversion or change in faith
- Examples: Yazidis in Iraq (religious persecution); Uyghur Muslims in China (religious persecution)

**C. Nationality:**
- Persecution based on citizenship or national origin
- Persecution of ethnic groups identified with foreign nation
- Examples: Afghans in Pakistan (persecution based on national origin); Palestinians in certain areas (national origin persecution)

**D. Political Opinion:**
- Persecution based on political belief, affiliation, or activity
- Persecution for speaking against government
- Persecution for suspected political opposition
- Persecution for working for democracy or human rights
- Examples: Activists in authoritarian countries; democracy advocates; human rights defenders

**E. Particular Social Group:**
- Persecution based on membership in social group with immutable characteristic
- Must be group with shared characteristic others cannot change
- Common PSGs:
  - **Women (Gender-based PSG):** Persecution of women as group (FGM, forced marriage, domestic violence in gender-discriminatory context)
  - **LGBTQ+ Persons:** Persecution based on sexual orientation or gender identity
  - **Family-based Groups:** Persecution based on family membership (e.g., family of political prisoner, family of murder victim, family of defector)
  - **Ethnic or Tribal Groups:** Persecution based on ethnic/tribal identity distinct from "race"
  - **Landless Peasants, Indigenous Groups, Caste-Based Groups:** Persecution based on socioeconomic or caste status
  - **Children:** Persecution based on status as child (child soldiers, child labor)

---

### 3. Nexus - Connection Between Persecution and Protected Ground

**Legal Requirement:**
Applicant must demonstrate persecution is "on account of" protected ground. Persecution cannot be for other reasons unrelated to protected ground.

**Strong Nexus Examples:**
- Police target religious minority for arrest
- Government denies job because of political opinion
- Gang targets ethnic minority for violence
- Government persecutes women's rights activists
- Military forcibly recruits from ethnic minority group
- Religious minority denied passport/documents

**Weak Nexus Examples (Problematic):**
- Criminal arrested and imprisoned (no protected ground shown)
- Business owner facing debt and business closure (general economic hardship)
- Individual harmed by general political instability (no protection ground shown)
- Person facing violence but motivation unclear or unrelated to protected ground
- Victim of gang violence from same ethnic background (personal vendetta, not group persecution)

**Nexus Analysis:**
For each persecution example, ask: "Why did persecutor target applicant?"
- If answer is protected ground characteristic: strong nexus
- If answer is unrelated to protected ground: weak nexus
- If answer is personal (personal vendetta, business dispute, romantic jealousy): no protection

---

### 4. Well-Founded Fear - Likelihood of Future Persecution

**Legal Standard:**
Applicant must demonstrate reasonable possibility (not mere speculation) that persecution will occur if applicant returns to country.

**Factors Supporting Well-Founded Fear:**
- Prior persecution experience (strongest evidence)
- Similar persecution of others in same group
- Applicant's identifying characteristics that would trigger persecution
- Pattern and practice in country of persecution targeting applicant's group
- Government's explicit policy targeting group
- Credible reports of recent persecution of group
- Applicant's profile (activist, dissident, minority member) making him/her vulnerable

**Factors Undermining Well-Founded Fear:**
- Applicant's characteristics no longer visible or known
- Persecution stopped in country
- Government changed policy toward group
- Alternative routes to safety exist
- Applicant able to relocate within country safely

---

### 5. Unable or Unwilling to Return - Government Protection

**Legal Requirement:**
Applicant must be unable to get protection from government of persecution country OR government is unwilling to provide protection.

**Government Unwillingness:**
- Government persecutes applicant (clearest case)
- Government condones private persecution (gang violence, domestic violence)
- Government unable to maintain order (state collapse, civil war)
- Police corrupt; cannot provide protection
- Applicant previously sought government protection without success

**Government Ability to Protect:**
- Police effective and accessible
- Courts function and enforce law
- Government has monopoly on force
- Government willing to intervene

**Alternative Internal Relocation:**
If applicant can relocate within country and obtain government protection:
- Burden shifts to applicant to show relocation not feasible
- Applicant must show:
  - Relocation area unsafe or without government presence
  - Applicant identifiable to persecutors in new location
  - Relocation would be unduly harsh (health, family, etc.)

## Country Conditions Research Methodology

### Identifying Relevant Country Conditions

**Step 1: Identify Protected Ground and Persecution Type**

For each claim, identify:
- What protected ground applies?
- What specific persecution is feared?
- Who would perpetrate persecution?
- Is government involved?

Example: "Applicant fears persecution as Kurdish political activist in Turkey; fears police arrest and detention based on political opinion and nationality"

---

**Step 2: Gather General Country Conditions Evidence**

Obtain overview of country's human rights situation:
- State Department Human Rights Report (comprehensive overview)
- UNHCR Eligibility Guidelines (asylum protection analysis)
- News articles (recent events, political situation)

This establishes country context and credibility factors.

---

**Step 3: Research Specific Group Persecution**

Research persecution patterns for applicant's specific protected ground:
- How common is persecution of this group?
- What forms does persecution take?
- Are government officials involved?
- Is persecution systematic or isolated incidents?
- Which government agencies are involved?

Sources:
- Human Rights Watch country reports
- Amnesty International country assessments
- EASO country guidance
- Academic journals
- News archives focusing on specific group

---

**Step 4: Research Credibility Factors**

Gather evidence supporting applicant's credibility:
- Is applicant's story consistent with documented patterns?
- Do applicant's identifying characteristics align with persecuted group's profile?
- Are applicant's claimed experiences consistent with documented forms of persecution?

---

**Step 5: Compile Evidence with Source Citations**

For each piece of evidence:
- Identify source (author, organization, date published)
- Provide URL/citation
- Summarize relevant finding
- Explain how evidence supports applicant's claim

## Country Conditions Research Organization

### Research Memo Structure

**1. Executive Summary**
- One-page overview of country conditions and applicant's vulnerability
- Summary of persecution evidence
- Credibility assessment
- Bottom line: Is country safe for applicant's return?

**2. General Country Conditions**
- Political system (democracy, autocracy, transitional)
- Stability (stable, unstable, conflict, post-conflict)
- Rule of law (functional courts, police, corruption level)
- General human rights situation
- Recent political events relevant to claim

**3. Protected Ground Analysis**
- Status of applicant's protected ground in country
- Government policy toward group
- Discrimination laws
- Enforcement patterns

**4. Persecution of Applicant's Specific Group**
- Evidence of persecution of applicant's protected group
- Forms of persecution documented
- Prevalence/systematic nature
- Government involvement
- Recent cases of persecution

**5. Government Ability to Protect**
- Police effectiveness
- Court system functionality
- Government willingness to enforce law
- Corruption level
- Ability of group to obtain police protection

**6. Credibility Analysis**
- Applicant's story consistency with documented country conditions
- Applicant's profile alignment with persecuted group
- Documented forms of persecution applicant experienced
- Pattern and practice supporting applicant's experience
- Factors supporting credibility

**7. Conclusion**
- Whether country conditions support well-founded fear
- Whether government unable/unwilling to provide protection
- Overall assessment of asylum claim viability

## Common Country Conditions Issues by Protected Ground

### Political Opinion Claims

**Key Evidence to Gather:**
- Government persecution of political activists
- Whether government monitors political activity
- Whether activists face arrest/detention
- Documentation of specific arrests of activists
- State Department human rights reports on political freedoms
- NEWS of recent arrests or persecution

**Common Problems:**
- Applicant's claim of "political opinion" vague or overstated
- No documented pattern of persecution of applicants' specific political views
- Government has democratic institutions allowing free speech
- Court system functions and protects political expression rights

**Strengthening Claims:**
- Document specific political activities applicant engaged in
- Find news reports of government crackdown on similar activities
- Obtain UNHCR guidance on political opinion claims in that country
- Research government's track record prosecuting political opponents
- Document applicant's visibility/profile making persecution likely

---

### Particular Social Group Claims (Women, LGBTQ+, Family-based)

**Gender-Based PSG (Women Fleeing Gender Violence):**
- Must show persecution of women as group (not just personal domestic violence)
- Evidence needed: Government failure to protect women from violence; gender-discriminatory laws; pattern of gender violence; government acquiescence

**LGBTQ+ PSG:**
- Must show LGBTQ+ persons targeted for persecution based on sexual orientation/gender identity
- Evidence needed: Criminalization of homosexuality; police persecution of LGBTQ+ persons; violence against LGBTQ+ community; government failure to protect

**Family-Based PSG (Family of Government Official, Defector, Etc.):**
- Must show government targets family members of politically significant persons
- Evidence needed: Pattern of government targeting family members; documentation of specific family persecution; applicant's family member's significance to government

---

### Religious Freedom Claims

**Evidence to Gather:**
- Government policy on religious minorities
- International Religious Freedom Report
- Documentation of religious persecution in country
- Government restrictions on worship
- Persecution of specific religious group applicant belongs to
- Evidence of government's enforcement of restrictions

**Key Sources:**
- State Department International Religious Freedom Report (most important for religious claims)
- Human Rights Watch religious freedom reports
- Religious persecution documentation from religious organizations
- News coverage of religious persecution

## Asylum Case Preparation with Country Conditions

### Integrating Country Conditions into Case Strategy

**For Credibility:**
- Use country conditions to establish applicant's story is consistent with documented patterns
- Show applicant's experiences align with known forms of persecution
- Address any implausibilities by showing conditions support applicant's account

**For Nexus:**
- Show persecution applicant fears is specifically targeted at applicant's protected group
- Document government or tolerated private actor focus on group
- Establish applicant's status as member of persecuted group

**For Well-Founded Fear:**
- Show current conditions still persist
- Show recent persecution of group members
- Establish that applicant's profile creates vulnerability
- Document government changes (or lack thereof) since persecution occurred

**For Government Inability/Unwillingness:**
- Show police ineffectiveness or corruption
- Document government's role in persecution or toleration
- Show government's policy against applicant's group
- Address any government capacity to protect (and explain why applicant unlikely to receive protection)

## Expert Declarations in Asylum Cases

Country conditions research often supports expert declarations by country specialists, anthropologists, human rights experts, or psychologists.

**When to Use Expert:**
- Complex country conditions requiring specialized knowledge
- Applicant's story requires cultural/religious context
- Medical evaluation of torture/trauma needed (for credibility/corroboration)
- Political situation in country needs expert analysis
- Family-based social group needs anthropological analysis

**Expert Declaration Content:**
- Introduction of expert's qualifications
- Methodology for country research
- Findings on country conditions
- Application to applicant's specific case
- Expert's opinion on credibility and viability of claim

**Strengthens Asylum Case By:**
- Corroborating applicant's account with independent expert analysis
- Addressing gaps in applicant's memory/knowledge
- Providing cultural context for applicant's experience
- Legitimizing applicant's account through professional validation
