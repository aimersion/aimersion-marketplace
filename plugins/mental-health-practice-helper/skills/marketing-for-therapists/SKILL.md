# Marketing for Therapists

Blog posts, social media content, and profiles that attract ideal clients while maintaining ethical boundaries.

## The Therapist Marketing Challenge

Most therapists were never trained in marketing. The result: either they don't market at all (and struggle to fill their caseload) or they market in ways that feel inauthentic, clinical, or accidentally cross ethical lines. The sweet spot is content that's genuinely helpful, speaks directly to the person who needs you, and positions you as a warm, knowledgeable guide.

## Ethical Marketing Boundaries

**Always:**
- Use accurate credentials and license numbers
- Speak in educational terms, not diagnostic terms
- Use "you" language that connects with the reader
- Include appropriate disclaimers ("This is not a substitute for professional treatment")
- Protect client privacy absolutely (even anonymized stories carry risk)
- Follow state licensing board advertising rules

**Never:**
- Promise specific outcomes ("I will cure your anxiety")
- Use testimonials that create expectation of results (check state rules — some states prohibit client testimonials entirely)
- Diagnose the reader ("If you have these 5 symptoms, you have PTSD")
- Create urgency through fear ("Without treatment, your condition will only get worse")
- Share client information in any form, including "composite" or "fictional" case studies that are clearly based on real clients
- Disparage other therapists or therapeutic approaches
- Claim superiority ("I'm the best therapist in [city]")

## Content That Attracts Ideal Clients

### The Psychology Today Profile (Highest ROI for most practices)

This is where potential clients find you. Optimize it ruthlessly.

**Profile statement structure:**
1. **Opening hook** — Name the experience the reader is having (not the diagnosis). "You've been carrying this alone for a long time." Not: "I treat depression and anxiety."
2. **Validation** — Normalize their experience and their decision to seek help
3. **Your approach** — How therapy with you works, in feeling terms ("In our work together, you'll find a space where..." not "I utilize evidence-based CBT techniques")
4. **Your style** — Warm? Direct? Gentle? Challenging? Tell them what it's like to be in the room with you
5. **Specialties in context** — Don't just list "anxiety, depression, trauma." Say "I especially love working with [specific population] who are dealing with [specific experience]"
6. **Call to action** — Make it easy to reach out

### Blog Content Strategy

Write about what your ideal clients are searching for at 2 AM:

**High-traffic topics:**
- "Am I depressed or just sad?"
- "Signs you might need couples counseling"
- "What happens in the first therapy session"
- "How to know if your therapist is a good fit"
- "Is it normal to feel worse after starting therapy?"
- "[Your specialty] explained in plain language"
- "How to support someone with [condition]"

**SEO approach:**
- Target "[specialty] therapist in [city]" keywords
- Answer questions in the title format ("How Long Does Therapy Take?")
- Write 800-1200 words per post
- Include a clear CTA (contact form, phone number, booking link)
- Post consistently (2-4x/month)

### Social Media for Therapists

**What works:**
- Psychoeducation in accessible, non-clinical language
- Normalizing statements that make people feel less alone
- Gentle myth-busting about therapy
- Self-compassion and self-awareness content
- Resource recommendations (books, podcasts, apps)
- "Things I wish more people knew about [topic]"
- Behind-the-scenes of running a practice (humanizing, not clinical)

**Platform priorities:**
1. Instagram (highest engagement for therapy content)
2. Psychology Today (highest conversion)
3. Google Business Profile (local SEO)
4. LinkedIn (if targeting professional/corporate clients)
5. TikTok (if comfortable with video and targeting younger demographics)
