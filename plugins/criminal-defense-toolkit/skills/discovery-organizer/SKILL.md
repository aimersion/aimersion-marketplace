# Discovery Organizer Skill

As a criminal discovery organizer, you systematically track all evidence in a case, organize disclosure materials for accessibility, identify missing or withheld discovery, flag Brady and Giglio obligations, and review chain of custody to ensure evidence integrity.

## Understanding Discovery in Criminal Practice

Discovery in criminal cases is fundamentally different from civil discovery. The prosecution has broad discovery obligations, and the defense has increasing reciprocal obligations. Understanding these obligations is critical for case management.

## Prosecution's Discovery Obligations

**Brady Material (Exculpatory Evidence):**
The prosecution has a constitutional obligation under Brady v. Maryland to disclose all evidence that is favorable to the defendant and material to guilt or punishment. This includes:

- **Exculpatory Evidence**: Evidence that directly tends to show innocence or negate a required element
- **Impeaching Evidence**: Evidence that could be used to impeach prosecution witnesses (prior convictions, dishonesty, credibility issues)
- **Mitigating Evidence**: Evidence relevant to sentencing that might reduce punishment
- **Evidence of Government Misconduct**: Evidence showing police or prosecutor dishonesty, evidence planting, or constitutional violations

The critical standard is "materiality": the evidence must be such that "there is a reasonable probability that, had the evidence been disclosed to the defense, the result of the proceeding would have been different."

**Common Brady Material:**
- Witness statements inconsistent with prosecution theory
- Prior inconsistent statements by prosecution witness
- Criminal history of police officers or informants
- Evidence of mistaken identification
- Exculpatory physical evidence
- Evidence of alternative suspects
- Victim statements inconsistent with prosecution theory
- 911 calls, dispatch recordings, body camera footage showing different account
- Lab reports showing inconclusive results or contamination

**Giglio Material (Credibility Evidence):**
The prosecution must disclose evidence impeaching government witnesses, including:

- **Informant Deals**: Any cooperation agreement, immunity, payment, or leniency with informant
- **Prior Dishonesty**: Prior convictions for dishonesty, perjury allegations, disciplinary findings
- **Motive to Lie**: Financial incentives, bias against defendant, pending criminal charges against informant
- **Bias**: Personal conflicts, romantic involvement, rivalries with victim or defendant
- **Reliability History**: Pattern of dishonest testimony, prior false accusations

The obligation extends to police officer credibility issues: Brady and Giglio material includes officer dishonesty, use of force issues, or credibility problems that could affect the officer's testimony.

**Work-Product Exception:**
The prosecution may withhold attorney work product (attorney notes, mental impressions, trial strategy). But factual information underlying work product must be disclosed.

**Adequate Notice of Witnesses:**
The prosecution must provide reasonable notice of witnesses, including:
- Witness name and contact information (or reason for withholding)
- Type of testimony expected
- Criminal history if relevant to credibility

**Pretrial Testing and Reports:**
- Autopsy reports
- Medical reports
- Lab reports (toxicology, DNA, fingerprints, drugs, explosives, firearms)
- Accident reconstruction
- Financial records and analysis
- Expert reports

## Prosecution's Continuing Obligation

Discovery obligations are not one-time. They continue throughout the case:

- **Brady/Giglio duty continues**: If impeaching evidence comes to prosecution's attention at any time, it must be disclosed
- **Witness list updates**: As new witnesses are identified or statements taken, they must be disclosed
- **Lab results**: As testing is completed, results must be disclosed even if unfavorable to prosecution
- **Late-Discovered Evidence**: If prosecution discovers exculpatory evidence late, it must still be disclosed

## Defense Obligations (Reciprocal Discovery)

Modern criminal procedure includes reciprocal discovery obligations on the defense:

**Notice of Intent to Introduce Evidence:**
- Notice of alibi witnesses (name, location at crime time)
- Notice of expert witnesses (name, qualifications, expected testimony)
- Notice of character witnesses

**Expert Disclosures:**
- Expert name and qualifications
- Subject matter of expected testimony
- Substance of expected testimony
- Reports and materials reviewed

**Reciprocal Brady Obligation:**
Some jurisdictions impose reciprocal Brady obligation on defense: defense must disclose exculpatory evidence if known.

## Organizing Discovery: The Master Discovery Log

The foundation of discovery management is a master discovery log that tracks every piece of evidence.

**Key Information for Each Item:**
1. **Unique ID**: Assign each item unique identifier (e.g., "State Ex. 1", "Defense Ex. A")
2. **Description**: Brief description of evidence (e.g., "police report from 3/15/2024", "handwritten note found in defendant's apartment")
3. **Type**: Physical evidence, documentary evidence, digital evidence, testimonial
4. **Source**: Who provided the evidence (police, prosecution, defendant investigation)
5. **Date Received**: When was evidence received by defense
6. **Location**: Current physical location (with attorney, with expert, in storage)
7. **Custody/Chain of Custody**: Record of who has possession (ensure proper chain)
8. **Legal Significance**: Brady material? Giglio? Impeachment? Exculpatory? Aggravating?
9. **Authentication Status**: Has evidence been authenticated (especially for physical evidence)?
10. **Expert Review**: Has evidence been reviewed by defense expert? What's their finding?
11. **Status**: Under review, reviewed, pending testing, in expert possession

**Organization by Category:**

*Physical Evidence:*
- Weapons, ammunition
- Drugs and drug paraphernalia
- Stolen property
- Clothing, fibers
- DNA evidence
- Trace evidence (hair, fingernails, etc.)
- Vehicles
- Tools or implements

*Documentary Evidence:*
- Police reports and supplemental reports
- Witness statements and interviews
- Victim statements
- Medical records
- Financial records
- Communications (texts, emails, social media)
- Contracts or business documents
- Permits, licenses, records

*Digital Evidence:*
- Surveillance footage (video, photos)
- Body camera footage
- Cell phone records and GPS data
- Computer forensics
- Cloud storage data
- Social media posts
- Video or audio recordings

*Testimonial Evidence:*
- Witness list
- Detective reports
- Lab technician reports
- Expert reports
- Confidential informant information

## Identifying Missing Discovery

Systematically identify what discovery has NOT been received but should have been:

**Create Missing Discovery Checklist:**

*Standard Items for Most Cases:*
- Police incident reports
- Detective follow-up reports
- All supplemental reports
- Officer narratives
- Dispatch recordings and dispatch logs
- 911 calls and recordings
- Body camera footage
- Vehicle camera footage
- Surveillance footage from businesses, residences, traffic cameras
- Cell phone location records (if relevant)
- Cell phone content (calls, texts, photos if relevant)
- Vehicle GPS or tracking data
- Financial records (if relevant)

*Lab and Expert Reports:*
- Toxicology report
- DNA analysis
- Fingerprint analysis
- Forensic examination reports
- Trace evidence analysis
- Drug analysis and field test results
- Ballistics analysis
- Accident reconstruction (if applicable)
- Medical examiner report and autopsy results

*Witness Information:*
- Witness interview reports
- Witness criminal history (Brady material)
- Witness prior inconsistent statements
- Witness contact information

*Discoverable Criminal History:*
- Defendant's criminal history (prosecution will use at trial)
- Prosecution witness criminal history (Brady/Giglio material)
- Police officer history (Brady/Giglio material: credibility issues, prior dishonesty)
- Confidential informant history (Brady/Giglio material)

*Brady/Giglio Specific:*
- Police officer personnel files (to the extent relevant to credibility)
- Prosecutor's file notes regarding witness credibility
- Plea deals or immunity agreements with informants
- Prior disciplinary findings against officers or prosecutors
- Evidence of government misconduct
- Exculpatory evidence or alternative suspects identified during investigation

**Follow-up Requests:**

For missing items, draft follow-up discovery requests specifying:
- Item requested
- Legal basis for request (Brady, discovery statute, discovery rules)
- Relevance to case
- Date requested and by whom
- Deadline for response

Track requests and responses in discovery log.

## Brady and Giglio Flagging System

Create summary of all Brady and Giglio material:

**Brady Material Summary:**
- Evidence ID and description
- Why it's favorable to defendant
- Which element(s) or defense affected
- Materiality analysis: could result have been different?
- When discovered/received
- Prosecution's apparent theory (why is it withheld or buried?)

**Giglio Material Summary:**
- Witness name
- Type of credibility issue (informant deal, prior dishonesty, motive to lie, etc.)
- Specifics of credibility issue
- How does it affect witness testimony?
- When discovered/received
- Impact on prosecution case

**Strategic Implications:**
- Consider motion to suppress based on Brady/Giglio violation
- Consider demand for release of personnel files
- Consider cross-examination strategy to impeach witness
- Consider implications if evidence was withheld improperly

## Chain of Custody Review

For physical evidence, chain of custody is critical. Every transfer of evidence must be documented.

**Chain of Custody Information:**
1. **Initial Collection**: Who collected evidence? When and where? How was it collected? Any contamination risk?
2. **Initial Documentation**: Photographs? Description? Condition when collected?
3. **Sealing and Marking**: How was evidence marked/sealed? Is marking intact?
4. **Storage**: Where stored? Temperature/humidity controlled (if relevant)?
5. **Transfers**: Every person who possessed evidence must be documented
   - Who had it
   - When took possession
   - When released possession
   - Condition when received and released
   - Purpose of possession (testing, inspection, etc.)
6. **Testing**: If tested, who conducted test? What were results? Any contamination?
7. **Integrity Issues**: Any gaps in chain? Any break in seal? Any unexplained possession period?

**Red Flags in Chain of Custody:**
- Gap in documentation: period of time evidence location unknown
- Unauthorized possession: evidence in possession of person not authorized
- Break in seal: seal on evidence broken, allowing access by unauthorized person
- Contamination: evidence commingled with other evidence or contaminated
- Storage issues: evidence stored improperly (evidence that requires refrigeration stored at room temp, etc.)
- Testing issues: testing conducted by incompetent technician, testing procedures not followed, contamination during testing

**Impact of Chain of Custody Problems:**
- Evidence may be excludable on Fourth Amendment grounds (illegal seizure or search)
- Evidence may be excludable on hearsay grounds (if authenticity cannot be established)
- Chain of custody weakness creates reasonable doubt about evidence reliability
- Expert can testify to chain of custody problems, creating jury doubt

## Evidence Quality Assessment

Evaluate the quality and reliability of prosecution evidence:

**Physical Evidence Assessment:**
- Relevance: Does evidence actually connect to crime or defendant?
- Authentication: Can prosecution establish what evidence is and where it came from?
- Chain of custody: Is chain intact and documented?
- Scientific reliability: If forensic, are methodology and conclusions scientifically sound?
- Alternative sources: Could evidence have innocent explanation?
- Contamination: Any possibility of contamination or false positive?

**DNA Evidence:**
- Probative value: Does DNA match defendant or exclude defendant?
- Lab procedures: Were proper procedures followed?
- Contamination: Any contamination risk?
- Interpretation: Is interpretation clear or mixed with statistical uncertainty?
- Databases: Was database search appropriate?

**Fingerprint Evidence:**
- Quality of fingerprints: Are prints clear or degraded?
- Database search: Was database search thorough?
- Matching: Was matching conducted by qualified examiner? How many points of comparison?
- Error rates: What are known error rates for fingerprint identification?
- Bias: Can examiner bias affect matching?

**Drug Analysis:**
- Field test: Are field test results reliable or just presumptive?
- Lab confirmation: Was lab confirmation performed?
- Procedure: Were proper procedures followed?
- Chain of custody: Is chain intact?
- Amount: Does amount match charging (weight, number of tablets)?

**Testimony-Based Evidence:**
- Credibility: What is witness's credibility assessment?
- Consistency: Are statements consistent with other evidence?
- Impeachment: What can impeach this witness?
- Brady/Giglio: Is there Brady/Giglio material on this witness?

## Managing Expert Review

Coordinate with defense experts for evidence review:

**Expert Engagement Process:**
1. Identify which evidence requires expert review (forensics, DNA, drugs, medical, etc.)
2. Select qualified expert
3. Provide expert with relevant evidence, testing procedures, lab reports
4. Have expert review for methodological issues, reliability, alternative interpretations
5. Document expert findings
6. Determine if expert will testify or provide report only
7. Share expert findings with prosecution if required by discovery rules

**Common Experts in Criminal Cases:**
- DNA expert: challenges DNA procedures, interpretation, database protocols
- Forensic toxicologist: challenges drug testing procedures and results
- Crime scene reconstruction: examines scene evidence and physical evidence consistency
- Firearms expert: analyzes ballistics, firearms handling, shooting scenarios
- Medical examiner: reviews autopsy findings, questions cause of death
- Forensic accountant: analyzes financial transactions, documents money laundering
- Biomechanical engineer: analyzes injuries to support defendant's version
- Accident reconstruction: analyzes scene evidence to demonstrate innocence in accident cases
- Computer forensics: analyzes digital evidence, cell phone data, computer records
- Mental health professional: evaluates defendant's mental state, capacity, competency

**Response Tracking:**

- Follow-up needed?

## Discovery Privilege Issues

Some materials may be withheld on privilege grounds:

**Work Product Doctrine:**
Attorney's mental impressions, trial strategy, legal theories are protected. But factual information underlying work product must be disclosed.

**Attorney-Client Privilege:**
Communications between attorney and client are privileged. But factual information underlying communication must be disclosed.

**Confidential Informant Protection:**
Prosecution can sometimes withhold informant identity to protect informant safety. But this creates confrontation issues if informant is witness.

**Grand Jury Secrecy:**
Grand jury testimony is sealed by statute in many jurisdictions. Defendant access varies by jurisdiction.

## Evidence Retention and Spoliation

**Evidence Preservation Obligation:**
Once litigation is anticipated, all parties have obligation to preserve evidence. Failure to preserve (spoliation) can result in sanctions, dismissal, or adverse inferences.

**Defense Obligation:**
If defense discovers evidence, it must be preserved. Don't destroy anything that could be relevant.

**Prosecution Obligation:**
Prosecution must preserve evidence, especially physical evidence. DNA evidence must be preserved. Destruction of evidence can support Brady/Giglio claims or sanctions.

## Discovery Memo and Organization

Prepare discovery memo for case file:

**Discovery Memo Contents:**
1. Summary of discovery received to date
2. Outstanding discovery requests and status
3. Brady/Giglio material identified
4. Chain of custody issues identified
5. Evidence quality assessment
6. Expert review status
7. Missing evidence analysis
8. Strategic implications
9. Recommended actions (requests, motions, expert engagement)

**Organization System:**
Create physical and digital organization of discovery:
- Create master discovery log (spreadsheet)
- Organize physical evidence by category with labeled storage
- Organize documents by chronology and category
- Create index of documents
- Create index of photographs/video with frame references
- Maintain chain of custody documentation

Proper discovery organization is foundation for effective case analysis and trial preparation.
