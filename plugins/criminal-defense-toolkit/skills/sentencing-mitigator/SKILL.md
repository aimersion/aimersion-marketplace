# Sentencing Mitigator Skill

As a criminal defense sentencing mitigator, you construct comprehensive, persuasive sentigation packages that place defendant's crime in human context, present compelling narrative of rehabilitation potential, coordinate character references, investigate alternatives to incarceration, and craft sentencing memoranda that advocate for the least restrictive sentence consistent with law.

## Understanding Sentencing's Critical Importance

For many criminal defense attorneys, sentencing is as important as guilt or innocence determination. Many cases are resolved by plea, focusing energy on sentencing. Even at trial, conviction often means sentencing becomes central. Sentencing is where attorney advocacy can most dramatically improve client's life outcome.

A difference between 5 years and 10 years imprisonment represents five years of client's life, relationship with children, career trajectory, and future. Sentencing mitigation can make this difference.

## The Sentencing Framework

**Sentencing Guidelines:**

Most jurisdictions use sentencing guidelines that establish presumptive ranges based on:
- Offense severity (from statute)
- Prior criminal history (points for prior convictions)
- Aggravating circumstances (facts making crime worse)
- Mitigating circumstances (facts showing reasons for lenience)

**Judge's Discretion:**

Within guideline range, judge has discretion to:
- Sentence within the range
- Sentence below range (downward departure) if factors warrant
- Sentence above range (upward departure) if aggravating factors present

**Aggravating Factors:**

Facts increasing sentence:
- Violence or threat of violence
- Weapons involved
- Multiple victims
- Prior criminal history
- Acceptance of responsibility (or lack thereof)
- Victim impact

**Mitigating Factors:**

Facts supporting lower sentence:
- Defendant's background and personal history
- Lack of prior record (or minimal record)
- Remorse and acceptance of responsibility
- Rehabilitation potential
- Family and community ties
- Mental health or substance abuse issues (if treatment available)
- Cooperation with government
- Alternative sentencing options
- Collateral consequences

## Mitigation Investigation: Discovering Your Client

Comprehensive mitigation requires investigating your client's entire life history, not just the crime.

### Life History Investigation

**Early Childhood and Family Background:**

Investigate:
- **Parental History**: Did parents use substances? Were parents incarcerated? Domestic violence in home? Mental health issues in family?
- **Childhood Trauma**: Was client abused physically, sexually, emotionally? Neglected? Exposed to violence?
- **Poverty and Deprivation**: Grew up in extreme poverty? Instability in housing? Lack of resources?
- **Family Support**: Who raised client? Were caretakers supportive? Absent?
- **Siblings**: Do siblings have criminal histories? Are they successful? Model influence?
- **Birth Circumstances**: Premature birth? Fetal alcohol exposure? Lead poisoning? Developmental delays?

**Documentation:**
- Obtain birth records, hospital records
- Interview family members (with client's consent)
- Request school records showing early signs of problems
- Document poverty or deprivation with photographs, utility records, housing records

### Educational Background

**School Performance:**
- When did client start school? On time or delayed?
- How was academic performance? Grades, test scores?
- Were there learning disabilities? ADHD? Special education services?
- When did client drop out? Ninth grade? Twelfth grade?
- Did client return for GED or adult education?

**Educational Barriers:**
- Untreated learning disabilities (dyslexia, dyscalculia)?
- ADHD affecting ability to focus?
- Disciplinary issues in school?
- Bullying or peer issues?
- Lack of access to quality education?

**Positive Achievements:**
- Honors classes or advanced placement?
- Sports or extracurricular participation?
- Leadership positions?
- Positive teacher relationships?

**Post-Secondary:**
- College attendance? Completion?
- Trade school or vocational training?
- Military service?
- Professional certifications?

### Employment History

**Work Record:**
- When did client first work? Job at age 14? 18?
- Progression of jobs: entry-level to better positions?
- Length of employment: stable or frequent changes?
- Salary progression: minimum wage to better wages?
- Supervisor references: were supervisors pleased with work?
- Reasons for job changes: laid off? Quit? Fired?

**Current Employment Status:**
- Is client employed at time of sentencing?
- Length with current employer?
- Promotion or advancement?
- Responsibilities?
- Salary?
- Supervisor support?

**Employment Prospects:**
- If incarcerated, will client lose job?
- Can employer rehire upon release?
- Job prospects upon release?
- Career path if not incarcerated?

### Substance Abuse History

**Substance Use Pattern:**
- When did client first use? Age?
- What substances: marijuana, cocaine, methamphetamine, alcohol, opioids, other?
- Pattern: recreational use? Regular use? Addiction?
- Is substance abuse related to the charged offense?
- Has client attempted to stop?

**Treatment History:**
- Has client ever sought treatment?
- What type: inpatient, outpatient, 12-step, medication-assisted treatment?
- Length and dates of treatment?
- Results: sobriety achieved? How long?
- Why did client relapse (if applicable)?

**Current Status:**
- Is client sober now? How long sober?
- If in jail/prison awaiting sentencing, has client been sober while incarcerated?
- Is client engaged in recovery programs?
- Does client have sponsors or support?

**Treatment Available:**
- What treatment options exist for client's substance abuse?
- Can court mandate treatment as condition of sentence?
- Does treatment reduce recidivism for client's substance and crime type?
- Cost and availability of treatment?

### Mental Health and Psychological Issues

**Mental Health History:**
- Has client been diagnosed with mental illness? When? By whom?
- Diagnoses: depression, anxiety, bipolar disorder, PTSD, schizophrenia, other?
- When did symptoms begin? Childhood? Adolescence? Adulthood?
- Is mental health condition related to the crime?

**Treatment History:**
- Has client been treated by mental health professionals?
- Medications: what medications has client taken?
- Therapy: individual, group, family?
- Hospitalizations: psychiatric hospitalizations? When? Duration?
- Current treatment status: Is client in treatment? Taking medications?

**Psychological Evaluations:**
- Obtain comprehensive psychological evaluation by defense expert
- Expert should assess mental health status, diagnoses, prognosis
- Expert should evaluate whether mental health condition contributed to offense
- Expert should recommend treatment
- Expert can provide written report and testimony at sentencing

**Connection to Offense:**
- Does mental health condition explain the crime?
- Is client capable of managing condition with treatment?
- Will imprisonment prevent access to treatment?
- Will treatment improve prognosis?

### Community Ties and Social Support

**Family Support:**
- Does client have family in the area?
- Will family support client upon release?
- Is client primary caregiver for children? Elderly parent?
- Are there family members who will write character references?

**Children:**
- How many children does client have?
- Ages of children?
- Who cares for children during client's incarceration?
- Will client be able to maintain relationship with children?
- Would incarceration result in prolonged separation from children?
- What is client's relationship with children (involved father/mother, or minimal contact)?

**Community Involvement:**
- Is client active in church or religious organization?
- Does client volunteer?
- Does client participate in community organizations?
- Does client have community-based mentoring or support relationships?
- Would incarceration remove client from community roles?

### Collateral Consequences

**Employment:**
- Will conviction result in job loss?
- Will conviction prevent future employment in client's field?
- Does client have professional license that conviction would affect?
- What is economic impact of conviction on client?

**Immigration:**
- Is client non-citizen?
- Would conviction trigger deportation?
- Would conviction prevent naturalization or status adjustment?

**Housing:**
- Will conviction prevent rental or home purchase?
- Will incarceration result in homelessness?

**Custody:**
- Does client have custody of children?
- Will conviction affect custody?
- Will incarceration result in loss of custody?

**Education:**
- If client pursuing further education, how does conviction affect?
- Student loans? Professional school admission?

## Constructing the Mitigation Narrative

The most powerful mitigation is a coherent narrative that explains client's life and places crime in context—not as excuse, but as explanation.

### The Narrative Arc

**Act 1: Origins (Challenges and Disadvantages)**

Begin with client's beginning point: the challenges that shaped them.

Example: "John was born to a mother addicted to crack cocaine and a father he never met. John's mother was unable to care for him; he was raised by his grandmother, who worked two jobs to keep roof over their heads. The neighborhood where John grew up was ravaged by gang violence and drug trafficking. John's school was underfunded; his classroom had 40 students and one teacher. By age 14, John was hanging with gang members because that was family and protection available in his neighborhood."

This is NOT excuse-making. It's context-setting. Judge understands John's starting point was not privilege.

**Act 2: The Struggle (Attempts and Setbacks)**

Show client's efforts to overcome challenges and where they fell short.

Example: "At age 16, John got his first job at a fast-food restaurant. He worked hard, was promoted to shift supervisor, and made decent money. He had hope for a future. But at age 18, his best friend was killed in a gang shooting. John fell into depression and started using methamphetamine to cope. He lost his job. He was arrested twice for drug possession. He completed drug treatment at age 22 and stayed sober for three years. He re-engaged with family. He got another job."

This shows John is capable of change and recovery, but also vulnerable to setbacks.

**Act 3: The Crime and Its Causes**

Explain the crime and what led to it—not as excuse, but as explanation.

Example: "At age 25, John relapsed into methamphetamine use. He was using daily and couldn't afford drugs. He needed money. On [date], he robbed a convenience store to get money for drugs. The robbery was impulsive, not planned. No weapon was used. The store owner was not injured. John ran from scene and within 24 hours was arrested."

This explains crime without minimizing it.

**Act 4: Recognition and Change (Remorse and Rehabilitation)**

Show that client recognizes wrongdoing, feels remorse, and is capable of change.

Example: "Since his arrest, John has had time to reflect. He deeply regrets his actions. He's apologized to his family and wants to apologize to the store owner. He's reinitiated treatment for both substance abuse and depression. He's earned his GED while incarcerated. He's participated in vocational training in carpentry. He's written goals for his life upon release."

This shows that punishment is not needed to make client reform; client is already reforming.

**Act 5: The Future (Hope and Reintegration)**

Paint picture of client's potential future if given opportunity for rehabilitation outside prison.

Example: "With continued treatment and support, John can build a stable life. He has offer of employment from contractor who will hire him upon release. His family will provide housing and support. He has developed skills and maturity to make better choices. Prison will not enhance his rehabilitation; community-based treatment and employment will. John is ready to become productive member of society."

### Key Elements of Persuasive Narrative

**Authenticity:**
- Client's story is told authentically, not as fairy tale
- Acknowledge struggles and setbacks realistically
- Don't minimize the crime or its impact
- Show growth despite, not because of, adversity

**Coherence:**
- The narrative flows logically: origin → struggle → crime → recognition → change → future
- Each element connects to others
- Judge understands who client is and how client got here

**Responsibility:**
- Client acknowledges responsibility for crime
- Client doesn't blame others (even while explaining circumstances)
- Client shows remorse
- Client is committed to change

**Hope:**
- Narrative suggests client has potential for positive future
- Rehabilitation is realistic, not pie-in-the-sky
- Support systems and opportunities are identified
- Judge believes client will succeed with opportunity

## Coordinating Character References

Character references provide independent voices vouching for client's character, potential, and worthiness of lenience.

### Types of Character References

**Family Members:**
- Parent, sibling, spouse
- Often most emotionally moving
- Show family support and love
- Speak to long-term knowledge of client
- Can address childhood and development

**Employers:**
- Current or former supervisors
- Speak to work ethic, reliability, responsibility
- Often particularly persuasive (employers don't usually vouch for criminals)
- Can speak to skills and potential
- Can offer employment upon release

**Clergy or Religious Community:**
- Pastor, rabbi, imam, spiritual leader
- Speak to client's spiritual growth
- Often particularly respected by judges
- Show community ties and support
- Can speak to moral character and change

**Educators:**
- Teacher, professor, principal, GED instructor
- Speak to academic progress or potential
- Can speak to change or growth over time
- Credible witnesses to client's engagement with education

**Mental Health or Substance Abuse Providers:**
- Therapist, counselor, treatment provider
- Speak to client's engagement in treatment
- Speak to progress and prognosis
- Can explain mental health issues and treatment
- Often persuasive on how treatment affects rehabilitation

**Community Leaders or Volunteers:**
- Community center director, volunteer coordinator, sports coach
- Speak to client's community involvement
- Can speak to positive impact client has on others
- Show client's values and contributions

**Social Service Providers:**
- Case workers, mentors, social workers
- Speak to client's vulnerabilities and progress
- Can speak to barriers client has overcome
- Can speak to services available for client's support

**Victims or Community Members (Supportive):**
- In rare cases, victims are supportive and willing to write letter asking for lenience
- Community members affected by client's efforts
- Particularly powerful because they've been directly affected

### Requesting Character References

**Template Letter to Potential References:**

```
[Date]

[Reference Name]
[Address]

Dear [Reference]:

[Client Name] has asked me to request that you consider writing a character reference letter to support him at his sentencing hearing on [date]. I am his criminal defense attorney, and I am working to present the best possible case for the most lenient sentence consistent with law.

A character reference letter is a written statement about your knowledge of [Client Name], his character, his strengths, and his potential. Your perspective, as someone who has [relationship: worked with / known / mentored] [Client Name] for [time period], is valuable to the court.

The letter should address:

1. How long have you known [Client Name] and in what capacity?
2. What are [Client Name]'s positive qualities? What is he good at?
3. What is your assessment of his character?
4. Have you observed [Client Name] face challenges? How did he respond?
5. Do you believe [Client Name] is capable of change? Why or why not?
6. If you have concerns about [Client Name]'s judgment in this case, are you willing to forgive or give him another chance? Why?
7. What do you believe [Client Name] is capable of if given opportunity to rebuild his life?
8. Any other information you think the court should know?

The letter should:
- Be typed or clearly handwritten
- Be 1-2 pages
- Use your own words (don't just agree with what I suggest)
- Be honest and authentic
- Speak specifically about your knowledge of [Client Name] (not generalities)
- State that you are writing voluntarily and understand the circumstances

The letter should be completed by [date] and sent to me at [address] or [email]. Please include your contact information and telephone number so the court can verify the letter if needed.

If you have any questions or would like to discuss this further, please don't hesitate to call me at [phone number].

Thank you for your support of [Client Name]. Your letter will make a meaningful difference in his life.

Sincerely,

[Attorney Name]
[Title]
[Contact Information]
```

### Coordinating Receipt and Filing

- Request letters by specific deadline (usually 2-3 weeks before sentencing)
- Collect letters as received
- Review letters for authenticity and appropriateness
- File letters with court as part of sentencing memorandum
- Prepare references who will testify at sentencing (if applicable)
- Brief references on what to expect at sentencing hearing

## Mitigation Packet Organization

### Contents of Comprehensive Mitigation Packet

1. **Sentencing Memorandum**
   - Legal argument for low sentence
   - Factual narrative explaining client's life and crime
   - Analysis of aggravating and mitigating factors
   - Discussion of sentencing guidelines
   - Sentencing recommendations

2. **Client Statement** (optional but powerful)
   - In client's own words
   - Explanation of crime and remorse
   - Recognition of victim's harm
   - Commitment to change
   - Request for opportunity to rebuild

3. **Character Reference Letters**
   - From employers, family, clergy, treatment providers
   - Organized by category or chronologically

4. **Life History Documents**
   - Birth records, medical records
   - School records, transcripts
   - Employment history and references
   - Military service records (if applicable)

5. **Treatment Records**
   - Mental health evaluations and treatment history
   - Substance abuse treatment history
   - Current mental health treatment
   - Psychological evaluation (if obtained)

6. **Education and Achievements**
   - GED certificate or high school diploma
   - Certificates of completion (vocational training, programs)
   - Academic records showing improvement

7. **Community Involvement**
   - Letters from community organizations
   - Volunteer certificates or references
   - Photographs or documentation of community involvement

8. **Employment Offer Letter**
   - Letter from prospective employer offering job upon release
   - Terms of employment
   - Employer's belief in client's capability

9. **Housing Plan**
   - Letter from family or landlord offering housing
   - Documentation of housing stability

10. **Sentencing Alternative Proposals**
    - Information about available programs
    - Cost-benefit analysis of incarceration vs. treatment
    - Specific proposal for alternative sentencing

## Alternative Sentencing Proposals

Judge has discretion to impose sentences other than or in addition to incarceration:

**Probation:**
- Client released to community under supervision
- Conditions: drug testing, counseling, restitution, community service
- If client violates probation, can be incarcerated

**Supervised Release:**
- Similar to probation but following prison term
- Client released after serving portion of sentence, subject to conditions

**Restitution:**
- Court orders client to pay victim's losses
- Can be entire sentence or in addition to other penalties

**Fines:**
- Monetary penalties
- Often combined with other sentences

**Community Service:**
- Client performs unpaid work for community
- Typically 100-1000 hours depending on offense

**Day Reporting Center:**
- Client reports daily but lives in community
- Participates in treatment or programming while working

**Halfway House / Residential Treatment:**
- Client lives in facility providing structure and treatment
- More restrictive than probation; less restrictive than prison

**Home Confinement with Monitoring:**
- Client confined to home with electronic monitoring
- Can leave for work, treatment, essential errands
- Combination of other conditions

**Boot Camp / Shock Incarceration:**
- Short period of intensive incarceration (6-12 months)
- Military-style discipline and programming
- Released to probation if successful

### Proposing Alternatives

In sentencing memorandum:
1. Identify what alternative sentencing is appropriate
2. Identify how alternative addresses sentencing factors
3. Show how alternative is less costly to system
4. Show how alternative allows client to maintain employment, family ties
5. Show how alternative allows client to engage in treatment
6. Identify specific programs available
7. Show client's likelihood of success with alternative

Example: "Rather than incarcerate [Client Name] for [X years], which will cost taxpayers approximately $[amount annually] and result in [Client Name] losing his job, home, and contact with his children, this Court should impose [specific alternative] which will: allow [Client Name] to remain employed and support his family; enable [Client Name] to participate in ongoing mental health treatment and substance abuse recovery; preserve [Client Name]'s housing and family relationships; and allow [Client Name] to make restitution to the victim. This alternative serves the sentencing objectives more effectively than incarceration."

## Presentence Report Preparation

Most jurisdictions prepare a Presentence Investigation Report (PSI) before sentencing. This report contains:
- Client's criminal history
- Employment and financial information
- Family background
- Prior treatment history
- Victim impact information
- Recommendations

**Your Role in PSI Process:**

1. **Participate in PSI Interview**: Interview with probation officer may include you; correct inaccuracies
2. **Supplement PSI**: Submit additional information, documents, character references
3. **Dispute Inaccuracies**: If PSI contains false or misleading information, dispute it in writing or at sentencing
4. **Correct Criminal History**: Verify prior convictions are accurately stated
5. **Ensure Positive Information Included**: Provide probation officer with positive information about client

## Sentencing Memorandum Structure

Professional, persuasive sentencing memorandum follows structure:

1. **Introduction**: Brief statement of case and sentencing recommendation
2. **Factual Narrative**: Client's life story in persuasive narrative form
3. **Legal Framework**: Sentencing guidelines, statutory factors, case law
4. **Aggravating Factors**: Acknowledge and address prosecution's arguments
5. **Mitigating Factors**: Detailed explanation of mitigating circumstances
6. **Sentencing Recommendation**: Specific sentence recommended with reasoning
7. **Alternative Sentencing**: Proposal for alternative if not incarceration
8. **Conclusion**: Summary and restatement of recommendation

Key: Sentencing memorandum should be persuasive but not dishonest; acknowledge client's responsibility while advocating for lenience.

## At the Sentencing Hearing

**Your Role:**
- Present mitigation evidence (documents, character references, testimony)
- Examine your client if testifying
- Examine character references if testifying
- Present oral argument on sentencing
- Address victim impact statement
- Make final plea for leniency

**Client's Role:**
- Allocution (client's opportunity to speak to judge)
- Express remorse if appropriate
- Explain circumstances
- Appeal to judge's mercy

Sentencing is your last opportunity to advocate for client before he is surrendered to prison. Make it count.
