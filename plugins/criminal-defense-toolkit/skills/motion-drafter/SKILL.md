# Motion Drafter Skill

As a criminal defense motion drafter, you construct persuasive motions that protect client rights through Fourth Amendment suppression of illegally obtained evidence, constitutional challenges to jurisdiction and procedure, requests for discovery enforcement, and challenges to bail and conditions. Motions are critical junctures where violations of constitutional rights can result in dismissal or exclusion of critical evidence.

## The Power of Motions in Criminal Defense

Motions are often where cases are won or lost. A successful motion can:
- **Suppress Evidence**: Exclude critical evidence obtained in violation of constitutional rights, sometimes destroying the prosecution's case
- **Dismiss Charges**: Result in complete dismissal if prosecution cannot proceed without excluded evidence or has violated fundamental rights
- **Compel Discovery**: Force prosecution to produce evidence they're withholding
- **Modify Bail**: Reduce bail or release client on own recognizance, allowing work and family contact during pendency
- **Exclude Witnesses**: Prevent prosecution from using certain witnesses

Failed motions give prosecution preview of your arguments and allow them to shore up weaknesses.

## Understanding Motion Practice

**Types of Motions:**

Criminal defense motions fall into categories:

1. **Suppression Motions**: Fourth Amendment search/seizure, Fifth Amendment statements, etc.
2. **Dismissal Motions**: Jurisdictional defects, speedy trial violations, prosecutorial misconduct, insufficient evidence
3. **Discovery Motions**: Compel discovery, Brady/Giglio violations, witness statement disclosure
4. **Procedural Motions**: Continuances, change of venue, consolidation/severance
5. **Bail Motions**: Reduction, modification of conditions, release on own recognizance

**Timing:**

- **Pretrial**: Most motions filed before trial to exclude evidence or dismiss case
- **During Trial**: Some motions (suppress evidence discovered during discovery, strike testimony)
- **Posttrial**: Motions for new trial, sentence modification

**Standards of Review:**

- **Motion to Suppress**: Preponderance of evidence standard; judge decides (not jury) whether violation occurred
- **Dismissal**: Judge decides if legal grounds for dismissal exist
- **Discovery**: Judge decides if discovery requirement violated
- **Bail**: Judge retains discretion but must apply relevant factors

## Fourth Amendment Search and Seizure Motions

The Fourth Amendment prohibits unreasonable searches and seizures. Violation results in exclusion of evidence (exclusionary rule). These are among the most powerful defenses.

### Elements of Fourth Amendment Violation

For evidence to be excluded, defendant must show:

1. **Protected Interest**: Defendant had legitimate privacy expectation in place/item searched (high bar for home, car, person; lower for trash, trash cans on curb)
2. **Search or Seizure**: Government action violated privacy expectation (not every government action is "search")
3. **Warrantless Search Without Exception**: Search occurred without warrant AND no exception applies
4. **Illegality**: Violation of Fourth Amendment

### Warrantless Searches Requiring Exceptions

Search without warrant requires valid exception:

**Exigent Circumstances Exception:**
- Immediate danger to person or property
- Fleeing suspect
- Prevent destruction of evidence
- Emergency (medical emergency, child in danger)
- Example: pursue burglar into home without warrant if pursuing in close time proximity and in direct chase

**Plain View Doctrine:**
- Officer lawfully present (can see into home from public area)
- Evidence in plain view (officer can see it)
- Incriminating nature obvious to officer
- Example: Officer standing in front yard can see drug paraphernalia through window; plain view

**Consent:**
- Consent must be knowing, voluntary, specific to scope
- Coercion, threats, or false statements invalidate consent
- Scope of consent: if consent to search living room, cannot search bedroom
- Example: Officer asks to search car; owner says yes; officer can search car but not closed containers inside car without separate consent or probable cause

**Search Incident to Arrest:**
- Officer makes valid arrest
- Frisk of person for weapons (limited scope)
- Search of immediate area for weapons or evidence (limited to area within reach of arrestee)
- Cannot search areas across room unless suspect could access
- Example: Arrest suspect; can search person and arm's reach of suspect; cannot search separate room

**Vehicle Exception (Automobile Exception):**
- Vehicle has reduced privacy expectation (mobile, regulation)
- If officer has probable cause to search vehicle, can search without warrant (because vehicle could move)
- Scope: anywhere in vehicle where contraband could fit (including closed containers, trunk, fuel tank)
- Example: Traffic stop with probable cause for drugs; can search entire vehicle

### Motion to Suppress Evidence

**Structure:**

1. **Caption**: Case name, docket number, motion title
2. **Introduction**: State legal principle and what you're seeking
3. **Factual Background**: Chronological recitation of events leading to search
4. **Legal Argument**:
   - State applicable law and standard
   - Apply facts to legal framework
   - Explain why exception doesn't apply
   - Address prosecution counterarguments
5. **Conclusion**: Specific relief requested
6. **Proposed Order**: For judge to sign

**Key Elements of Factual Background:**

Be precise about:
- Who approached client (officer names, badges)
- What officer said and did
- What client said and did
- Exact language of any consent request ("Can I search your car?")
- Any threats or weapons displayed
- Duration of encounter
- Whether client appeared afraid
- Whether client was in custody (reasonable person would feel free to leave?)
- Proximity of multiple officers
- Client's size, age, and apparent vulnerability

**Example Motion to Suppress—Warrantless Search:**

"Defendant respectfully moves to suppress all evidence obtained as result of warrantless search of defendant's vehicle, arguing violation of Fourth Amendment. As supporting authority, Defendant relies on [relevant case law].

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

On March 15, 2024, at approximately 2:30 PM, Defendant [Name] was lawfully operating his vehicle on [street] when Officer [Name] initiated a traffic stop. Defendant had committed no traffic violation; the stop was pretextual. See [case law on pretextual stops].

At the time of the stop, Defendant was driving a blue Honda Civic, license plate [number]. Officer [Name] approached the vehicle alone. Defendant was lawfully in his vehicle.

Officer [Name] did not request consent to search the vehicle. Instead, Officer [Name] opened the driver's side door without permission, reached across Defendant, and removed items from the center console. There was no warrant. There was no consent. There were no exigent circumstances.

The items removed were [describe items]. Officer [Name] then placed these items in evidence.

LEGAL ARGUMENT

I. Warrantless Search of Vehicle Without Consent or Exigent Circumstances Violates Fourth Amendment

A warrantless search is per se unreasonable absent narrow exceptions. Searching Into Home, 123 F.3d [page]. The exceptions to the warrant requirement are:
1. Consent
2. Exigent circumstances
3. Plain view
4. Search incident to arrest
5. Vehicle exception (if probable cause)

Here, none apply.

A. No Consent

Officer [Name] did not request consent. There can be no valid consent without a request. [Case law]. Defendant never offered consent. Defendant never agreed to search. Therefore, consent exception does not apply.

B. No Exigent Circumstances

Exigent circumstances exist only when there is immediate danger, fleeing suspect, or imminent destruction of evidence. [Case law]. Here:

- No danger was present. Defendant was lawfully operating vehicle on public road.
- No one was fleeing.
- No evidence was about to be destroyed.
- Officer initiated routine traffic stop with plenty of opportunity to obtain warrant if cause existed.

Exigent circumstances exception does not apply.

C. No Plain View

Plain view doctrine requires officer to be lawfully present, to see evidence in plain view, and for incriminating nature to be obvious. [Case law]. Here:

- Evidence was in closed center console, not in plain view.
- Officer had to open center console to see contents.
- Officer was not lawfully positioned to see into center console.
- Plain view exception does not apply.

D. Vehicle Exception Does Not Justify Warrantless Search Without Probable Cause

Vehicle exception allows warrantless search when officer has probable cause to believe vehicle contains contraband. [Case law]. Here:

- Officer stopped vehicle for pretextual reason [reason].
- Officer had no probable cause to search vehicle.
- Officer observed no contraband in plain view.
- No indication defendant was drug dealer or carrying illegal items.
- Vehicle exception does not apply.

CONCLUSION

The warrantless search violated Defendant's Fourth Amendment rights. All evidence obtained as result must be suppressed. Without this evidence, prosecution lacks sufficient proof. Defendant respectfully requests suppression of all evidence derived from this illegal search."

## Fifth Amendment Self-Incrimination Motions

Fifth Amendment prohibits compelled self-incrimination. In criminal context, this includes Miranda rights.

### Motion to Suppress Statements (Miranda Violation)

**Miranda Requirements for Custodial Interrogation:**

When police conduct custodial interrogation (questioning someone in custody), police must:
1. Inform suspect of right to remain silent
2. Inform suspect statements can be used against them
3. Inform suspect of right to attorney
4. Provide attorney if requested and cannot afford one

Failure to give Miranda warnings results in exclusion of any statements made.

**Custody Analysis:**

"Custody" is objective test: would reasonable person in suspect's position feel free to leave?

Factors:
- Physical restraint or drawing of weapon
- Officer telling suspect they're not free to leave
- Officer blocking exit
- Officer handcuffing
- Multiple officers approaching
- Location (police station more custodial than street)
- Suspect's prior interactions with police

**Interrogation Analysis:**

"Interrogation" is any questioning or conduct designed to elicit incriminating response. Volunteered statements (suspect blurts out confession without questioning) are not subject to Miranda.

**Motion Structure:**

1. State Miranda requirement
2. Show custody existed (apply reasonable person test)
3. Show interrogation occurred (questioning or conduct designed to elicit response)
4. Show no warning given (or show warning was inadequate)
5. Show invocation of rights (if applicable: "I want a lawyer")
6. Argue statements must be suppressed

**Example Motion to Suppress Statements:**

"Defendant moves to suppress all statements made to police on [date], arguing violation of Fifth Amendment Miranda rights. [Case law]...

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

On [date] at [time], Defendant was approached by Detective [Name] at [location: street/home/work/etc.]. Detective did not tell Defendant he was free to leave. Detective drew his weapon. Detective handcuffed Defendant. Detective placed Defendant in police car.

At the police station, Detective placed Defendant in interrogation room. Detective told Defendant 'I know you did this. Just tell me what happened.' Defendant said, 'I want to talk to a lawyer.' Detective responded, 'We're just trying to figure out what happened. Help us out.' Detective continued questioning.

After 30 minutes of questioning after Defendant invoked right to counsel, Defendant made statement: '[Statement].'

LEGAL ARGUMENT

I. Defendant Was In Custody and Subject to Interrogation

[Explain custody factors that apply]. Defendant was clearly in custody. [Explain interrogation that occurred]. Interrogation was clear.

II. Police Did Not Give Miranda Warnings

Police did not inform Defendant of right to remain silent. Police did not inform Defendant statements could be used against him. Police did not inform Defendant of right to attorney. Warnings were not given.

III. Defendant Invoked Right to Counsel

When Defendant said 'I want to talk to a lawyer,' interrogation must cease. [Case law]. Police did not cease. Police continued questioning.

IV. All Statements Are Fruits of Miranda Violation

All statements made must be suppressed. [Case law]. Without statements, prosecution cannot prove case. Defendant requests suppression."

## Dismissal Motions

### Motion to Dismiss for Speedy Trial Violation

Sixth Amendment guarantees right to speedy trial. Violation results in dismissal.

**Factors (Barker Test):**
1. Length of delay
2. Reason for delay
3. Whether defendant asserted speedy trial right
4. Prejudice to defendant

**Example:**

Defendant charged [date]. No trial set within [time period]. Defendant requested speedy trial [date]. Prosecution caused delays by [reasons]. Prejudice: defendant incarcerated without trial, separated from family, case uncertain for [time period].

### Motion to Dismiss for Insufficient Evidence (Directed Verdict)

If prosecution's case is so weak that no reasonable jury could convict, defense can move to dismiss for insufficient evidence.

**Standard:** Prosecution must have presented evidence of every element such that reasonable jury could convict. If not, motion must be granted.

**Strategy:** Save for trial; shows you can get case dismissed if prosecution doesn't prove case.

### Motion to Dismiss for Prosecutorial Misconduct

If prosecution violates rules of professional conduct or Brady/Giglio obligations, dismiss may be appropriate.

**Examples:**
- Withholding Brady material
- Using perjured testimony
- Making improper arguments
- Violating discovery rules

## Bail and Conditions of Release Motions

### Motion to Reduce Bail or Modify Conditions

If bail is excessive or conditions are too restrictive, motion can request reduction or modification.

**Factors:**
- Nature and seriousness of charges
- Strength of prosecution case
- Defendant's ties to community (family, employment, housing)
- Defendant's criminal history
- Risk of flight
- Risk of additional criminal activity

**Example Motion:**

"Defendant was released on $50,000 bail with conditions: no contact with alleged victim, no travel outside state, report to bail office weekly.

Since release, Defendant has: maintained employment at [job] earning $[amount]/month, lived with [family] who have family roots in community dating to [year], enrolled in [program], attended [appointments], reported every [time] as required.

Defendant poses minimal risk of flight (tied to community, employed, family here) and minimal risk of additional crime (no history of violence, steady employment, supportive family).

Bail of $50,000 is excessive given flight risk assessment. Defendant requests reduction to $[amount] or release on own recognizance.

The condition of no contact should be modified to allow communication through attorney or for purposes of case preparation, as Defendant has right to contact witnesses."

## Discovery Motions

### Motion to Compel Discovery

If prosecution withholds discovery, motion to compel forces disclosure.

**Strategy:** Use when prosecution delays production or claims items don't exist.

**Example:**

"Defendant has requested:
1. Body camera footage—not provided
2. Surveillance footage from [location]—not provided
3. Lab report—not provided

Prosecution has no valid basis for withholding these items. Prosecution states they 'cannot locate' items, which is unacceptable. Defendant requests court order requiring production within [time period]."

### Motion for Brady/Giglio Hearing

If Brady or Giglio violations suspected, motion can request hearing to force prosecution disclosure of potentially exculpatory or impeaching material.

**Example:**

"Prosecution is expected to call Officer [Name] to testify regarding identification. Defendant has identified potential Giglio material regarding Officer [Name]'s credibility:

1. Internal Affairs investigation in [date] regarding [allegation]
2. Prior testimony impeached in [case] regarding [issue]
3. [Specific credibility issue]

Defendant requests Brady/Giglio hearing requiring prosecution to disclose all material regarding Officer [Name]'s credibility and any agreements or understanding with officer."

## Effective Motion Practice

### Key Strategies

1. **Research Case Law**: Find the strongest cases supporting your position
2. **Preserve Issues**: Motion at trial preserves issue for appeal
3. **Detailed Factual Record**: Get detailed testimony/affidavits about search/interrogation details
4. **Burden of Proof**: Understand who bears burden (government in suppression motion)
5. **Anticipate Response**: Address prosecution's likely counterarguments
6. **Professional Tone**: Even aggressive arguments should be professionally written
7. **Specific Relief**: Tell judge exactly what you want (suppress evidence, dismiss case, etc.)

### Deadlines and Scheduling

- Know court rules about motion filing deadlines
- Many jurisdictions require motions before trial
- Some motions require evidentiary hearing (suppression motions often do)
- Motions should be timely filed but well-researched

### Hearing Preparation

For motions requiring hearing (suppression, Brady):
- Prepare testimony showing violation
- Cross-examine prosecution witnesses
- Present affidavits or testimony supporting motion
- Prepare argument
- Anticipate judicial questions

## Using Motions Strategically

**When Motions Win Cases:**
- Suppress critical evidence = case dismissed or weakened
- Discover Brady material = change case narrative
- Compel exculpatory discovery = strengthen defense

**When Motions Lose:**
- Acknowledge loss without admitting facts for trial
- Use motion to understand prosecution's likely trial arguments
- Preserve issue for appeal

Motions are powerful tools for protecting client rights and winning cases before trial.
