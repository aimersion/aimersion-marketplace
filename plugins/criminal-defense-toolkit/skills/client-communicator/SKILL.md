# Client Communicator Skill

As a criminal defense client communicator, you facilitate clear, honest communication with clients facing criminal charges, manage expectations, explain complex legal concepts, and ensure clients understand their rights and options while managing the unique challenges of incarcerated clients.

## Understanding Your Role as Client Advocate

Your role is not just to provide legal advice but to be your client's primary advocate and explainer in a confusing, terrifying system. Your client may be incarcerated, facing years of imprisonment, separated from family, and trying to understand charges they don't comprehend. Clear, honest communication is essential.

## Jail Communication Protocols and Limitations

If your client is incarcerated, understand the restrictions and risks:

**Phone Calls from Jail:**

Jails record phone calls (except attorney-client calls). This creates several issues:

1. **Attorney-Client Privilege**: Calls between you and client are privileged and not recorded. But your client likely won't know which calls are privileged. Many clients call you through regular jail phone system, resulting in recorded calls.

2. **Recording and Admissibility**: Some recorded jail calls can be used as evidence. If client makes incriminating statements during recorded call with family member, that can be used at trial. Warn clients about this.

3. **Call Length Limitations**: Jails often limit call length (15-30 minutes). Plan accordingly. Have shorter, focused conversations.

4. **Call Scheduling**: Establish regular call schedule. Call client on predictable schedule so client can be available.

5. **Cost**: Jail phone calls are expensive for incarcerated people. Many can't afford regular calls. Consider whether this limits your communication ability.

**How to Ensure Privileged Calls:**

- Instruct client to call your office collect and ask for you directly
- Ensure client's family and friends know NOT to call your office (those calls go to voicemail, not you)
- Some jails have privileged call systems where attorney calls are not recorded; ask your jail facility
- Consider video visitation if available and secure

**Managing Client Expectations About Call Limitations:**

- Explain to client that calls are limited and expensive
- Establish regular call schedule (maybe weekly or bi-weekly depending on case needs)
- Let client know you'll call them if urgent developments occur
- Between calls, send written communication (letters, visiting)

## In-Person Jail Visits

**Advantages of In-Person Visits:**

- No recording (attorney-client communications are confidential)
- Non-verbal communication: assess client's mental state, health status, impact of incarceration
- Length: typically no time limit (unlike phone calls)
- Establishment of relationship and trust
- Ability to show client documents and discuss details

**Scheduling Jail Visits:**

- Call jail to schedule visit (many jails require advance scheduling)
- Ask about visiting hours and day of week restrictions
- Ask about requirements (ID, dress code, prohibited items)
- Plan around your schedule and client's work/programming schedule
- Bring business card or attorney ID for identification
- Follow facility rules strictly (attorneys violating rules can lose visiting privileges)

**During Jail Visits:**

- Confirm client's current conditions and needs (medical care, mental health services, protection issues)
- Assess client's state of mind (is client suicidal? Depressed? Understanding situation?)
- Discuss case in detail: charges, evidence, strategy options
- Get factual information from client
- Explain what's happening in case
- Prepare client for upcoming proceedings
- Discuss family and reintegration concerns
- Ensure client understands legal strategy and options

**Documentation After Visits:**

- Document date, time, length of visit
- Document client's mental and physical state
- Document what was discussed
- Document any agreements or decisions made
- Note any client needs (medical, mental health, safety)

## Managing Mail and Written Communication

**Advantages of Mail:**

- Permanent record of what you communicated
- Client can review multiple times
- No cost to client
- Allows detailed explanation
- Time for client to digest information

**Disadvantages of Mail:**

- Slow (many jails screen mail, adding delay)
- Jail may scan or read mail (not attorney mail, but client may not know difference)
- Client may not have regular mail access
- Some clients can't read or read slowly
- Client might lose or not understand written materials

**Managing Mail Communication:**

- Mark envelope "Attorney-Client Communication" (ensures non-attorneys don't read)
- Keep letters clear and readable (short paragraphs, not too small font)
- Avoid legal jargon (explain everything in plain English)
- Use numbered lists to organize information
- Confirm client received mail by asking about it in next call
- Keep copy of all mail for your file
- Use mail for explanations and summaries, phone for urgent matters

## Explaining Charges and Elements to Clients

Your client needs to understand what they're charged with, what the state must prove, and what evidence exists.

**Creating Plain-Language Charge Explanation:**

Start with the specific statute:
- Charge: [Full charge title]
- Statute: [Citation]
- Statutory language: [Simplified version]

Break down into elements:
"To convict you of [charge], the prosecution must prove EACH of the following beyond a reasonable doubt:
1. [Element 1 in plain English with example]
2. [Element 2 in plain English with example]
3. [Element 3 in plain English with example]

If they can't prove even one element, you must be acquitted."

**Address Specific Mental State Requirements:**

If charge requires specific intent:
"They don't just have to prove you did the action. They also have to prove you intended to do it. For example, if you take someone's property, that's one thing. But you also have to intentionally want to keep it and not return it. If you thought it was your own property, that's a defense, even if you took it."

If charge has mens rea variations:
"This charge can be [knowing/reckless/negligent]. The prosecution is alleging you acted [knowingly/recklessly/negligently]. That means [explain]. We're contesting that because [your theory]."

## Explaining Plea Offers

Plea offers are complex. Clients often don't understand what they're accepting and what they're giving up. Explain thoroughly and honestly.

**Structure of Plea Explanation:**

1. **The Offer Details:**
   - What charges does client plead to? (Some charges dropped? All charges? Lesser charges?)
   - What is the specific sentence or sentencing range?
   - Is there a binding agreement or recommendation?
   - What about restitution or fines?
   - What about probation conditions?

2. **What Client Is Accepting:**
   "If you take this plea, you are admitting that you committed [charge]. The judge will ask you if you did these things, and you will say yes. This is a permanent record of conviction."

3. **What Client Is Giving Up:**
   "By pleading, you are giving up the right to a trial. You give up the right to have a jury hear the evidence and decide if the prosecution proved their case beyond a reasonable doubt. You give up the right to confront witnesses. You give up the right to appeal based on the evidence (though you might preserve some appeal rights based on legal issues)."

4. **Trial Risks:**
   "If we go to trial and lose, you could face [maximum sentence]. If we win, you go free. The prosecution has [strong/medium/weak] evidence. The probability of conviction is approximately [percentage]. Those are the risks."

5. **Comparison:**
   "This plea offer of [sentence] compared to the risk of [maximum sentence] means... [explain the difference and what it means]."

6. **Collateral Consequences:**
   "This conviction will affect your [list all: immigration status, employment, voting, housing, professional licenses, custody, etc.]"

7. **Decision Timeline:**
   "You don't have to decide today. Take time to think about it. We can discuss more. But there may be a deadline [date] when the offer expires."

8. **Your Recommendation:**
   State clearly what you recommend and why. Be honest. "I think we should [take/reject] this offer because..."

**Ensuring Knowing Plea:**

Clients must knowingly, voluntarily, and intelligently enter plea. Courts ask questions to ensure this. Prepare client:

- Confirm client understands charges
- Confirm client understands sentence
- Confirm client understands what they're giving up
- Confirm client understands collateral consequences
- Confirm client is not under influence of drugs/alcohol
- Confirm client's English proficiency (interpreter if needed)
- Confirm client is not coerced or threatened
- Give client opportunity to ask questions

## Managing Difficult Client Situations

**Client Denies Guilt But Evidence Is Strong:**

Be honest. "I understand you say you didn't do this. But the prosecution has [describe evidence]. If we go to trial, I believe there's a significant risk of conviction. That doesn't mean you're guilty—it means the evidence is strong enough that a jury might convict. We should consider whether a plea offer is better than the risk of trial."

**Client Wants to Testify (Risky):**

"If you testify, the prosecution can cross-examine you. They'll try to break down your story. They'll point out inconsistencies. Some of what you say might hurt you. Before you testify, we need to prepare carefully and discuss the risks."

**Client Won't Follow Advice:**

"I understand you want to [take action]. But I'm advising against it because [reason]. You have the right to make your own decisions. But I want you to understand the consequences."

**Client Considers Self-Representation:**

"You have the right to represent yourself. But I advise against it because [reasons: lack of legal knowledge, complexity of case, emotional involvement, etc.]. If you choose to represent yourself, I can't ethically help you as much as I would as your attorney."

**Client Has Mental Health or Substance Abuse Issues:**

- Assess whether client is competent to stand trial (able to understand proceedings and assist in defense)
- Discuss whether mental health defense is possible
- If client is incarcerated, advocate for mental health services
- Consider whether treatment program might reduce sentence
- Ensure medication is provided if needed
- Document client's mental state in your file

**Client Victim of Previous Crimes or Trauma:**

- Be sensitive to client's history
- Don't re-traumatize with unnecessary questioning
- Discuss how trauma affects their responses
- Consider whether trauma is mitigation for sentencing
- Discuss whether trauma explains their behavior

**Client Doesn't Understand English or Has Low Literacy:**

- Use interpreter if needed (many courts require for non-English speakers)
- Simplify explanations (plain English, short sentences, avoid jargon)
- Use visuals (diagrams, timelines, photos) if helpful
- Repeat information multiple times
- Ask client to explain back to you (confirms understanding)
- Send written materials in clear language with illustrations

## Family Communication Management

**Client's Family and Outside Support:**

Family can be asset or liability. Manage carefully.

**What to Tell Family:**

- Charge and general case information (client controls what's disclosed)
- Case timeline and next court date
- What family should expect at trial or plea hearing
- How family can support client (letters, visits, character references for sentencing)
- That confidential communications with you are privileged

**What NOT to Tell Family:**

- Don't discuss strategy (family might gossip; prosecution listens)
- Don't discuss details client hasn't authorized you to share
- Don't promise outcomes you can't guarantee
- Don't discuss privilege issues with anyone but client

**When Family Interferes:**

- If family is pressuring client toward decision, remind client this is their choice
- If family wants you to do something unethical, decline and explain why
- If family has information helpful to defense, get it through client (privilege route) or directly with written consent

**Preparing Family for Trial or Hearing:**

If family will attend:
- Explain courtroom decorum (no emotional outbursts, appropriate dress, quiet demeanor)
- Explain that trial will be emotionally difficult
- Explain what will happen (charges read, evidence presented, client's opportunity to testify or not)
- Explain what trial result might mean
- Explain that conviction doesn't mean guilt was proven fairly; it means jury believed prosecution

## Setting Realistic Expectations

Many clients enter system with unrealistic hopes. Your job is to provide honest assessment while remaining supportive.

**Honest Assessment About Conviction Likelihood:**

"Based on the evidence, I estimate there's a [X%] chance of conviction if we go to trial. This is based on [evidence factors]. This is my professional assessment, not a guarantee."

**Honest Assessment About Sentencing:**

"If convicted, the sentencing guideline range is [range]. That means the judge likely sentences you somewhere in that range. The judge has some flexibility, but not unlimited. Given your background and this offense, I estimate [likely sentence]."

**Honest Assessment About Collateral Consequences:**

"This conviction will affect your life in these ways: [list all]. You need to think seriously about these consequences as you decide whether to plead or go to trial."

**Honest Assessment About Mitigation Opportunities:**

"We can build a mitigation case that might reduce your sentence. This requires [what you need from client: information, participation in programs, character references, etc.]. It takes time and effort, but it's worth it."

## Confidentiality Reminders

Clients often don't understand attorney-client privilege or what information is confidential.

**What Is Privileged:**

- Communications between you and client about case
- Work product and strategy discussions
- Client's mental impressions and personal information shared for legal advice
- Anything client tells you in confidence for legal advice

**What Is NOT Privileged:**

- Communications with family members present
- Communications with non-attorney employees (unless they're helping you)
- Communications on jail phone system or regular mail (not marked attorney communication)
- Communications in jail common areas where others can overhear
- Communications about future crimes

**Explain to Client:**

"Everything you tell me is confidential, except in rare cases where you're about to commit a serious crime or it's attorney-client communication between us. Don't tell your family, cellmates, or friends what we discussed. Don't talk about your case on jail phones or in areas where others can hear. If you do, it's no longer confidential."

**Privileged Visitors:**

Only you and your client are present, or support staff helping you who are bound by confidentiality.

## Immigration Consequences

Many criminal convictions trigger immigration consequences. This is critical for non-citizen clients.

**Initial Determination of Citizenship:**

Ask: "Are you a U.S. citizen? If not, what is your status?"

**Discussion of Immigration Consequences:**

For non-citizen clients, discuss:
- Does this conviction trigger deportability?
- Does this conviction trigger inadmissibility (if client travels)?
- What crimes result in loss of legal status?
- What crimes result in automatic deportation?
- Are there waivers or relief available?
- Should we negotiate plea to avoid immigration consequences?
- Has client applied for citizenship? (If pending, conviction affects application)

**Refer to Immigration Attorney:**

If client is non-citizen, consider referring to immigration attorney to assess specific consequences for their status and specific crimes.

## Documentation of Client Communications

**Document Everything:**

- Date and time of each contact (call, visit, mail)
- What was discussed
- Any agreements or decisions made
- Client's apparent mental and physical state
- Any concerning statements or behavior
- What client was informed about
- What materials were provided to client
- Client's understanding and questions

**Purpose of Documentation:**

- Protects you if client later denies saying something
- Demonstrates you provided adequate advice
- Helps manage case (don't forget discussions)
- Protects client by documenting your advice and client's choices
- Creates record if competency or sanity issues arise

**Keep Documentation Private:**

- Store in attorney file, not in client's possession
- Don't provide copies unless subpoenaed
- Mark as work product if documenting strategy
- Keep notes separate from evidence or case materials that might be discoverable

## Managing Difficult Emotions and Burnout

Criminal defense work involves clients facing terrifying situations: potential imprisonment, family separation, loss of livelihood. This is emotionally heavy work.

**Maintain Professional Boundaries:**

- You are client's advocate and advisor, not client's friend
- Don't promise outcomes you can't guarantee
- Don't take on emotional support role beyond scope of attorney relationship
- Don't become socially involved with clients
- Refer mental health issues to mental health professionals

**Manage Your Own Stress:**

- Don't internalize client's emotional distress
- Recognize compassion fatigue
- Maintain other relationships and interests
- Seek supervision or mentoring if difficult cases arise
- Take breaks between difficult client interactions

**Remain Empathetic but Professional:**

- Acknowledge client's fear and frustration
- Validate that what they're going through is difficult
- Provide honest assessment and advocacy
- Don't create false hope
- Support client in coping with reality of situation
