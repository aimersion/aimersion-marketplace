# Loss Prevention

Identify, track, and eliminate revenue loss through systematic shrinkage analysis, incident documentation, and preventive controls that protect margins without compromising customer experience.

## Shrinkage Analysis Framework

Shrinkage represents the difference between expected inventory (based on sales records) and actual inventory counts. Target: shrinkage under 1.5% of revenue for specialty retail, under 2% for general merchandise.

### Shrinkage Categories

**Administrative Shrinkage (30–40% of total)**
- Scanning errors at checkout
- Incorrect price lookups
- Failed transactions not reversed
- Receiving discrepancies (overages/shortages from vendors)
- Damage during receiving or handling

**Shoplifting (35–50%)**
- Soft goods theft (clothing, accessories)
- High-margin small items (cosmetics, electronics)
- Empty packaging concealment
- Organized retail crime (ORC) rings

**Employee Theft (10–20%)**
- Underrings (scanning lower price, paying difference)
- Sweethearting (giving discounts to friends/family)
- Voided transactions without proper documentation
- After-hours theft

**Operational Loss (5–15%)**
- Markdown errors (wrong price tag)
- Markdown processing delays
- Damaged goods not written off
- Expiration date misses (perishables)

### Calculating Shrinkage Rate

```
Shrinkage $ = Expected inventory value - Actual inventory value
Shrinkage % = (Shrinkage $ / Cost of goods sold) × 100
Shrinkage per square foot = Shrinkage $ / Store square footage
```

**Example**: Store with $500K COGS, actual shrinkage of $6,500:
- Shrinkage % = ($6,500 / $500K) × 100 = 1.3% (healthy range)

## Incident Classification and Documentation

### Incident Types and Response

**Type 1: Suspected Shoplifting**
- Initial observation: Item visible in hand/bag, unpaid
- De-escalation first: No physical confrontation
- Documentation: Date, time, description, customer appearance, items involved, witness names
- Threshold action: Confrontation only if clear and item still in-store; otherwise report to management
- Follow-up: Video review within 24 hours, loss report, pattern analysis

**Type 2: Employee Misconduct**
- Indicators: Unusual discounts, voids for own transactions, consistent underages in drawer, after-hours access without authorization
- Investigation protocol: Quiet audit of transactions for 2–4 weeks; review video alongside POS logs
- Documentation: Specific transaction IDs, amounts, dates, circumstances
- Escalation: Management + HR; never confront directly without management present
- Resolution: Termination, restitution agreement, or corrective training

**Type 3: Inventory Discrepancy**
- Detection: Cycle count variance or audit mismatch
- Threshold investigation: >2% variance on any category
- Root cause: Receiving error, markdown miss, damage, theft, or system error
- Documentation: Last receiving date, transaction review, physical walkthrough, markdown audit
- Adjustment: Only after audit completion and root-cause verification

**Type 4: Cash Handling Error**
- Register overage/shortage: >1% drawer variance triggers audit
- Till reconciliation: Count twice, verify with two people
- Documentation: Exact amount, shift dates, employee names
- Pattern detection: Flag employees with consistent shortages >3 incidents/month
- Training: Review cash handling procedures; consider supervised tills for repeat offenders

### Incident Report Template

Create a standardized report for each incident:

1. **Basic Info**: Date, time, location (aisle/zone), reporting employee name and position
2. **Incident Summary**: Clear, factual description (avoid assumptions)
3. **Classification**: Type 1–4 category
4. **Items Involved**: SKU, description, retail price, cost, quantity
5. **Estimated Loss**: Retail and cost values
6. **Parties Involved**: Employee/customer description (if applicable)
7. **Witnesses**: Names, statements, contact info
8. **Evidence**: Video timestamps, photos, damaged items
9. **Immediate Action**: What was done (confrontation, stop sale, management notification)
10. **Recommended Prevention**: Root-cause mitigation
11. **Follow-up**: Investigation results, resolution, outcome

## Employee Theft Indicators

Monitor for behavioral and transactional red flags:

### Transaction Level
- Excessive voids or refunds by specific employee
- Underrings: Supervisor override to reduce price on own purchases
- Sweethearting: Discounts on transactions without legitimate promotional reason
- No-sale transactions followed by cash draws
- Repeated "damaged merchandise" write-offs

### Behavioral Level
- Sudden unexplained income changes
- Unusual purchasing of store merchandise (above normal)
- Avoiding video camera areas or shift timing changes
- Resistance to inventory counts or audits
- Changes in schedule requests (avoidance of audits/counts)

### Environmental Factors
- High employee turnover in specific shifts/zones
- After-hours access without clear justification
- Inadequate supervision during slow periods
- Weak POS access controls (shared logins, no audit trail)

### Response Protocol

1. **Observation phase** (2–4 weeks): Monitor POS logs, video, and behavior
2. **Data collection**: Pull transaction reports, video clips, inventory variance
3. **Management consultation**: Brief manager with findings (no accusations)
4. **Quiet suspension** (if warranted): Adjust schedule or remove from till access
5. **Investigation closure**: Determine whether pattern continues (supports theft) or stops (supports suspicion)
6. **Disciplinary action**: Based on evidence, consult HR/legal before termination

## Shoplifting Deterrent Strategy

### Environmental Design (CPTED Principles)

- **Sightlines**: Remove visual obstructions; ensure checkout area can see all retail floor zones
- **Lighting**: Bright, even illumination (min. 50 foot-candles in retail areas); dark corners = theft zones
- **Fixtures**: Low shelving near entrance (max 5 feet) allows clear sight lines
- **Blind spots**: Eliminate corner dead zones; use mirrors if necessary
- **Exit control**: Single exit for checkout monitoring (or electronic article surveillance on all exits)

### Technology and Access Controls

- **Electronic Article Surveillance (EAS)**
  - Hard tags on high-risk items: small electronics, cosmetics, designer brands, luxury goods
  - Soft labels on apparel; effective when properly applied
  - Cost-benefit: Tag ~5–8% of inventory; focus on items with loss rate >2.5%
  - Gate monitoring: Alarm threshold set to minimize false positives

- **CCTV and Recording**
  - Coverage: All entrances, exits, blind spots, high-shrinkage zones, checkout areas
  - Resolution: Minimum 1080p (60fps for detail identification)
  - Retention: 30 days minimum; 90 days for high-loss areas
  - Review: Spot check weekly; intensive review post-incident

- **POS Controls**
  - Unique logins (no shared accounts)
  - Supervisor override requirements for discounts >10%
  - Audit logs: All voids, refunds, manual price changes with employee ID and timestamp
  - Video linkage: POS transactions time-synced to CCTV

### Operational Tactics

- **Greeting**: Acknowledge all customers within 30 seconds of entry (strong deterrent)
- **Associates on floor**: Visible, engaged associates in high-risk zones during peak hours
- **Signage**: Clear messaging about EAS systems and loss prevention focus
- **Checkout positioning**: Monitor sightlines; cashiers aware of bagging area
- **High-risk zone management**
  - Cosmetics/fragrances: Testers only on floor; boxed products behind counter
  - Electronics/small goods: Locked cases or counter-only sale
  - Apparel: Monitor fitting rooms; limit items; count out/in
- **Exception management**: Suspicious behavior = discreet observation, not accusation

## Cash Handling Controls

### Register Operations

**Opening Till**
- Count drawer in front of two employees; document starting balance
- Verify opening balance against system; reconcile discrepancies before using till
- All notes: Document if till not balanced or bills missing

**During Shift**
- No personal transactions
- Drawers pulled every 2 hours during peak (reduce target size)
- Tips or extra cash never held in drawer; use envelope system
- Register access: Unique login; no shared keys or overrides

**Closing Till**
- Count in front of manager + closing associate
- Verify total against system Z-report
- Document overage/shortage amount, reason if known, both signatures
- Secure deposit: Same-day or locked safe; never left unsecured overnight

### Deposit and Reconciliation

- **Frequency**: Daily deposits for stores >$50K weekly revenue; 2–3x weekly for smaller stores
- **Security**: Two employees for deposits; never alone
- **Transport**: Use armored service for high-value stores or late-night deposits
- **Bank reconciliation**: Weekly P&L; flag variances >$100 immediately
- **Documentation**: Maintain deposit slips, reconciliation reports, variance logs

### Problem Drawer Protocol

**Drawer overage >2%**
- Rare; indicates processing error at bank or overage in prior reconciliation
- Action: Hold aside; investigate with manager

**Drawer shortage >2%**
- Likely causes: Scanning errors (missed item), price ring errors, counterfeit/declined tender accepted
- Action 1 (first occurrence): Staff training on common errors; monitor next 3 shifts
- Action 2 (repeat offender): Supervised till; increased frequency of drawer pulls; possible reassignment
- Action 3 (pattern >3%): Investigation of theft or systematic error

## Inventory Discrepancy Analysis

### Cycle Counting Method

Conduct perpetual inventory counts to identify discrepancies before period-end audits:

1. **Schedule counts**: Rotate high-risk categories weekly; lower-risk categories monthly
2. **Count procedure**
   - Two-person count (separate counts, reconciled)
   - Full physical count of category; don't rely on system counts
   - Document discrepancies immediately
   - Root-cause investigation same day or next day
3. **Variance tolerance**
   - 0–0.5%: Acceptable (processing variance)
   - 0.5–2%: Investigate; likely administrative or damage-related
   - 2–5%: Escalate; likely theft or receiving error
   - >5%: Intensive investigation required; possible systematic issue

### Root-Cause Categories

**Receiving Errors**
- Vendor overages or shortages on shipments
- Receiving not matched to PO; items counted but not logged into system
- Damaged on arrival; not written off in system

**Markdown Miscues**
- Markdown processed late; sold at old price
- Markdown only on POS, not on price tags (customer confusion, scanning errors)
- Promotional discounts applied system-wide when inventory not updated

**Damage and Disposal**
- Damaged items returned to sales floor after damage write-off
- Waste (expiration, water damage, obsolescence) not documented
- Donation or sample use not logged

**Theft or Diversion**
- Unexplained variance after administrative and operational causes eliminated
- Often category-specific; focus investigation on that category

### Corrective Actions

- Administrative: Retrain staff on receiving, marking, processing
- Operational: Update procedures (e.g., same-day markdown updates, damage photo documentation)
- Security: Implement EAS, adjust staffing, increase monitoring in high-variance zones
- Vendor: Audit receiving discrepancies with vendors; negotiate chargeback for repeated errors

## ABC Analysis for Loss Prevention

Prioritize prevention spending based on financial impact:

**A-items** (top 20% of value, ~80% of inventory cost)
- Highest theft target
- Implement full controls: EAS hard tags, video focus, frequent counts, restricted access
- Staff training on these SKUs

**B-items** (middle 30% of value, ~15% of cost)
- Moderate risk
- Basic controls: Prominent placement, regular counts, pricing accuracy
- Standard staffing and monitoring

**C-items** (bottom 50% of value, ~5% of cost)
- Lower priority unless category shows high shrinkage rate
- Minimal controls; focus on administrative accuracy (markdown, receiving)

## Metrics and KPIs

Track monthly:

- **Shrinkage rate** (target: <1.5% for specialty, <2% general)
- **Loss per square foot** (compare year-over-year and to retail benchmarks)
- **Inventory variance by category** (identify problem categories)
- **Incident frequency** (track types; identify patterns)
- **Employee turnover in high-loss areas** (correlate with theft indicators)
- **Cash drawer variance** (>2% incidents per employee per month)
- **Recovery rate** (restitution/recovery as % of documented loss)
