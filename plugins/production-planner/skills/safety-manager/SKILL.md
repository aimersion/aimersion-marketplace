# Safety Manager

## OSHA Recordkeeping Requirements

OSHA (Occupational Safety and Health Administration) recordkeeping applies to most manufacturers.

### Who Must Record

**Coverage**: Most private employers in the U.S. with 11+ employees in a covered industry (manufacturing, construction, etc.)

**Exemptions**: 
- Businesses with 10 or fewer employees
- Certain low-hazard industries (retail, insurance, finance)
- Self-employed individuals

**Records required**:
1. **OSHA 300 Log** — injury and illness log (entire year)
2. **OSHA 300A** — annual summary (posted Feb 1 - Apr 30)
3. **OSHA 301** — incident report form (one per case)

### Recordable Injuries/Illnesses

An injury or illness is **recordable** if it results in ANY of:
- Work-related (causal connection to work)
- AND one or more of:
  - Days away from work
  - Restricted duty or job transfer
  - Medical treatment beyond first aid
  - Loss of consciousness
  - Diagnosis of significant injury or illness (fractured bone, puncture wound, chemical burn, etc.)

**NOT recordable**:
- First-aid only (bandage, ice, over-the-counter pain reliever, topical antibiotic)
- Single-occurrence muscle strains from normal activities
- Non-occupational injuries

### Key Dates

- **Case occurs**: Injury/illness happens at work
- **Report date**: As soon as case is identified (can be hours or days later)
- **Posting date**: OSHA 300A posted by Feb 1 (covers prior year)
- **Retention**: Keep 300 logs for 5 years (through March 31 following the year of occurrence)

### Retaliation Protection

OSHA prohibits retaliation against employees who:
- Report workplace injuries/illnesses
- Participate in safety activities
- Exercise safety rights

Penalty: Up to $10K+ per violation.

## Job Safety Analysis (JSA) / Job Hazard Analysis (JHA)

JSA is a systematic method to identify hazards in every step of a job and develop controls.

### Five-Step JSA Process

**Step 1: Select the Job**
- Choose a job with:
  - Recent injury or near-miss
  - High hazard (machinery, chemicals, heights)
  - Frequent task (cumulative risk)
  - New job (no track record)

**Step 2: Break Job into Steps**
- Divide job into logical, distinct steps (5-15 steps typical)
- Each step is an action toward the goal
- Example (assembly): Get part → Position part → Tighten bolt → Inspect → Set aside

**Step 3: Identify Hazards for Each Step**
- Ask: "What could go wrong?" "How could someone get hurt?"
- Consider interaction hazards (e.g., tightening bolt + hand in machine = pinch point)
- Don't over-think; include all potential hazards

**Step 4: Determine Controls**
- Apply Hierarchy of Controls:
  1. **Elimination**: Remove the hazard entirely (e.g., don't use this chemical)
  2. **Substitution**: Use safer alternative (e.g., non-toxic solvent instead of toxic)
  3. **Engineering Controls**: Design out the hazard (e.g., machine guard, enclosed system)
  4. **Administrative Controls**: Procedures, training, signage, work rules
  5. **PPE**: Personal protective equipment (last resort; only if above insufficient)

**Step 5: Implement & Train**
- Update work procedures/training based on JSA
- Train all workers before performing job
- Review and update JSA annually or after injury

### JSA Documentation

A written JSA template with three columns:

| Job Step | Potential Hazards | Recommended Controls |
|----------|---|---|
| Get part from bin | Pinch point (hand in bin); sharp edges; cut/laceration | Use tongs to retrieve; wear cut-resistant gloves |
| Position in fixture | Crushing hazard if clamp releases; hand trap | Two-hand control; position hands clear of clamp; machine guards in place |
| Tighten bolt | Torque wrench twist injury; over-tightening strips threads | Proper torque value documented; use calibrated tool; use grip correctly |
| Inspect | Visual strain; distraction in noisy area | Adequate lighting; quiet inspection area; use magnifier if needed |
| Set aside | Drop hazard; strain from heavy part | Proper storage height; mechanical assist if >50 lbs; ergonomic positioning |

## Hierarchy of Controls

Prioritize controls in this order (top = most effective):

```
           ELIMINATION
        (Remove hazard entirely)
                  ↓
          SUBSTITUTION
       (Safer alternative)
                  ↓
       ENGINEERING CONTROLS
        (Design out hazard)
                  ↓
      ADMINISTRATIVE CONTROLS
      (Procedures, training, signage)
                  ↓
              PPE
      (Last resort; least effective)
```

**Example — Solvent Exposure**:
1. **Eliminate**: Change process to avoid solvent (e.g., waterless cleaning)
2. **Substitute**: Use less toxic solvent
3. **Engineer**: Closed-loop system, local exhaust ventilation
4. **Admin**: Limit exposure time, rotation, training, signage
5. **PPE**: Gloves, respirator (only if controls above insufficient)

## Incident Investigation

**Goal**: Identify ROOT cause, not just blame. Prevent recurrence.

### Six-Step Process

**Step 1: Secure Scene**
- [ ] Prevent further injury (shut down equipment, block hazard area)
- [ ] Provide medical aid if needed
- [ ] Separate injured person from hazard
- [ ] Call 911 if serious
- [ ] Preserve evidence (don't move objects, take photos)

**Step 2: Immediate Corrective Actions**
- [ ] Determine: Is this a recurring hazard affecting others?
- [ ] If yes, take action NOW to protect others (stop operation, add guard, barricade)
- [ ] Do not wait for full investigation

**Step 3: Collect Facts**
- [ ] Interview injured employee (when medically stable)
- [ ] Interview witnesses separately; compare accounts
- [ ] Inspect the scene; measure distances, take photos/video
- [ ] Review work instructions, training records, maintenance logs
- [ ] Collect physical evidence (broken parts, failed equipment)

**Step 4: Root Cause Analysis**
- Use **5 Why** (ask "why" 5 times, not "who's at fault")
  - Why did hand get caught? → Guard was removed
  - Why was guard removed? → Operator said jams required access
  - Why frequent jams? → Inadequate material screening
  - Why no screening? → Equipment purchase decision didn't include it
  - Why? → Design/procurement process gap; no safety input

**Step 5: Corrective Actions**
- **Short-term**: Immediate fixes (guards, training, procedure change)
- **Long-term**: Root cause fix (design change, equipment upgrade, process redesign)
- **Verification plan**: How will we know the fix works? (weekly inspections, leading indicator)

**Step 6: Follow-up & Verification**
- [ ] Implement short-term actions within 24 hours
- [ ] Plan long-term actions (budget, timeline, owner)
- [ ] Re-train affected workers
- [ ] Verify effectiveness: Is injury rate down? Is compliance maintained?
- [ ] Share lesson learned across facility

## Near-Miss Reporting and Investigation

**Definition**: Event with potential to cause injury/illness but did not (e.g., near-fall, spilled chemical caught before contact, equipment failure avoided by luck).

**Importance**: Near-misses are "free" learning opportunities — no injury to the person, but hazard is real.

### Reporting Culture

- **Encourage reporting**: "Near-misses save lives; thank you for reporting"
- **No punishment**: Employees should never fear retaliation for reporting
- **Simplify process**: Make reporting easy (quick form, no extensive investigation)
- **Share results**: Communicate findings to prevent similar near-misses

### Near-Miss Investigation (Simplified)

| Element | Example |
|---|---|
| **What happened?** | Forklift almost hit pedestrian |
| **Why?** | Driver didn't see person; blind spot at corner |
| **Hazard** | Blind spot intersection → collision risk |
| **Action** | Install convex mirror; add audible backup alarm; paint yellow crossing |
| **Outcome** | Prevents future collisions |

## Lockout/Tagout (LOTO)

LOTO prevents unexpected startup of machinery during maintenance, preventing amputation, crushing, electrocution.

### Energy Control Program Elements

1. **Establish policy**: Who performs LOTO, when, procedures
2. **Identify energy sources**: Electrical, hydraulic, pneumatic, thermal, mechanical, chemical
3. **Develop equipment-specific procedures**: Steps for each machine
4. **Training**: All authorized employees and affected workers
5. **Audit**: Verify compliance monthly/quarterly

### Six-Step LOTO Procedure

1. **Prepare machine for shutdown**: Finish current cycle; alert workers
2. **Shut down machine**: Stop operation per normal procedures
3. **Isolate energy**: Turn off and lock/tag power switch (main disconnect)
4. **Dissipate residual energy**: Bleed pressure (hydraulic), discharge capacitors (electrical), allow hot surfaces to cool
5. **Apply locks and tags**: 
   - Each authorized worker applies own lock (not shared)
   - Tag states: Name, date, reason ("DO NOT OPERATE")
   - Lock prevents insertion of machine key
6. **Verify isolation**: Try to start machine; confirm no motion

**Re-energization**:
- [ ] Reverse steps: Remove locks (each authorized worker)
- [ ] Verify all workers clear
- [ ] Restore energy; test operation

**Critical rule**: NEVER remove another person's lock. If lock left overnight, investigate before removal.

## PPE Selection Guide

Use Hierarchy of Controls first; PPE is last resort when engineering/admin controls insufficient.

| Hazard | PPE | Type | Standards |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Eye** | Flying objects, chemicals, UV radiation | Safety glasses, goggles, face shield | ANSI Z87.1 |
| **Head** | Falling objects, electrical contact | Hard hat | ANSI Z89.1 |
| **Hand** | Cuts, abrasions, chemicals, burns | Cut gloves, chemical-resistant gloves, heat gloves | ANSI ISEA |
| **Foot** | Puncture, crush, slip | Steel-toe boots, metatarsal guards, slip-resistant | ASTM F-series |
| **Respiratory** | Dust, fumes, gases, lack of oxygen | Particulate respirator, cartridge respirator, SCBA | NIOSH approval |
| **Fall protection** | Falls from height >6 ft | Harness, lanyard, anchor point, lifeline | ANSI Z359 |

**Fit & Training**: PPE only works if properly fitted and used. Train employees on donning, use, limitations, and care.

## GHS (Globally Harmonized System) & Hazard Communication

GHS standardizes chemical hazard labeling and Safety Data Sheets (SDS).

### SDS Sections (16 sections)

1. Identification
2. Hazard identification
3. Composition/ingredients
4. First-aid measures
5. Fire-fighting measures
6. Accidental release measures
7. Handling and storage
8. Exposure controls / PPE
9. Physical and chemical properties
10. Stability and reactivity
11. Toxicological information
12. Ecological information
13. Disposal
14. Transport information
15. Regulatory information
16. Other information

**Requirement**: Maintain SDS for all chemicals; make available to employees within 15 minutes of request.

### Label Elements (GHS Format)

- **Pictogram**: Diamond symbol (flame, flame over circle, exploding bomb, health hazard, exclamation, gas cylinder, corrosion, environment)
- **Signal word**: "Danger" (high hazard) or "Warning" (moderate hazard)
- **Hazard statements**: "Flammable liquid," "Causes respiratory irritation"
- **Precautionary statements**: "Use only in well-ventilated area," "Wear gloves"

## Safety Audit Scoring

Conduct safety audits quarterly. Classify findings by severity.

### Scoring Scale

- **2 points**: Full compliance; no issues
- **1 point**: Partial compliance; minor gaps; correctable
- **0 points**: Non-compliant; significant risk; immediate action required

### Score Interpretation

| Total Score | Rating | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 90-100 | Excellent | Maintain; recognize team |
| 80-89 | Good | Minor improvements needed |
| 70-79 | Acceptable | Address gaps within 30 days |
| <70 | Poor | Critical non-compliances; plan remediation; possible regulatory exposure |

### Classification of Findings

| Severity | Definition | Action Timeline |
|---|---|---|
| **Critical** | Non-compliance on safety-critical item (e.g., missing machine guard, failed LOTO, electrical hazard) | 24-48 hours; halt work if necessary |
| **Major** | Non-compliance affecting multiple workers or high-consequence hazard (e.g., inadequate PPE stocking, incomplete training) | 2-7 days; prioritize |
| **Minor** | Low-risk observation (e.g., minor housekeeping, outdated signage) | 30-60 days |

## Leading vs. Lagging Indicators

**Lagging indicators** measure harm (reactive): Injury rate, lost-time accidents, OSHA recordables, workers' comp cost

**Leading indicators** measure prevention (proactive): Near-miss reports, safety training completion, hazard assessment updates, audit scores, LOTO audits, JSA reviews

**Best practice**: Track both. Leading indicators predict future safety performance; lagging indicate past failures.

**Example target**:
- Lagging: 0 recordable injuries (zero accident goal)
- Leading: 50+ near-miss reports per month (show reporting culture), 100% JSA completion, 95% audit score
