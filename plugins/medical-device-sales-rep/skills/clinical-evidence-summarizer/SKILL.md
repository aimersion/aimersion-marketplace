# Clinical Evidence Summarizer

Transform complex clinical studies into clear, accurate talking points that medical device reps can use in surgeon conversations.

## Study Summary Framework

For each clinical study, extract and organize:

### Quick Reference Card (30-second version)
- **Study type** — RCT, prospective cohort, retrospective, registry, meta-analysis
- **Population** — Number of patients, demographics, indications
- **Key finding** — The single most important result in one sentence
- **Clinical significance** — What this means for patient care
- **Headline stat** — The number surgeons will remember

### Detailed Summary (5-minute version)
1. **Study design and quality** — Methodology strengths and limitations, journal impact, peer-review status
2. **Patient population** — Inclusion/exclusion criteria, sample size, follow-up duration
3. **Primary endpoints** — What was measured and what were the results
4. **Secondary endpoints** — Additional findings that support the narrative
5. **Safety data** — Complication rates, adverse events, revision rates
6. **Comparison** — How results compare to competitor data or standard of care
7. **Limitations** — Honest assessment (builds credibility when reps acknowledge limitations)
8. **Clinical implications** — What should surgeons consider based on this evidence

## Evidence Hierarchy

Help reps understand and communicate the strength of evidence:
1. Systematic review / meta-analysis (strongest)
2. Randomized controlled trial (RCT)
3. Prospective cohort study
4. Retrospective study
5. Case series / case reports
6. Expert opinion / bench data (weakest clinical evidence)

## Talking Points Generation

Convert study data into conversational language:
- Lead with the clinical benefit to the patient (not the product feature)
- Use concrete numbers when compelling ("92% of patients returned to activity by 6 months")
- Anticipate surgeon objections ("The study only had 2-year follow-up, but the 5-year data is expected in Q3")
- Compare to competing evidence fairly and factually
- Know when to say "I'll get back to you" rather than overstate the evidence

## Compliance Notes

- Never misrepresent study findings or cherry-pick data out of context
- Distinguish between on-label and off-label indications
- Reference only published, peer-reviewed data in promotional conversations
- Clearly differentiate company-sponsored vs. independent research
- Do not create promotional claims — summarize evidence for educational discussion
