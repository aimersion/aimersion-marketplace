# Background Checks

Run background checks legally, fairly, and efficiently. This is one of the most compliance-sensitive parts of hiring — FCRA violations carry statutory damages of $100-$1,000 per violation, and class actions have resulted in multi-million dollar settlements. Get this right.

## Types of Background Checks

| Check Type | What It Covers | When to Use | Typical Cost |
|-----------|---------------|-------------|-------------|
| **Criminal history** | Felonies, misdemeanors by jurisdiction | Most roles | $20-50 |
| **Employment verification** | Past employer, title, dates | Most roles | $10-25/employer |
| **Education verification** | Degrees, certifications | Roles requiring specific credentials | $10-20 |
| **Reference checks** | Professional references | Most roles (do internally) | Free |
| **Credit check** | Credit history, liens, bankruptcies | Finance, fiduciary, exec roles only | $10-20 |
| **Motor vehicle records** | Driving history | Roles involving driving | $5-15 |
| **Drug screening** | Substance testing | Safety-sensitive, regulated industries | $30-60 |
| **Professional license** | License verification | Licensed roles (medical, legal, CPA) | $10-20 |
| **Social media screening** | Public social media review | Sensitive roles (use third party to avoid bias) | $20-50 |

## The Process

### Step 1: Disclose and Get Consent (Required by FCRA)
- Provide a **standalone disclosure** (not buried in the application) stating you will run a background check
- Get **written authorization** from the candidate
- This must happen before you order the check

### Step 2: Choose Your Checks
Select based on the role, not the candidate. Apply the same checks to all candidates for the same position.

**Recommended by role type:**
- **Standard (most roles):** Criminal + employment verification + reference checks
- **Finance/accounting:** Standard + credit check + education verification
- **Engineering:** Standard + education verification
- **Executive:** All applicable checks
- **Driving roles:** Standard + MVR
- **Healthcare/licensed:** Standard + professional license verification

### Step 3: Order Through a CRA (Consumer Reporting Agency)
Use a reputable background check provider. Common options for SMBs:
- Checkr, GoodHire, Sterling, HireRight, Certn

### Step 4: Review Results

If results are **clear** — proceed with hire.

If results have **findings** — follow the adverse action process (see below).

## Adverse Action Process (FCRA Required)

If you're considering not hiring someone based on background check findings, you MUST follow this two-step process:

### Pre-Adverse Action Notice
Before making a final decision, send the candidate:
1. A copy of the background check report
2. A copy of "A Summary of Your Rights Under the FCRA" (the CRA provides this)
3. A letter stating you are considering not moving forward based on the findings
4. A reasonable time to respond (typically 5 business days)

### Wait Period
Give the candidate time to:
- Dispute inaccuracies in the report
- Provide context or explanation
- Submit documentation

### Final Adverse Action Notice
If you decide not to hire after the waiting period, send:
1. Notice of the final decision
2. Name and contact info of the CRA
3. Statement that the CRA didn't make the hiring decision
4. Notice of right to dispute and get a free copy of the report

## Ban-the-Box Compliance

Many jurisdictions prohibit asking about criminal history on job applications or before a conditional offer:

**Federal contractors:** Must comply with the Fair Chance Act
**State/local laws:** Vary widely — some prohibit criminal history questions entirely until after conditional offer; others allow at interview stage

**Best practice regardless of jurisdiction:**
1. Don't ask about criminal history on the application
2. Make the offer contingent on background check
3. Run the check after the conditional offer
4. Use individualized assessment for any findings (see below)

## Individualized Assessment for Criminal Findings

If a criminal record appears, don't automatically disqualify. EEOC guidance requires individualized assessment considering:

1. **Nature and gravity of the offense** — Was it violent? Fraud? Minor misdemeanor?
2. **Time elapsed** — How long ago? Has there been evidence of rehabilitation?
3. **Nature of the job** — Is the offense related to the job duties? (Theft conviction + finance role = relevant; same conviction + software engineering = likely not)

Document your reasoning and decision. This protects you legally and ensures fairness.

## Reference Check Framework

Don't skip references — they're free and provide signal no other check can.

### Questions to Ask References
1. "How do you know [candidate] and how long did you work together?"
2. "What was [candidate]'s role and key responsibilities?"
3. "What would you say are their greatest strengths?"
4. "What areas could they develop or improve in?"
5. "Can you describe their work style and how they collaborated with the team?"
6. "Would you hire or work with them again?" (The most telling question)
7. "Is there anything else you think I should know?"

### Red Flags in References
- Reference can't speak to specific work or contributions
- Hesitation or very generic positive statements
- "I'd rather not comment on that"
- Inconsistencies with what the candidate told you
- Reference was clearly coached or is reading from a script
