# DEI Hiring Practices

Build hiring processes that are genuinely inclusive — not performative. This skill provides practical, actionable guidance for small teams that want to hire fairly but don't have a dedicated DEI function.

## Why This Matters for Growing Companies

Diverse teams outperform homogeneous ones. But at a 50-person company, the default is to hire from your network — which usually looks like you. Breaking that pattern requires intentional process changes, not just good intentions.

## The Inclusive Hiring Checklist

### Job Description Stage

- [ ] Salary range included (transparency reduces pay gaps)
- [ ] Requirements split into Required vs. Nice-to-Have (women apply at 100% match; men at 60%)
- [ ] No unnecessary degree requirements (correlates more with socioeconomic background than ability)
- [ ] Language checked for gendered, age-coded, or ability-coded terms
- [ ] No "culture fit" language — use "culture add" instead
- [ ] Posted on diverse job boards (not just LinkedIn and your network)

**Diverse Job Board Examples:**
- Jopwell, Valence (Black and Latinx professionals)
- PowerToFly (women and underrepresented groups in tech)
- Disability:IN, AbilityLinks (disability-inclusive hiring)
- Out & Equal (LGBTQ+ professionals)
- Circa, DiversityJobs (broad diversity platforms)
- Local HBCUs, community colleges, coding bootcamps

### Sourcing Stage

- [ ] Active sourcing from underrepresented communities (not just posting and waiting)
- [ ] Employee referral bonus — but track referral demographics to ensure network effects don't narrow your pipeline
- [ ] Partnerships with diversity-focused organizations, bootcamps, and community colleges
- [ ] Consider removing company name from initial resume review (reduces prestige bias)

### Screening Stage

- [ ] Structured screening criteria defined before reviewing any resumes
- [ ] Consider skills-based assessment before resume review
- [ ] Blind resume review where practical (remove names, photos, school names)
- [ ] Track pass-through rates by demographic at each stage — drops reveal where bias enters

### Interview Stage

- [ ] Structured interviews with consistent questions for all candidates
- [ ] Diverse interview panel (at minimum, not all-male or all-white panels)
- [ ] Interviewers trained on common biases (affinity, halo, horns, confirmation)
- [ ] Scoring rubrics with behavioral anchors (not gut feelings)
- [ ] Reasonable accommodations offered proactively ("Let us know if you need any accommodations for the interview process")

### Common Biases to Train Interviewers On

| Bias | Description | Countermeasure |
|------|-------------|----------------|
| **Affinity bias** | Preferring people who are similar to you | Diverse panels, structured scoring |
| **Halo effect** | One positive trait colors entire evaluation | Score each competency independently |
| **Horns effect** | One negative trait colors entire evaluation | Evidence-based scoring |
| **Confirmation bias** | Looking for evidence that confirms initial impression | Score after interview, not during |
| **Attribution bias** | Attributing success to skill (in-group) vs. luck (out-group) | Focus on specific behaviors and outcomes |
| **Recency bias** | Last interview impression dominates | Written notes immediately after each interview |

### Offer Stage

- [ ] Compensation benchmarked against market (not based on prior salary — illegal in many states)
- [ ] Same offer framework for all candidates at the same level
- [ ] Don't ask for salary history (illegal in 21+ states, and perpetuates pay gaps)
- [ ] Proactively share salary range before negotiation begins

### Accommodation Best Practices

When a candidate requests accommodations:
1. **Respond promptly and warmly** — "Absolutely, we're happy to accommodate that"
2. **Common accommodations to offer proactively:**
   - Extended interview time
   - Written questions in advance
   - Alternative assessment formats (verbal instead of written, or vice versa)
   - Remote interview option
   - Sign language interpreter
   - Flexible scheduling
3. **Never ask about the nature of a disability** — you only need to know what accommodation is needed
4. **Document the accommodation, not the disability**

### Neurodiversity Considerations

Neurodiverse candidates (autism, ADHD, dyslexia, etc.) may be disadvantaged by traditional interview formats. Consider:
- Providing questions in advance
- Allowing written responses instead of verbal
- Focusing on skills demonstrations rather than hypothetical questions
- Avoiding surprise questions or rapid context-switching
- Being explicit about expectations and evaluation criteria

## Metrics to Track

| Metric | What It Tells You | Target |
|--------|-------------------|--------|
| Pipeline diversity by stage | Where candidates drop off | Consistent pass-through rates across demographics |
| Offer acceptance rate by demographic | Whether your offers are equitable | No significant variation |
| Source effectiveness by diversity | Which channels bring diverse candidates | Diversify sources |
| Time-to-fill by demographic | Whether some groups face longer processes | No significant variation |
| Representation by level | Whether diversity exists at all levels | Matches or exceeds industry benchmarks |

## Legal Compliance Notes

- EEO reporting requirements (companies with 100+ employees or federal contractors with 50+)
- State-specific pay transparency laws (CO, CA, NY, WA, and growing)
- Ban-the-box laws for criminal history inquiries
- Salary history ban states
- Accommodation requirements under ADA

Always recommend consulting employment counsel for compliance questions — this skill provides best practices, not legal advice.
