# Offer & Negotiation

Structure offers that close candidates and handle negotiations professionally. At a growing company, losing a top candidate after weeks of interviewing is devastating — this skill helps you get to "yes."

## Offer Structure Framework

### Building the Package

| Component | What to Include | Notes |
|-----------|----------------|-------|
| **Base salary** | Annual amount, pay frequency | Benchmark at 50th-75th percentile for your market |
| **Equity** | Share count, type (RSU/ISO/NSO), vesting schedule, strike price (options), current valuation | Critical for startups; explain clearly |
| **Signing bonus** | One-time amount, clawback terms | Use to bridge gaps without raising base |
| **Annual bonus** | Target percentage, payout timing | Separate from guaranteed comp |
| **Benefits** | Health, dental, vision, 401k match, PTO | Quantify the dollar value |
| **Perks** | Remote stipend, learning budget, wellness | Small costs that candidates value highly |

### Offer Letter Essentials

Every offer letter must include:
1. Job title and reporting structure
2. Start date
3. Employment type (full-time, exempt/non-exempt)
4. Compensation details (all components)
5. At-will employment statement (if applicable)
6. Contingencies (background check, references, etc.)
7. Offer expiration date (typically 5-7 business days)
8. Benefits summary or link to details

## Negotiation Playbook

### When a Candidate Negotiates

This is normal and expected. 70%+ of candidates negotiate. Here's how to handle it:

**Step 1: Listen and understand**
- "Thank you for sharing that. Can you help me understand what's most important to you?"
- Identify their priorities: Is it base? Equity? Title? Remote flexibility? Start date?

**Step 2: Assess your room**
- Where is the offer within your band? (If at 50th percentile, you likely have room)
- What's the cost of losing this candidate and restarting the search?
- Are there non-cash levers you can pull? (title, equity, signing bonus, extra PTO)

**Step 3: Respond strategically**

| Candidate Request | Possible Responses |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| "I need $10K more in base" | Move within band if possible; if at top, offer signing bonus to bridge |
| "I have a competing offer at $X" | Ask what matters beyond comp; match if within band; don't get into a bidding war |
| "I want more equity" | Often easier to accommodate than base; consider accelerated vesting or larger grant |
| "I need a better title" | Low cost, high impact; align with your leveling framework |
| "Can I start later?" | Usually easy to accommodate; set a firm date |
| "I want more PTO" | Consider it; if policy doesn't allow exceptions, offer flexibility informally |

**Step 4: Get to a decision**
- Make your best offer clear: "This is the best we can do. Here's why we think it's competitive."
- Set a deadline: "We'd love your answer by [date]. Take the time you need, but we do have other candidates in process."
- Don't pressure — but don't leave it open-ended either.

### Handling Counter-Offers

When a candidate gets a counteroffer from their current employer:

**The data:** 50-80% of people who accept counteroffers leave within 18 months anyway. The reasons they wanted to leave usually haven't changed.

**What to say:**
- "That's great that your current company values you. Take your time to think about it."
- "I'd encourage you to think about why you started looking in the first place — does the counteroffer address those reasons?"
- Don't trash their current employer or pressure them.
- Do restate what makes your opportunity different (growth, mission, team, scope).

**What not to do:**
- Don't get into a bidding war with their current employer
- Don't withdraw the offer in frustration
- Don't make ultimatums

### Handling Competing Offers

When a candidate has multiple offers:

1. **Ask about their decision criteria** — what matters most to them beyond compensation?
2. **Highlight your differentiators** — culture, growth, mission, team quality
3. **Accelerate your timeline** — if they have a deadline, match it
4. **Connect them with the team** — sometimes a conversation with their future peers closes the deal
5. **Make your offer your best offer** — don't hold back and negotiate in rounds

### When to Walk Away

Sometimes a candidate's expectations exceed what the role can support:
- Their ask is 20%+ above your band maximum
- They want a title that doesn't exist in your framework
- The negotiation is adversarial rather than collaborative
- They're using you as leverage for a counteroffer and aren't genuinely interested

In these cases, be gracious: "We think highly of you, but we can't meet that number for this role. If anything changes, we'd love to reconnect."

## Salary History Ban Compliance

In many jurisdictions, you cannot ask candidates about their current or prior salary. Instead:

**Ask:** "What are your compensation expectations for this role?"
**Ask:** "Our range for this role is $X-$Y. Does that align with your expectations?"
**Don't ask:** "What do you currently make?" / "What was your salary at your last job?"

States with salary history bans include (as of 2025): CA, CO, CT, DE, HI, IL, MA, MD, NJ, NY, OR, PA (Philadelphia), RI, VT, WA, and others. The list is growing — when in doubt, don't ask.

## Offer Declined Follow-Up

When an offer is declined:
1. **Respond graciously** — "We're disappointed but respect your decision. Thank you for your time."
2. **Ask for feedback** — "Would you be willing to share what influenced your decision? It helps us improve."
3. **Keep the door open** — "If circumstances change, we'd welcome a conversation."
4. **Log the reason** — track why offers are declined to identify patterns (comp too low? slow process? bad candidate experience?)
