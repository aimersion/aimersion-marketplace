# Contractor-to-FTE Conversion

Convert contractors to full-time employees without overpaying, underpaying, or creating compliance problems. This is increasingly common at growing companies — you start with contractors for flexibility, then want to bring the best ones in permanently.

## When to Convert

Good signals that it's time to convert:
- Contractor has been with you 6+ months and is embedded in the team
- They're doing work that looks like an employee's (set hours, company tools, managed work)
- You want to retain them long-term and they're flight risk as a contractor
- Misclassification risk — if they look like an employee, they probably should be one

Red flags to address first:
- Converting just to save money (contractor rates include self-employment costs)
- Contractor doesn't want to convert (discuss honestly)
- Role is genuinely temporary or project-based

## Compensation Conversion Calculator

Contractor rates include costs that employers pay for employees. A naive conversion ("you make $80/hr, so $160K salary") typically overpays by 20-30%.

### The Math

```
Contractor hourly rate: $[X]/hr
Annual equivalent (2,080 hours): $[Y]

Subtract contractor-borne costs:
- Self-employment tax (7.65%): -$[Z]
- Health insurance (est. $6,000-$15,000/yr): -$[Z]
- PTO value (assume 15-20 days, 0 as contractor): -$[Z]
- Retirement match (if offering): -$[Z]
- Equipment/software (if contractor provides own): -$[Z]

Adjusted base salary range: $[A] - $[B]

Add employer costs:
- FICA (7.65%): +$[Z]
- Health insurance contribution: +$[Z]
- PTO cost: +$[Z]
- Workers' comp: +$[Z]
- Other benefits: +$[Z]

Total employer cost: $[C]
```

**Rule of thumb:** FTE base salary is typically 60-75% of the contractor's annualized rate, but the total compensation package (salary + benefits + equity) should be competitive with what they could earn as a contractor.

### Conversation Framework

1. **Acknowledge the transition openly:** "We value your work and want to bring you on full-time. Let's talk about what that looks like."
2. **Present total comp, not just salary:** The base number will be lower — show the full picture including benefits, equity, PTO, and growth opportunities.
3. **Be transparent about the math:** Explain why the base is different from their contractor rate.
4. **Give them time:** This is a big decision. Give 1-2 weeks to decide.

## Compliance Checklist

### Before the Conversion
- [ ] Confirm the person was properly classified as a contractor (if they were misclassified, consult legal counsel)
- [ ] Check for any contractual obligations — do you owe a staffing agency a conversion fee?
- [ ] Review non-compete or non-solicitation clauses in their contractor agreement
- [ ] Determine the employment start date (usually, there should be a clear end to the contractor relationship and start of employment)

### Legal & Tax
- [ ] End the contractor agreement formally (written termination notice)
- [ ] Execute a new employment agreement
- [ ] Collect W-4 (instead of W-9)
- [ ] Enroll in payroll system
- [ ] I-9 verification (required even if previously on 1099)
- [ ] State new hire reporting
- [ ] Workers' compensation enrollment

### Benefits & Systems
- [ ] Enroll in health insurance (note: may have waiting period)
- [ ] Set up retirement plan enrollment
- [ ] Issue employee equipment (if they were using their own)
- [ ] Transition from contractor tools to employee tools (email, Slack, etc.)
- [ ] Update access permissions to employee level
- [ ] Add to org chart and reporting structure

### Cultural Transition
- [ ] Announce the conversion to the team (celebrate it!)
- [ ] Assign to team meetings and recurring syncs they may not have been included in
- [ ] Include in performance review cycle
- [ ] Set 30/60/90 day goals as an employee (different from contractor deliverables)
- [ ] Manager 1:1 to discuss career growth path (contractors don't usually get this)

## International Contractor Conversions

If converting an international contractor:
- Consider an Employer of Record (EOR) if you don't have a local entity
- Research local employment law requirements (notice periods, mandatory benefits, termination protections)
- Factor in mandatory benefits that may not exist in the US (13th-month pay, mandatory severance, etc.)
- Currency and exchange rate considerations for compensation
