# Entity Formation Helper Skill

## Overview

This skill assists with entity formation for new business ventures or subsidiary structures. The entity-formation-helper enables entity type selection, formation document preparation, state-specific filing requirements, ongoing compliance setup, and post-formation administration to ensure proper legal structure and regulatory compliance.

## Core Capabilities

### 1. Entity Type Selection Framework

**Entity Type Decision Factors**

Business formation begins with entity type selection. The appropriate choice depends on:
- **Liability protection needs** — What personal liability exposure exists?
- **Tax treatment** — What's the optimal tax structure (pass-through vs. corporate)?
- **Ownership structure** — Single owner, multiple owners, or future investors?
- **Fundraising plans** — Will you raise capital from external investors?
- **Operating complexity** — What governance/management structure is needed?
- **State of formation** — Where will the business operate and form?
- **Timeline** — How quickly does the entity need to be formed?

### Entity Type Overview

**Limited Liability Company (LLC)**

*Structure:* Pass-through entity with liability protection. Members are not personally liable for entity's debts or liabilities (exceptions: personal guarantees, certain statutory obligations).

*Tax Treatment:* Default: pass-through to members' personal returns. Election available to be taxed as S-Corporation (tax savings if owner has W-2 wages).

*Governance:* Flexible; default is member-managed (like partnership) or manager-managed (like corporation). Governed by operating agreement (not required but strongly recommended).

*Ownership:* Unlimited number of owners; no restrictions on owner types (individuals, corporations, foreign investors). Flexible profit/loss sharing not required to match ownership percentages.

*Liability Protection:* Strong liability protection for members; business debts don't pierce to members' personal assets. Exceptions: personal guarantees, statutory obligations (payroll taxes, environmental), fraud/piercing the veil.

*Fundraising:* Straightforward capital contributions; flexible equity arrangements. Less attractive to venture capital investors (prefer C-Corps for liquidity and tax treatment) but acceptable for angel investment and private equity.

*Compliance Burden:* Low to moderate. Operating agreement optional but essential for multi-member LLCs. Annual reporting/registration requirements vary by state.

*Conversion/Exit:* Can convert to corporation; can merge into buyer's entity. Pass-through structure sometimes unattractive for acquisition (tax implications to members).

*Best For:* Small to mid-sized businesses, real estate ventures, professional services (where liability protection needed), family businesses, businesses expecting no external capital.

*Formation Cost:* $300-$1,500 (depending on state and complexity).

---

**C-Corporation**

*Structure:* Separate legal entity distinct from owners. Shareholders not personally liable for corporation's debts or liabilities (with exceptions for personal guarantees, piercing the veil).

*Tax Treatment:* Corporation pays federal income tax on profits (separate tax; shareholders also taxed on dividends). Results in "double taxation" — profit taxed at corporate level, then dividend taxed to shareholders.

*Governance:* Board of directors elected by shareholders; board manages business and appoints officers. Governed by articles of incorporation and bylaws.

*Ownership:* Unlimited number of shareholders; no restrictions on shareholder types (with exceptions for S-Corp election).

*Liability Protection:* Strong liability protection for shareholders; corporate debts don't pierce to shareholder assets (unless pierce the veil, personal guarantee, or statutory obligation).

*Fundraising:* Preferred entity for institutional capital (venture capital, private equity). Investors comfortable with corporate structure. Ability to issue multiple classes of stock with different rights. Stock option plans straightforward (not taxable to employees until sale).

*Compliance Burden:* Moderate to high. Articles of incorporation and bylaws required. Board meetings and shareholder meetings required (annual minimum). Corporate minutes required for major decisions. More formal governance requirements than LLC.

*Conversion/Exit:* Straightforward acquisition structure (buyer purchases stock or assets). Tax-deferred reorganizations available for certain acquisitions (Section 368 reorganizations).

*Best For:* Businesses planning to raise institutional capital, technology startups, businesses with multiple stakeholders requiring formal governance, businesses planning acquisition/IPO.

*Formation Cost:* $500-$3,000 (depending on state and complexity).

---

**S-Corporation (Tax Election)**

*Structure:* C-Corporation with S-Corporation tax election. Separate legal entity with liability protection, but taxed as pass-through entity.

*Tax Treatment:* Income passes through to shareholders' personal returns; shareholders don't pay corporate tax. Potential for significant tax savings if shareholders have substantial W-2 wages (self-employment tax savings on distributions).

*Governance:* Same as C-Corporation — board of directors, articles, bylaws, formal meetings.

*Ownership:* Must have 100 or fewer shareholders; shareholders must be U.S. citizens or residents. Only one class of stock (voting/nonvoting, but not economic classes).

*Liability Protection:* Same as C-Corporation.

*Fundraising:* Less attractive to institutional investors (restrictions on shareholder types, one class of stock, complexity of S-Corp tax election). Works well for family businesses, owner-operated businesses.

*Compliance Burden:* Same as C-Corporation governance requirements, plus S-Corp tax election and annual reporting. Must maintain reasonable W-2 wage for shareholder-employees.

*Best For:* Owner-operated businesses expecting profitable returns (to benefit from pass-through taxation and self-employment tax savings), family businesses, professional services where pass-through taxation desired but formal governance preferred.

*Formation Cost:* $500-$3,000 (plus $600+ annual cost for tax return preparation; tax savings often exceed additional costs).

---

**Limited Partnership (LP)**

*Structure:* Partnership with general partner(s) (liable) and limited partners (not liable). General partner manages business; limited partners are passive investors.

*Tax Treatment:* Pass-through; income flows to partners' personal returns.

*Governance:* General partner has full control and liability; limited partners have no management involvement or liability. Governed by partnership agreement.

*Ownership:* General partners have unlimited liability; limited partners have liability limited to capital contribution.

*Fundraising:* Traditional structure for investment funds (private equity, real estate, venture funds). Limited partners are passive investors.

*Compliance Burden:* Moderate; requires partnership agreement, annual reporting, potential personal guarantees from general partner.

*Best For:* Investment funds, real estate ventures with passive investor capital, situations where clear investor/manager distinction needed.

*Formation Cost:* $500-$2,000.

---

**Limited Liability Partnership (LLP)**

*Structure:* Partnership with liability protection for all partners. Each partner has limited liability (not liable for other partners' malpractice or conduct, though liable for own conduct).

*Tax Treatment:* Pass-through; income flows to partners' personal returns.

*Governance:* Partners manage business; governed by partnership agreement. Usually equal voting unless agreement specifies otherwise.

*Ownership:* Flexible; usually restricted to individuals (accountants, lawyers) by professional regulations.

*Compliance Burden:* Moderate; partnership agreement recommended, annual reporting.

*Best For:* Professional services (accounting firms, law firms, architectural firms, medical practices) where liability protection for partners important, and partnership structure preferred.

*Formation Cost:* $500-$2,000.

---

**Sole Proprietorship**

*Structure:* No separate legal entity. Business owner is the business.

*Tax Treatment:* Business income flows directly to owner's personal return (Schedule C).

*Governance:* Sole owner has full control and responsibility.

*Ownership:* One owner only.

*Liability Protection:* NO liability protection. Owner is personally liable for all business debts and liabilities.

*Fundraising:* Difficult to raise capital; investors typically require entity formation.

*Compliance Burden:* Low; no separate business entity filing required (though business license may be needed).

*Best For:* Solo service providers (freelancers, consultants) with minimal liability exposure, startup testing before formation, sole proprietor with no plans to raise capital or hire employees.

*Formation Cost:* $0-$300 (business license only).

---

**Benefit Corporation**

*Structure:* C-Corporation with additional legal obligation to consider impact on stakeholders (employees, customers, environment, community) alongside shareholder profits.

*Tax Treatment:* Same as C-Corporation (corporate income tax plus shareholder tax on dividends).

*Governance:* Board of directors with requirement to consider stakeholder impact. Third-party certification of benefit impact typically required.

*Liability Protection:* Same as C-Corporation.

*Compliance Burden:* Same as C-Corporation, plus requirement to assess and report on stakeholder impact; maintain third-party certification.

*Best For:* Social enterprises, businesses with explicit mission to benefit stakeholders alongside profits, venture capital-backed companies wanting to signal stakeholder commitment.

*Formation Cost:* $500-$3,000 plus annual certification cost ($1,000-$5,000).

### 3. Formation Document Preparation

**Articles of Formation/Incorporation**

The articles establish the entity's legal existence and basic parameters. Key provisions:

- **Business name** — Legal name exactly as will be registered; should not include "Inc." or "LLC" in articles (title requirements vary by state)
- **Registered agent and address** — Physical address for service of process; often commercial registered agent service (nationwide companies: $50-$200/year)
- **Principal place of business** — May be same as registered agent address or actual business location
- **Business purpose** — Usually broad language ("any lawful business purpose") to allow flexibility
- **Dissolution/winding up procedures** — Often standard; specifies how assets distributed if entity dissolves
- **Tax ID election** — May be included in articles or elected separately (Form 2553 for S-Corp)

**State-Specific Variations:**
- Delaware: Minimal required information; very flexible; incorporated with favorable corporate law
- California: Requires more specific information; Franchise Tax Board rules stricter
- New York: Mid-range requirements; specific form required
- Texas: Simple form; very low cost; favorable statutory structure

**Operating Agreement (LLC) or Bylaws (Corporation)**

Governance document establishing how the entity operates. Should address:

**For LLC Operating Agreement:**
- **Management structure** — Member-managed or manager-managed; managers' authority and responsibilities
- **Capital contributions** — Initial contributions, additional capital calls, return of capital
- **Profit and loss allocation** — Usually proportionate to ownership, but can be nonstandard
- **Distributions** — When and how profits distributed; restrictions on distributions
- **Voting and approval rights** — Approval requirements for major decisions (hiring/firing managers, capital expenditures, contracts, amendments)
- **Member buyout/transfer restrictions** — Right of first refusal, transfer approval, valuation mechanics
- **Dissolution/liquidation** — Process for dissolving LLC if needed; asset distribution
- **Amendment procedures** — How operating agreement can be modified (unanimous vs. majority vote)
- **Indemnification** — Protection for members and managers against liability for actions within authority
- **Confidentiality and non-compete** — If members have obligations to entity or non-compete

**For Corporation Bylaws:**
- **Board of directors** — Number of directors, election/removal, term length, board meetings, quorum, voting
- **Officers** — Titles, roles, authority, election, removal, compensation
- **Shareholder meetings** — Annual meetings, special meetings, notice requirements, quorum, voting procedures
- **Voting rights** — Voting per share, cumulative voting, voting agreement enforcement
- **Stock** — Authorized shares, par value, classes of stock, voting/dividend rights, issuance procedures
- **Distributions** — Dividend authority and procedures
- **Amendments** — How bylaws can be modified (board vs. shareholder approval)
- **Indemnification** — Protection for directors and officers
- **Conflict of interest** — Process for handling director conflicts of interest
- **Corporate records** — Record-keeping requirements, inspection rights

### 4. State-Specific Formation Requirements

**Delaware Formation** (favored for out-of-state businesses and capital-intensive ventures)

*Advantages:*
- Well-developed corporate law; extensive case law clarifies provisions
- Delaware courts (Court of Chancery) specialized in business law
- Flexibility in corporate governance
- Privacy — owners' names not disclosed in public filings
- No Delaware operations required (can be incorporated in Delaware, operate elsewhere)

*Requirements:*
- Certified registered agent in Delaware (required)
- Certificate of Incorporation filed with Delaware Secretary of State
- Minimal required provisions in certificate
- Annual Delaware Franchise Tax ($300+, varying by authorized capital)
- Annual filing of annual report (Form 1-U)

*Cost:* $100-$300 filing fee + $200-$300 annual registered agent + $300+ annual franchise tax = $600-$1,000+ annually

*Best For:* Technology startups planning venture capital funding, acquisition targets, multi-state operations, privacy preferences

---

**California Formation** (required if business operates in California)

*Advantages:*
- Home state incorporation for California operations
- No requirement for California incorporation if LLC or corp operates elsewhere
- If other-state entity, need Certificate of Qualification

*Requirements:*
- Articles of Organization (LLC) or Articles of Incorporation (Corp) filed with California Secretary of State
- Franchise Tax Board Initial Statement of Business Activities (Form 3522)
- Registered agent or authorized officer for service of process
- California Statement of Information filing (annual or biennial, depending on entity type)
- Annual Franchise Tax minimum $800 (corporations); exempt for LLCs unless they have California income

*Cost:* $70-$100 filing + $800+ annual franchise tax (corps) = $870-$900+ annually

*Best For:* Businesses headquartered in California with significant operations there

---

**Other States** (selective based on headquarters location)

- **Texas:** Favorable business laws, low filing costs, no annual franchise tax for LLCs, limited income. Popular for out-of-state businesses.
- **Nevada:** Very favorable corporate law, no corporate income tax, privacy protections. Costs competitive but annual Franchise Tax required.
- **Wyoming:** Favorable LLC law, no corporate income tax, business-friendly legislature. Growing popularity for LLC formation.
- **New York:** Required for New York operations, mid-range costs and requirements.

### 5. Post-Formation Administration

**Obtain EIN (Employer Identification Number)**

Form SS-4 filed with IRS (or online at IRS.gov). Free.

**Required for:**
- Multi-member LLCs (automatic, not treated as sole proprietor)
- Corporations (all)
- S-Corps (all)
- LPs and LLPs (all)
- Single-member LLC electing corporate tax treatment

**Not required for:**
- Single-member LLC with no employees (can use owner's SSN)

**Timeline:** Same-day if filed online; 4-6 weeks if paper form

---

**Set Up Business Banking**

- Open business bank account (EIN required for corporations/partnerships; SSN or EIN for single-member LLC)
- Obtain business credit card(s)
- Establish accounting procedures (separate business finances from personal)

---

**Ongoing Compliance Setup**

- **Annual reports/renewals** — File annually to maintain good standing; requirements vary by state
- **Registered agent renewals** — If using third-party registered agent, renew annually
- **Tax elections** — File S-Corp election (Form 2553) if applicable; deadline typically 15 days from business start
- **Business licenses** — Obtain required local/state/industry licenses
- **Payroll setup** — Register with state for unemployment insurance, state income tax withholding if hiring employees
- **Accounting systems** — Establish bookkeeping system, hire accountant or implement QuickBooks/Xero

---

**Equity/Capitalization Setup**

- **Equity records** — Maintain cap table, stock certificate registers, option pool documentation
- **Grant documents** — If issuing options, prepare option plan and grant agreements
- **Equity vesting** — Implement vesting schedules (typical 4-year vest with 1-year cliff)
- **Stock ledger** — Maintain detailed record of all equity issuances, transfers, cancellations

---

### 6. Common Issues and Solutions

**Problem:** Founder uses personal bank account instead of business account; commingling funds.

*Solution:* Establish separate business bank account immediately; implement accounting discipline; separate finances reduce liability protection piercing risk.

---

**Problem:** Operating agreement not updated as business evolves; capital structure, roles change without documentation.

*Solution:* Establish routine to review and update operating agreement annually; document major decisions (capital contributions, new members, profit allocation changes).

---

**Problem:** Capitalization table disorganized; unclear who owns what percentage.

*Solution:* Use cap table management tool (Carta, Excel template); update cap table with every equity grant, transfer, or buyback; ensure consistency with stock ledger.

---

**Problem:** Equity not formally granted; founder assumes ownership through handshake agreement; vesting informal.

*Solution:* Issue written equity grant agreements to all equity holders; specify vesting schedule, cliff, acceleration provisions; update as changes occur.

---

**Problem:** S-Corp tax election deadline missed; loses tax benefits for year.

*Solution:* Calendar S-Corp election deadline (Form 2553 due by 15 days from business start or March 15 of tax year); file early; work with CPA to ensure compliance.

---

**Problem:** Registered agent not notifying entity of service of legal documents; lawsuit proceeds unnoticed.

*Solution:* Ensure entity's address on file matches registered agent; maintain regular communication with registered agent; implement system to receive and process legal notices promptly.

## Application Framework

Use this skill to:

1. **Assess business profile** — Understand liability, tax, capital, and governance needs
2. **Recommend entity type** — Based on assessment, recommend optimal entity structure
3. **Prepare formation documents** — Draft articles, operating agreement/bylaws specific to chosen entity and state
4. **Identify state requirements** — Ensure compliance with state-specific filing, naming, agent requirements
5. **Establish post-formation procedures** — Create checklist for EIN, banking, ongoing compliance, accounting
6. **Document equity structure** — Establish cap table, equity grant process, vesting mechanics
7. **Implement ongoing compliance** — Calendar recurring filing deadlines, tax elections, business licenses
