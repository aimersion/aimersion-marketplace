# Treatment Plan Presenter

Turn clinical treatment plans into clear, empathetic patient presentations that drive case acceptance.

## Case Acceptance Psychology

The average dental practice case acceptance rate is 50-60%. Top practices achieve 80-90%. The difference isn't clinical skill — it's communication. Patients decline treatment because they don't understand it, don't see the value, fear the cost, or fear the procedure.

### The Three Patient Decision Factors

1. **Understanding** — "What's wrong and what will fix it?"
2. **Value** — "Is this worth the money and time?"
3. **Trust** — "Do I believe this dentist has my best interests in mind?"

Address all three in every presentation.

## Treatment Presentation Framework

### Step 1: Show, Don't Just Tell
Use intraoral photos, X-rays, or diagrams to show the patient what you see. Point to the specific area. Patients who can see the problem are far more likely to accept treatment.

### Step 2: Explain What Happens If Untreated
Be honest but not alarmist. Use a progression framework:
- "Right now, this is [small/moderate/significant]"
- "Without treatment, over the next [timeframe], it will likely [progression]"
- "At that point, the treatment becomes [more complex/more expensive/less predictable]"

### Step 3: Present the Recommended Treatment
- What the procedure involves in plain language
- How many visits and how long each visit takes
- What they'll experience (numbness, pressure, sound — set expectations)
- Recovery and aftercare
- How long the restoration should last

### Step 4: Present Alternatives (When Applicable)
Always present at least two options when clinically appropriate. This gives the patient agency. Include the "do nothing" option with honest consequences.

### Step 5: Discuss Investment
- Lead with insurance coverage (what's covered)
- Present patient portion clearly
- Immediately follow with payment options
- Never apologize for the cost — frame as health investment

## Handling Common Objections

**"It doesn't hurt, so why fix it?"**
→ Most dental problems don't hurt until they're advanced. Explain: cavities don't hurt until they reach the nerve. By then, a simple filling becomes a root canal and crown.

**"I can't afford it right now"**
→ Break it into phases. Present the most urgent treatment first. Offer payment plans. Mention that delaying often increases the final cost.

**"I need to think about it"**
→ Respect their timeline. Offer to send the treatment plan home (this is where the email-friendly version comes in). Set a follow-up reminder. Ask: "Is there a specific concern I can help you think through?"

**"I'm scared / I had a bad experience"**
→ Validate the fear. Describe comfort options (topical anesthetic, nitrous oxide, oral sedation, breaks during treatment, hand-raising signal). Offer to start with the simplest procedure to build trust.

**"My last dentist never said I needed this"**
→ Never criticize the previous dentist. Say: "Dental conditions change over time, and different dentists may catch things at different stages. Here's what I'm seeing today."
