# Client Intake Essentials

Successful legal practice begins with thorough intake. A well-conducted intake process captures all necessary information for conflict checking, case analysis, fee setting, and engagement. This skill guides you through building effective intake questionnaires, running conflict checks, and organizing initial client information for efficient case management.

## Why Intake Matters

Poor intake leads to missed conflicts of interest, inadequate fee estimates, overlooked legal issues, and unhappy clients. Thorough intake prevents these problems by:

- **Identifying conflicts** before representation begins, protecting both client and firm
- **Capturing critical facts** that shape legal strategy and timeline
- **Setting accurate fees** by understanding scope, complexity, and work required
- **Managing expectations** through clear communication of services and limitations
- **Creating documentation** that protects both attorney and client if disputes arise
- **Enabling delegation** with complete information for associates or paralegals

## The Intake Process

An effective intake process has several stages:

### 1. Initial Consultation
The first meeting (phone or in-person) is your opportunity to:
- Briefly describe your firm's services and experience
- Ask open-ended questions about the client's situation
- Listen carefully for red flags (conflicts, multiple parties, complex jurisdictions)
- Assess the client's sophistication and communication needs
- Determine if this is a case your firm should take
- Establish rapport and set expectations

During initial consultation, ask:
- How did you find us?
- Briefly, what brings you in today?
- Have you worked with an attorney on this matter before?
- Do you have any relationships with others involved in this matter?
- What outcome are you hoping for?
- What's your timeline?
- Is there a deadline approaching?

### 2. Comprehensive Questionnaire
After initial consultation, provide a detailed intake questionnaire. This document captures:
- Complete party identification (names, addresses, contact info, relationship to client)
- Detailed facts and timeline
- Prior legal involvement and current counsel
- Document inventory
- Client goals and expectations
- Conflict check information
- Insurance or funding sources

The questionnaire serves as:
- A checklist ensuring nothing is missed
- A reference document for future team members
- Evidence of diligence if questions arise later
- A basis for fee agreements and cost estimates

### 3. Conflict Check
Before signing a retainer, you MUST:
- Run all parties' names through your conflicts database
- Ask the client directly if they've had prior dealings with any opposing party
- Check if your firm has any connection to opposing parties
- Review any referral sources for potential conflicts
- Document the conflict check in your file
- Obtain conflict waivers if necessary and appropriate

Conflict checking is mandatory under Model Rule 1.7 (concurrent conflicts) and 1.9 (former clients). Missing a conflict can result in disqualification and malpractice liability.

### 4. Information Organization
Organize intake information in a way that allows quick reference:
- Client contact information at top of file
- Parties and key dates highlighted
- Legal issues and claims clearly summarized
- Timeline of events created
- Document inventory listed
- Prior representation clearly noted
- Conflict check results documented
- Engagement terms summarized

## Practice-Area-Specific Intake Approaches

Different practice areas require different information focus:

### Personal Injury
Focus on:
- Detailed description of accident/incident with date, time, location
- Injuries sustained (medical records review)
- Medical treatment (doctors, hospitals, costs)
- Wage loss and lost capacity
- Property damage
- Insurance information (both sides)
- Witness information (names, contact, relationship to parties)
- Prior similar claims or injuries
- Liability witnesses (who saw what)
- Pain and suffering details

### Family Law
Focus on:
- Other spouse/partner name and address
- Children (names, ages, custody arrangement desired)
- Property overview (home, vehicles, retirement accounts, business)
- Income and employment
- Child support and alimony considerations
- Domestic violence or safety concerns
- Prior family law proceedings
- Mediation/collaborative options discussed with client
- Timeline for divorce or family change

### Criminal Defense
Focus on:
- Charges and alleged conduct
- Arrest circumstances and detention status
- Bail/bond information
- Prior criminal history
- Prosecution discovery received
- Witness information (defense and prosecution)
- Prior representation history
- Client employment/community ties
- Mental health or substance abuse issues
- Immigration status if relevant

### Estate Planning
Focus on:
- Current will or trust documents
- Asset inventory (real property, financial accounts, business interests)
- Beneficiary goals and family structure
- Tax considerations
- Prior estate documents or name changes
- Healthcare wishes and proxy designations
- Guardianship plans for minor children
- Special needs beneficiary considerations
- Client health status and urgency

### Business/Contract
Focus on:
- Contract/agreement details (date, parties, consideration)
- Breach allegations (what was promised, what was done)
- Damages claimed and calculation
- Prior communications with other party
- Any third parties affected
- Insurance coverage for claim
- Prior disputes or relationship history
- Desire for relationship continuation or termination

## Conflict Check Procedures

Conflict checking is both art and science. Use these procedures:

### Step 1: Direct Client Questions
Ask client explicitly:
- Have any of the opposing parties, their families, or their businesses been clients of yours in the past five years?
- Do you have any personal or business relationships with the opposing party or their family?
- Have you been in disputes with anyone representing the other side?
- Are there any situations where someone might think you have a conflict?

### Step 2: Firm Database Review
Check your firm's conflicts database (or manual system) for:
- Opposing party name, variations, and business names
- Opposing party family members and business associates
- Opposing counsel name and firm
- Any party mentioned in the matter

### Step 3: Name Variations and Aliases
Remember that clients sometimes use:
- Nicknames (Robert/Bob, William/Bill)
- Married and maiden names
- Business trading names
- Abbreviated or spelled variations

### Step 4: Documentation
Document in the file:
- Date of conflict check
- Names checked
- System used (database name, manual search, etc.)
- Results (no conflicts found, or conflicts identified and waived)
- If conflicts found: details and waiver documentation

### Step 5: Ongoing Vigilance
Even after engagement:
- Watch for new party information that might trigger conflicts
- Update conflict check if matter expands to new parties
- Ask client periodically about new information
- Review opposing counsel's work product for potential conflicts

## Organizing the Intake File

A well-organized intake file should allow any member of your firm to quickly understand the case. Use this structure:

```
CLIENT INTAKE FILE
├── Client Information (name, contact, emergency contact)
├── Matter Summary (brief description, key dates, claims)
├── Parties and Representation
├── Conflict Check Documentation
├── Timeline of Events
├── Intake Questionnaire (completed)
├── Documents Received (list and summary)
├── Engagement Letter (signed)
├── Fee Agreement
├── Initial Assessment (legal issues, risks, timeline)
└── Action Items from Intake
```

Each section should be current and reviewed regularly.

## Red Flags During Intake

Watch for situations that require special attention:

- **Multiple representation**: Client has another attorney (need to coordinate)
- **Timing urgency**: Approaching deadline (need immediate court filing)
- **Undisclosed conflicts**: Party reveals information suggesting conflict
- **Client sophistication mismatch**: Very complex case for unsophisticated client
- **Fee disputes likely**: Client focused heavily on cost or negotiating rates
- **Unrealistic expectations**: Client wants guaranteed outcome or unrealistic timeline
- **Mental health concerns**: Client exhibiting mental illness or substance abuse
- **Threat of harm**: Client or opposing party expressing violence
- **Fraudulent intent**: Client asking you to help with illegal or fraudulent conduct
- **Communication barriers**: Language, hearing, or mental ability to communicate

Each red flag requires specific handling — sometimes declining representation.

## Key Takeaways

- Intake is your opportunity to build rapport, gather information, and protect your firm
- Thorough conflict checking prevents disqualification and malpractice
- Tailored questionnaires capture the information you actually need
- Well-organized intake documentation allows efficient case management
- Regular review of intake information keeps you on track with client expectations
- Investment in intake saves time and problems later in the representation
