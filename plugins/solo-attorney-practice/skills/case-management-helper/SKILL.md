# Case Management and Deadline Tracking

Missing a deadline is one of the few guaranteed ways to lose a client, damage your reputation, and face malpractice liability. Effective case management systems ensure deadlines are tracked, tasks are organized, and nothing falls through the cracks. This skill provides frameworks for organizing cases and managing timelines.

## Why Case Management Matters

Effective case management:
- **Prevents malpractice** — Most malpractice claims arise from missed deadlines
- **Improves client satisfaction** — Clients appreciate organized, timely work
- **Increases profitability** — Organized cases are handled more efficiently
- **Reduces stress** — You know what's happening in each case
- **Demonstrates competence** — Well-managed cases show professional mastery
- **Enables delegation** — Clear task lists allow paralegals/associates to assist
- **Protects against bar discipline** — Timely communication and diligence satisfy ethics rules
- **Creates leverage** — Clear deadlines encourage client decision-making

### Step 2: Master Task List

Create a master task list for the case type and current stage. See the task-lists-by-case-type.md reference for detailed checklists by practice area.

Each task should include:
- Task name and description
- Current status (not started, in progress, completed, delayed)
- Assigned to (attorney, paralegal, client)
- Start date and due date
- Priority (critical, high, normal, low)
- Time estimate
- Notes

**Example:**

```
CASE MANAGEMENT TASK LIST
Smith v. Jones (Contract Dispute)
Updated: March 25, 2026

CRITICAL TASKS (Next 30 Days):
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
[ ] Task #1: File Answer to Complaint
    Status: IN PROGRESS
    Assigned to: Attorney [Name]
    Due Date: March 30, 2026 (5 days remaining)
    Priority: CRITICAL — Non-waivable deadline
    Notes: Complaint received 3/15. Answer due 20 days after service.
           Court: [Court name]

[ ] Task #2: Serve opposing party with interrogatories
    Status: NOT STARTED
    Assigned to: Paralegal [Name]
    Due Date: April 15, 2026
    Priority: HIGH — Discovery plan requires this
    Time Est: 3 hours drafting + service
    Notes: Coordinate service with Paralegal [Name]

ONGOING TASKS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
[ ] Weekly status calls with client
    Due: Every Friday by 5pm
    Assigned to: Attorney

[ ] Track expenses and unbilled time
    Due: Daily
    Assigned to: Attorney
```

### Step 3: Deadline Tracking Calendar

Maintain a master calendar (digital or paper) with all deadlines:

**Digital Options:**
- ~~Google Calendar~~ — Color code by client/type
- ~~Outlook Calendar~~ — Set reminders for upcoming deadlines
- Dedicated case management software (Clio, Rocket Matter, LawLab)
- Spreadsheet with deadline column

**Paper Option:**
- Large calendar on office wall with case info next to each date
- Color highlighters for different case types

**What to track:**
1. **Court deadlines** — Response dates, motions, trial dates
2. **Statute of limitations** — Expiration dates for claims
3. **Agreement deadlines** — Deadlines specified in contracts
4. **Self-imposed deadlines** — 5 days before actual deadline for buffer
5. **Client deadlines** — When client must provide information
6. **Payment deadlines** — When bills are due
7. **CLE deadlines** — Annual education requirements (separate from cases)

**Critical rule:** Once a deadline is entered in the calendar, it must be reviewed weekly and noted in a reminder system.

### Step 4: Reminder System

Create multiple reminder levels:

**Level 1: Far out reminder (60 days before)**
"Note to self: [Case name] has [deadline] approaching in 60 days. Plan preparation timeline."

**Level 2: Intermediate reminder (30 days before)**
"[Case name] has [deadline] in 30 days. Review required steps and assign tasks to team."

**Level 3: Imminent reminder (7 days before)**
"[Case name] has [deadline] in 7 days. Confirm all work is done and filing/response is ready."

**Level 4: Day-of reminder (morning of deadline)**
"[Case name] deadline TODAY: [deadline]. Verify submission/filing completed."

**Level 5: Buffer reminder (5 days before for critical deadlines)**
"[Case name] has critical deadline in 5 days. This is your buffer date; all work must be complete."

**Implementation:**
- Calendar alerts (automatic through ~~Google Calendar, Outlook~~, or case management software)
- Weekly review of upcoming deadlines (every Friday, 30 minutes)
- Monthly calendaring meeting (if team practice)
- Email to team when deadline approaches

### Step 6: Statute of Limitations Tracking

The statute of limitations (SOL) is the deadline by which a claim must be filed. Missing it is catastrophic and often results in malpractice liability.

**Critical SOL management:**

```
STATUTE OF LIMITATIONS MASTER LOG

Client | Matter | Type of Claim | SOL Deadline | Days Left | Status | Notes
-------|--------|---------------|--------------|-----------|--------|-------
Smith | PI/Auto | Negligence | 3/15/2027 | 355 days | Active | Incident 3/15/2024
Johnson | Divorce | Family Law | On hold | — | Pending | Requires written request
ABC Corp | Contract | Breach | 7/15/2028 | 841 days | Active | Keep retainer

FOR EACH CLAIM:
1. Calculate deadline precisely based on incident/breach date and state law
2. Enter deadline in calendar (not SOL date, but 30 days BEFORE SOL date)
3. Review SOL quarterly (spreadsheet updated)
4. When deadline approaches (60 days), create action plan:
   - File suit, or
   - Send tolling/settlement demand, or
   - Request client extension in writing
5. Document decision and any tolling agreements

WORST CASE: SOL runs while matter is pending and you forget to file suit. At that point, claim is barred. Malpractice liability is automatic.

BEST PRACTICE: Set calendar reminder 60 days before SOL. When reminder hits, you MUST either:
☐ File suit
☐ Obtain written tolling agreement
☐ Get written client instruction to delay
☐ Transfer case to specialist

No exceptions.
```

### Step 7: Court Rules and Procedural Deadlines

Different courts have different rules. Missing a procedural deadline can result in dismissal, sanctions, or waiver of claims.

**For each case in litigation:**
1. Identify the court (state trial court, federal court, appellate court)
2. Obtain the court's local rules (usually online)
3. Note any local rule quirks (e.g., some courts require notice 5 days before, not 3)
4. Create a procedural checklist:
   - What pleadings are required and when?
   - What discovery deadlines exist?
   - How much notice is required for motions?
   - What page limits apply?
   - Are there scheduling orders?

**Example checklist for civil litigation:**

```
PROCEDURAL CHECKLIST — Smith v. Jones (State Court)
Court: [County Circuit Court]
Judge: [Judge name]

LOCAL RULES COMPLIANCE:
☐ Complaint filed (within statute of limitations)
☐ Service of summons + complaint on defendant within 90 days ✓ 3/20/26
☐ Defendant answer due 20 days from service ← Deadline 4/9/26
☐ Case management conference: [To be scheduled]
☐ Scheduling order to be entered
☐ Discovery: Must comply with state discovery rules
☐ Motions: 5 days notice required (see local rule 4.2)
☐ Motion hearing: Scheduled for [Date]

DISCOVERY TIMELINE:
Initial Disclosures: Due [30 days from case start]
Interrogatories: Due [Responsive deadline]
Requests for Production: Due [Responsive deadline]
Requests for Admission: Due [Responsive deadline]
Expert Disclosures: Due [Date per scheduling order]

KEY DATES:
Scheduling Conference: [To be scheduled]
Discovery Cutoff: [TBD per order]
Expert Disclosure: [TBD per order]
Motion Deadline: [TBD per order]
Trial Date: [Pending scheduling order]

COMMON MISTAKES IN THIS COURT:
• Judges expect all discovery complete before motions
• Local rule 4.2 requires 5-day notice, not 3
• Interrogatory limit is 20 (not 25); need approval for more
• Experts must be identified 30 days before trial or waived
```

## Red Flags in Case Management

Watch for these situations requiring intervention:

1. **Accumulating deadlines** — If 3+ deadlines hit in same week, risk of missing one
2. **Unclear assignments** — If no one is clearly responsible for a task, it often gets missed
3. **Old cases stuck in intake** — If case hasn't moved in 30 days, investigate why
4. **Communication gaps** — If you haven't heard from client in 60 days, contact them
5. **Growing file without progress** — If file has grown but no milestones reached, reassess strategy
6. **Untracked expenses** — If expenses aren't logged daily, they won't be billed
7. **Paralegal overload** — If paralegal has too many tasks, quality suffers and deadlines slip
8. **Unclear fee arrangement** — If client doesn't understand billing, disputes arise
9. **Missing documentation** — If engagement letter isn't signed, enforceability questioned
10. **No escalation plan** — If case becomes outside your expertise, plan transfer before deadline

## Key Takeaways

- **Create case summaries** — One-page overview of critical dates and issues
- **Use task lists** — Organize work by case type and stage
- **Track deadlines obsessively** — Calendar, reminders, weekly review
- **Know statute of limitations** — Calculate and track for every claim
- **Understand court rules** — Local rules vary; know them before deadlines hit
- **Communicate regularly** — Document all client contact
- **Assign accountability** — Each task assigned to specific person with due date
- **Weekly review** — Every Friday, review upcoming deadlines and case progress
- **Buffer dates** — Set internal reminders 5-7 days before actual deadlines
- **Escalate early** — If deadline will be missed, notify client immediately
