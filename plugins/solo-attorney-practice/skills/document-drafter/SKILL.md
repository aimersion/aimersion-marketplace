# Legal Document Drafting

Professional legal writing is a skill that separates good attorneys from great ones. Clear, persuasive, properly formatted documents protect your clients, document your work product, and enhance your professional reputation. This skill provides frameworks for drafting the documents you'll use repeatedly in practice.

## Principles of Legal Document Drafting

### 1. Clarity Over Complexity

**Poor:** "Pursuant to the aforementioned bilateral agreement, the obligations thereunder require performance of the stipulated activities within the prescribed temporal parameters."

**Better:** "Under the contract, you must complete the work by March 30, 2026."

Clear writing is more professional, persuasive, and legally sound.

### 2. Active Voice Over Passive

**Passive (weaker):** "It is believed by counsel that the defendant's position is without merit."

**Active (stronger):** "We believe the defendant's position lacks merit."

Active voice is clearer, more persuasive, and more direct.

### 3. Plain English

**Avoid:** Legalese, Latin phrases, archaic language.

**Use:** Modern English that educated non-lawyers can understand.

**Example:**
- AVOID: "hereinafter referred to as"
- USE: "the client" or "ABC Corp"

### 4. Specificity

**Vague:** "The services will be provided in a timely manner."

**Specific:** "Legal services will be provided within 3 business days of request."

Specific language prevents disputes.

### 5. Consistency in Terminology

Pick a term for a concept and use it throughout. Don't use "the company," "the corporation," "ABC Inc.," and "employer" for the same entity.

**Example:** If you call the other party "the defendant," don't switch to "Mr. Smith" or "the respondent."

## Document Structure and Formatting

### Standard Legal Document Format

```
[FIRM LETTERHEAD]

[Date]

[Recipient address block — for letters/demands]

Re: [Matter Description] / [Case Name if litigation]

[Salutation — Dear [Title] [Name]:]

[Body — opening, content, closing]

[Closing salutation — Sincerely,]

[Signature block]

[Reference initials — JD/abc]

[cc: line if copying others]

[Enclosures if any documents attached]

Attachments: [List]
```

### Pagination and Numbering

**First page:**
- Letterhead at top
- Leave 1-2 inches of white space (margins)
- Start body text at least 1 inch from top

**Subsequent pages:**
- Headers with: [Client Name] — [Matter] — [Document Name] — Page [#]
- Page numbers at bottom (centered or right-aligned)
- Do NOT repeat letterhead on subsequent pages

**Margins:** 1 inch on all sides (standard)

**Font:** 12-point Times New Roman or similar (avoid decorative fonts)

**Line spacing:** Single-spaced within sections; double-spaced between sections for readability

### Headings and Subheadings

Use hierarchical headings to organize document:

```
I. MAJOR SECTION
   A. Subsection 1
      1. Sub-subsection
         a. Further subdivision

Example:

I. FACTS AND BACKGROUND
   A. Client's Business
   B. The Disputed Transaction
      1. Initial Agreement
      2. Performance and Breach
         a. Defendant's Obligation
         b. Defendant's Failure
```

## Common Documents: Templates and Guidance

### 1. Engagement Letter

**Purpose:** Create attorney-client relationship, document fee arrangement, explain services

**Key sections:**
- Identification of client and matter
- Scope of representation (what you will do, what you won't)
- Fee arrangement (hourly rate, flat fee, retainer, contingency)
- Cost and expense policy
- Termination clause (how representation can end)
- Confidentiality and privilege explanation
- Client responsibilities
- Signature by both attorney and client

**Critical language:**
- "This letter constitutes our entire agreement regarding fees and services"
- Specify any limits: "Our representation is limited to [scope]"
- "You are responsible for [costs/expenses/decisions]"
- "You may terminate representation by written notice; you remain responsible for fees earned"

### 2. Retainer Agreement

**Purpose:** Document advance payment for future services

**Key sections:**
- Amount of retainer ($X)
- How retainer will be applied (hourly work, costs, etc.)
- Refund policy (unused retainer refunded? charged?)
- Notification when retainer nearly used
- Client authorization to bill against retainer

**Example language:**
"You are retaining ABC Law Firm for [services] and paying a retainer of $[amount]. We will bill our hourly rate ($[rate]) against this retainer for services rendered. When the retainer balance reaches $500, we will notify you. You may replenish the retainer or authorize us to bill additional amounts. Unused retainer will be refunded within 30 days after engagement ends."

### 3. Representation Letter

**Purpose:** Confirm your representation to opposing counsel or third parties

**Key sections:**
- Client identification
- Matter identification
- Scope of representation
- Client contact information
- Instruction that all communications go through attorney

**Example language:**
"This letter confirms that [Attorney] represents [Client] in [matter description]. Our representation is limited to [scope]. Please direct all future communications regarding this matter to the undersigned. Do not communicate directly with our client regarding legal strategy or settlement."

### 4. Demand Letter

**Purpose:** Formally demand payment or performance before litigation

**Key sections:**
1. **Introduction:** "This letter is a formal demand for [payment/performance]"
2. **Facts:** Detailed factual narrative of what happened (be specific with dates, amounts, details)
3. **Contractual/Legal Basis:** What contract was breached or what law was violated
4. **Damages:** Calculation of losses (itemized)
5. **Demand:** Clear statement of total amount demanded
6. **Deadline:** "Payment due by [specific date]" (typically 30 days)
7. **Consequences:** "If payment not received, we will pursue litigation"
8. **Contact:** Instructions for payment and who to contact

**Critical:** Do not threaten illegal conduct (extortion); do not create unreasonable deadline. Threatening legitimate legal action is appropriate.

**Example structure:**

```
Dear [Opposing Counsel/Party]:

This letter is a formal demand for payment of $[amount] for [brief description of claim].

FACTS:
[Detailed narrative of facts. Include dates, names, amounts, and all relevant details. Be specific.]

LEGAL BASIS:
[Explain why defendant is legally responsible. Cite contract clause or legal theory.]

CALCULATION OF DAMAGES:
Direct damages:
- Medical expenses: $[amount]
- Lost wages: $[amount]
- Property damage: $[amount]
Subtotal: $[amount]

Non-economic damages (pain/suffering): $[amount]
TOTAL DAMAGES: $[amount]

DEMAND:
We demand payment of $[amount] by [specific date — typically 30 days from letter].

If payment is not received by that date, our client will pursue litigation, and you will be responsible for court costs and attorney fees authorized by law.

Please contact [attorney name] at [phone/email] to arrange payment.

Sincerely,
[Attorney name]
```

### 5. Settlement Demand

**Purpose:** Propose settlement in dispute (typically used in negotiations)

**Key sections:**
- Brief factual summary
- Legal position (why you'll prevail)
- Damages estimate
- Settlement amount requested
- Deadline for response
- Invitation to discuss

**Tone:** Less adversarial than demand letter; assume other side wants to settle

**Example:**
"We represent [Client] regarding [matter]. Based on the facts and law, we estimate damages at approximately $[amount]. In the interest of resolving this matter efficiently, we propose settlement in the amount of $[settlement offer]. Please respond by [date] if you wish to discuss further. We believe this offer represents fair value and avoids the cost and delay of litigation."

### 6. Termination of Representation Letter

**Purpose:** Formally end attorney-client relationship

**Key sections:**
- Clear statement that representation is terminated
- Effective date of termination
- Any final obligations (outstanding bills, document return)
- Notice that client must find new counsel (if active matter)
- Offer to transfer files to new attorney

**Example language:**
"This letter confirms that our representation of [Client] regarding [matter] is terminated, effective [date]. Our final bill for services through [date] is enclosed and due within 30 days. Please arrange for transfer of your file to new counsel within 5 business days. If you do not retain new counsel, be advised that any legal deadlines may pass and your claims may be barred. We remain available to discuss transition."

### 7. Referral Letter

**Purpose:** Refer client to specialist attorney in different practice area

**Key sections:**
- Clear statement of referral
- Reason for referral ("This matter requires expertise in [area]")
- Recommendation of referred attorney
- Offer to cooperate with new counsel
- Clear that you're withdrawing from this issue (don't leave doubt)

**Example:**
"We represent you in [matter]. Your case also involves [specialty area], which requires expertise beyond our practice focus. We recommend you consult with [Referred Attorney], who specializes in [area]. We're happy to provide her with background on your matter and will cooperate fully. Please contact us with any questions about the transition."

## Motion and Pleading Shells

### Motion to Dismiss

**Components:**
1. Caption (court, case number, title)
2. Introduction: "Defendant moves to dismiss the complaint for failure to state a claim"
3. Standard of review: "A motion to dismiss tests whether the complaint states a claim"
4. Legal analysis: Explain why complaint fails as matter of law
5. Conclusion: "The complaint should be dismissed"

**Key legal standard:** For motion to dismiss, assume all factual allegations are true; argue that even if true, no legal claim exists.

### Motion for Summary Judgment

**Components:**
1. Caption and title
2. Introduction: "Defendant moves for summary judgment on ground there is no genuine dispute of material fact"
3. Facts (undisputed): Narrative of facts neither party disputes (cite evidence)
4. Legal standard: "Summary judgment appropriate where no reasonable jury could find for plaintiff"
5. Analysis: Apply law to undisputed facts; argue plaintiff cannot prevail
6. Conclusion: "Defendant is entitled to judgment as matter of law"

**Key strategic point:** Build strongest possible factual record through declarations, exhibits; show plaintiff has no contradictory evidence.

### Motion to Compel Discovery

**Components:**
1. Caption
2. Introduction: "Plaintiff moves to compel defendant to respond to interrogatories"
3. Background: What discovery was requested, when, and what response (or lack thereof)
4. Legal standard: "Court may compel discovery upon motion when party fails to respond"
5. Analysis: Explain why discovery is relevant, proportionate, and why response inadequate
6. Conclusion: "Defendant should be ordered to provide complete, compliant responses"

**Key requirement:** Most jurisdictions require "meet and confer" (try to resolve before filing motion).

### Motion for Continuance

**Components:**
1. Caption
2. Introduction: "Plaintiff moves to continue the trial date scheduled for [date]"
3. Reason: Specific factual reason (cannot be ready, key witness unavailable, settlement negotiations ongoing)
4. Legal standard: "Court may grant continuance for good cause shown"
5. Analysis: Explain why continuance necessary and prejudice if denied
6. Proposed new date
7. Conclusion: "Trial should be continued to [date]"

**Key requirement:** Many judges disfavor continuances; have strong reason.

## Writing Standards for Each Document Type

### Demand Letters: Persuasive Tone

**Goal:** Convince recipient to pay without litigation

**Techniques:**
- Factual narrative is detailed and compelling
- Legal position presented with confidence
- Damages calculation is specific and supported
- Tone is businesslike but firm (not threatening)
- Deadline is reasonable (30 days typical)

### Engagement Letters: Clear and Protective

**Goal:** Create binding agreement, protect attorney, set expectations

**Techniques:**
- All terms are explicit (no hidden fees or surprises)
- Scope is clear: what attorney will and won't do
- Client responsibilities stated
- Signature by both parties demonstrates understanding
- Each party retains a copy

### Litigation Motions: Legally Sound and Efficient

**Goal:** Persuade judge to rule in your favor

**Techniques:**
- Legal standard stated clearly
- Facts organized logically
- Law applied to facts methodically
- Citations to authority (case law, statutes)
- Argument concise and focused
- Conclusion restates request

## Common Drafting Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

### Mistake 1: Vague Language

**Problem:** "Services will be provided in a timely manner"

**Fix:** "Services will be provided within 3 business days of your request"

**Why it matters:** Vague language leads to disputes and client dissatisfaction

### Mistake 2: Inconsistent Terminology

**Problem:** "ABC Corp," "the company," "ABC, Inc.," and "the client" used interchangeably

**Fix:** Choose one name; use it consistently throughout

**Why it matters:** Inconsistency creates confusion and looks unprofessional

### Mistake 3: Passive Voice

**Problem:** "It is our opinion that the defendant's position lacks merit"

**Fix:** "We believe the defendant's position lacks merit"

**Why it matters:** Active voice is clearer, more direct, more persuasive

### Mistake 4: Outdated Language

**Problem:** "Hereinafter referred to as," "shall," "heretofore"

**Fix:** "from now on," "will," "previously"

**Why it matters:** Modern language is more readable and professional

### Mistake 5: Over-Lawyering

**Problem:** "Notwithstanding the foregoing provisions and contingent upon the subsequent resolution of the aforementioned matter..."

**Fix:** "After the contract is signed and paid for, you will..."

**Why it matters:** Clients should understand their documents; over-lawyering is unnecessary

### Mistake 6: Inconsistent Formatting

**Problem:** Some sections single-spaced, some double; different fonts throughout; inconsistent heading styles

**Fix:** Consistent formatting throughout: same font, same spacing, same heading styles

**Why it matters:** Professional appearance reflects on your competence

### Mistake 7: Missing Critical Information

**Problem:** Demand letter without specific deadline for payment

**Fix:** "Payment due by [specific date — e.g., August 15, 2026]"

**Why it matters:** Lack of specificity weakens your position and creates disputes

## Document Assembly and Version Control

### Creating Templates

1. **Draft a strong version** — Spend time on first version; it becomes template
2. **Test with real client** — Use with client; refine based on their questions
3. **Save as template** — Create a "template" version (with blanks for client-specific info)
4. **Document the blanks** — Mark blanks clearly: [CLIENT NAME], [DATE], [AMOUNT]
5. **Create instructions** — Note which sections are optional, when to modify, etc.

### Version Control

**Problem:** Using outdated template because you forgot you updated it last year

**Solution:**
- Date all templates: "Engagement Letter Template — Updated March 2026"
- Note any revisions: "Revised to add [new provision] per 2026 change in law"
- Store in organized folder structure:
  ```
  /Templates
    /Engagement Letters
      /Engagement-Hourly-v2026.docx
      /Engagement-Flat-Fee-v2026.docx
    /Letters
      /Demand-Letter-v2026.docx
      /Termination-v2026.docx
    /Motions
      /Motion-to-Dismiss-v2026.docx
  ```

## Quality Control Checklist

Before sending any document, complete this checklist:

**Content:**
- [ ] All facts accurate and verified
- [ ] All legal citations correct and current
- [ ] Law is correctly stated
- [ ] Conclusion logically follows from facts/law
- [ ] No legal claims overstated or understated

**Formatting:**
- [ ] Consistent font and size throughout
- [ ] Headings organized hierarchically
- [ ] Margins correct (1 inch all sides)
- [ ] Page numbering correct
- [ ] Letterhead professional and current

**Language:**
- [ ] No spelling or grammar errors
- [ ] Terminology consistent throughout
- [ ] No passive voice where active would be better
- [ ] Plain English used (no unnecessary legalese)
- [ ] Tone appropriate for document type

**Professionalism:**
- [ ] Signature block complete (attorney name, bar number, contact info)
- [ ] Recipient name/address correct
- [ ] Dates correct (letter date, deadline, effective date)
- [ ] No confidential information disclosed inadvertently
- [ ] Document professional in appearance

**Prior to sending:**
- [ ] Read aloud (catch errors ear misses)
- [ ] Have colleague read (fresh eyes catch mistakes)
- [ ] Verify all client information is correct
- [ ] Confirm deadline is legally compliant (is 30 days enough time?)

## Key Takeaways

- **Clarity over complexity** — Write for educated non-lawyers
- **Active voice** — Direct and persuasive
- **Consistency** — Use same terminology throughout
- **Specificity** — "by March 30" not "promptly"
- **Professional appearance** — Formatting matters
- **Templates are your friend** — Draft once, use many times
- **Proofread carefully** — Typos undermine credibility
- **Appropriate tone** — Match document type and recipient
