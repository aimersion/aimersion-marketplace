# Billing and Time Tracking System

The difference between a thriving solo practice and a struggling one often comes down to billing discipline. Many attorneys undercharge, fail to track time, or mismanage trust accounts — costing thousands in lost revenue annually. This skill establishes systems for consistent, ethical, and profitable billing.

## Why Billing Matters

Sound billing practices:
- **Generate revenue** — Tracked time equals paid work; untracked time is lost revenue
- **Ensure fairness** — Clients pay for work actually done, not estimates
- **Protect relationships** — Clear billing prevents client disputes and surprises
- **Create leverage** — Invoices create accountability and encourage client decision-making
- **Enable planning** — Billing data shows which practices areas are profitable
- **Demonstrate value** — Detailed invoices show clients exactly what they're paying for
- **Ensure compliance** — Proper billing and trust accounting satisfies ethics rules

## The Time Tracking Foundation

### Daily Time Tracking

**The Rule:** Record time on the same day you work.

Waiting until week's end to reconstruct your time leads to:
- Forgotten work
- Underestimation of hours
- Client disputes ("I don't remember authorizing that")
- Inaccurate billing codes
- Ethical violations (backdating time entries)

**The Method:** Use a time tracking system (paper, spreadsheet, or software) that captures:
1. **Date** — Specific date work performed
2. **Client/Matter** — Client name and specific matter (e.g., "Smith v. Jones — Motion to Dismiss")
3. **Time spent** — Actual hours and minutes (decimal format: 0.5 hours = 30 minutes)
4. **Description** — What you did (not just "legal work")
5. **Billing code** — Standard code for the type of work (if using codes)

**Example time entry:**
```
Date: 3/15/2026
Client: ABC Corp
Matter: Contract Review
Time: 1.75 hours
Description: Review proposed service agreement, identified vendor lock-in provision, prepared memo
Billing Code: 2.1 (Legal Analysis)
Billable: Yes
```

### Minimum Billing Increments

Most attorneys bill in increments (minimum billable unit):
- **0.1 hours (6 minutes)** — Aggressive minimum, appropriate if billing 2000+ hours/year
- **0.25 hours (15 minutes)** — Common for litigation and complex work
- **0.5 hours (30 minutes)** — Standard for most solo practitioners
- **1.0 hour (60 minutes)** — Common for fixed-fee work or less detailed tracking

**How to apply:** If you work 12 minutes on a task, round to the nearest increment:
- With 0.25 increment, 12 minutes rounds to 0.25 hours
- With 0.5 increment, 12 minutes rounds to 0.5 hours

**Best practice for solos:** Use 0.25 hour increments for most work, 0.5 hour for consultations where time is harder to track precisely.

**Never:** Round down (billing 0.25 for 1 hour of work). Always round up to the nearest increment or bill actual time.

### Billing Codes and Categories

Establish standard codes for different work types. This allows you to analyze profitability by task:

**Standard Legal Billing Codes:**
- 1.0 Client consultation
- 1.1 Phone consultation
- 1.2 In-person meeting
- 1.3 Email/correspondence with client
- 2.0 Legal research and analysis
- 2.1 Contract review
- 2.2 Case law research
- 2.3 Statute/regulation research
- 3.0 Drafting
- 3.1 Letters
- 3.2 Motions and pleadings
- 3.3 Contracts and agreements
- 3.4 Wills and trusts
- 4.0 Court appearances
- 4.1 Depositions
- 4.2 Oral arguments
- 4.3 Trial testimony
- 5.0 Administrative work
- 5.1 Billing and file management
- 5.2 Court filings and administrative tasks
- 6.0 Travel time (typically at 50% rate)

Use these codes consistently. At the end of the year, your billing codes tell you:
- Which practice areas are most profitable
- Which tasks consume the most time
- Where to raise rates or reduce time investment

### What to Track and What Not to Track

**ALWAYS track:**
- Any time spent on client matters
- Phone calls with clients
- Legal research
- Document drafting
- Court appearances
- Client consultations
- Meetings with opposing counsel
- Work on discovery, motions, negotiation

**DO NOT track (typically non-billable):**
- Office management (unless charging flat fee)
- Marketing and business development
- CLE and professional development
- Personal phone calls
- Lunch and breaks
- Time spent looking for work
- Time correcting your own errors

**SOMETIMES track:**
- Waiting time (depends on context; waiting for court to call your case = billable; waiting for client to call back = sometimes not billable)
- Travel time (typically bill at 50% rate)
- Paralegal/staff work (bill at staff rate if different from your rate)

### Dealing with Underestimation

If you work 2 hours on a task but estimated 1 hour:
- **Option 1 (Most ethical):** Bill actual time (2 hours) and explain the overage to client
- **Option 2 (Client-friendly):** Bill estimated time (1 hour) and absorb the difference
- **Option 3 (Negotiated):** Bill 1.5 hours as compromise

**Best practice:** Always discuss work exceeding estimates with client. Surprising clients with large bills creates disputes.

**Note:** Some attorneys maintain "unbilled time" — work they did but didn't charge for. This is fine for genuine errors or client relations, but don't make it a habit.

## Invoice Generation

### Invoice Structure

A professional invoice includes these elements:

```
[FIRM LETTERHEAD]

INVOICE

Invoice #: [Number]
Invoice Date: [Date]
Matter: [Client Name and Case/Matter Description]
Bill To: [Client Name and Address]

Billing Period: [Start Date] to [End Date]
Due Date: [Date — typically 30 days from invoice date]

DESCRIPTION OF SERVICES

Date        Description                                Hours    Rate        Amount
---         ---------                                  -----    ----        ------
3/1/2026    Initial client consultation               1.0      $250/hr     $250.00
3/3/2026    Phone call re: document review            0.5      $250/hr     $125.00
3/5/2026    Legal research on contract enforceability 2.0      $250/hr     $500.00
3/7/2026    Draft representation letter               1.5      $250/hr     $375.00
3/10/2026   Email correspondence with client          0.25     $250/hr     $62.50

                                           SUBTOTAL:              $1,312.50

REDUCTIONS:
Less: Retainer paid 3/1/2026                                    ($1,000.00)
Less: Previous payment 3/5/2026                                 ($200.00)

                                           BALANCE DUE:           $112.50

PAYMENT INSTRUCTIONS:
Make checks payable to: [Firm Name]
Mail to: [Office Address]
ACH/Wire: [Bank information if applicable]

Thank you for your business!
[Attorney Name and Contact Info]
```

### Invoice Details

**Each line item should include:**
- Date of work performed
- Description of work (specific enough that client understands what they're paying for)
- Hours spent
- Hourly rate (or note if flat fee)
- Total amount for that line item

**"Legal work" is not a sufficient description.** Instead of:
- "Legal services" — write "Research on contract interpretation; reviewed case law on vendor lock-in provisions"
- "Attorney time" — write "Telephone call with client regarding settlement discussions; discussed terms and tax implications"
- "Court appearance" — write "Attended motion hearing in [Court], argued motion to compel discovery; hearing lasted 30 minutes"

Detailed descriptions show clients value for their money and reduce billing disputes.

### Billing Variations

**Hourly Billing:**
- Standard model: charge per hour worked
- Client benefits: pays only for actual work
- Attorney benefits: simple to track, easy to increase rates
- Risk: client disputes if work takes longer than expected

**Flat-Fee Billing:**
- Model: fixed price for defined scope of work
- Examples: estate plan ($2,000), uncontested divorce ($5,000), business formation ($1,500)
- Client benefits: knows total cost upfront
- Attorney benefits: if work is efficient, profit improves; if work runs long, you absorb cost
- Risk: underestimating time required; requires experience to price accurately

**Retainer Plus Hourly:**
- Model: client pays retainer upfront, then billed hourly against the retainer
- Example: $5,000 retainer for litigation; client billed hourly, retainer deducted from invoices
- Client benefits: ensures attorney commitment; cost certainty for initial phase
- Attorney benefits: upfront payment improves cash flow
- Note: Most ethics rules require that unearned retainers be held in trust account

**Contingency:**
- Model: attorney recovers percentage of settlement/judgment (typically 25-40%)
- Uses: personal injury, employment discrimination, class actions
- Client benefits: no upfront cost; attorney shares risk
- Attorney benefits: higher recovery on successful cases; cash flow may be delayed
- Risk: may not recover costs if case unsuccessful

**Blended Billing:**
- Model: different rates for different activities (partners higher than associates, etc.)
- Example: Partner research at $300/hr, Associate research at $150/hr
- Advantage: matches rate to expertise level
- Common in larger firms; less common in solo practice

### Tracking Expenses and Disbursements

Beyond time, track expenses you incur on behalf of clients:

**Direct Expenses:**
- Court filing fees
- Service of process costs
- Expert witness fees
- Deposition transcripts
- Copy costs (if charged separately)
- Travel expenses (if client reimburses)
- Postage and shipping

**Handled properly:**
1. Pay the expense yourself
2. Document the expense with invoice/receipt
3. List on the invoice as "Costs and Expenses" or "Reimbursable Costs"
4. Deduct from retainer or add to invoice total
5. Reimbursement is typically separate from attorney's fee

**Example:**
```
SERVICES (as shown above):                  $1,312.50

COSTS AND EXPENSES:
Court filing fee (3/8/2026)                $    45.00
Service of process (3/10/2026)             $   150.00
Expert report (3/15/2026)                  $   750.00
                                           __________
Total Costs and Expenses:                  $   945.00

SUBTOTAL (Services + Costs):               $2,257.50
Less: Retainer Applied                     ($1,000.00)
BALANCE DUE:                               $1,257.50
```

**Trust account compliance:** Some expenses must come from a trust account (client funds held in trust). See trust accounting section below.

### Creating Professional Invoices

**Options for generating invoices:**

1. **Billing Software** (highly recommended for practices with multiple clients):
   - LawPay, Clio, Rocket Matter, Bill4Time
   - Features: time tracking, invoicing, payment processing, client portal
   - Cost: $50-300/month depending on features
   - Benefit: saves time, creates professional invoices, tracks payments

2. **Spreadsheet Template:**
   - Excel or Google Sheets with invoice template
   - Format: standard invoice with time entries
   - Benefit: free, customizable, no software learning curve
   - Drawback: manual, error-prone for large practices

3. **Word Template:**
   - Microsoft Word invoice template
   - Benefit: easy to customize letterhead
   - Drawback: must manually calculate, easy to make errors

4. **Online Invoicing Tools:**
   - FreshBooks, Wave, Zoho, Square
   - Benefit: designed for professional invoicing, payment processing
   - Cost: free or $10-50/month

**Best practice for solos:** Start with a spreadsheet template. As practice grows, move to dedicated billing software.

## Payment Follow-Up Workflow

### Invoice Collection Strategy

An invoice sent is not an invoice paid. Implement a follow-up system:

**Day 1-5:** Send invoice
- Email or mail invoice to client
- Include clear payment instructions
- Note due date (30 days is standard)
- Include payment methods accepted

**Day 30:** Invoice due date
- Monitor whether payment received

**Day 31-35:** First reminder
- Friendly email: "We haven't received payment on invoice #123 due 3/30. Could you confirm receipt and process by [new date]?"
- Assume it's administrative delay, not refusal to pay

**Day 36-45:** Second reminder
- More formal: "Payment on invoice #123 is now [15 days] overdue. Please remit payment immediately to [address]. If you have questions about the invoice, call [number]."
- Address any client complaints or disputes

**Day 46-60:** Third contact
- Phone call to discuss status
- "I want to make sure there's no problem with the invoice. Can you confirm payment is coming?"
- Listen for complaints (billing excessive, work not done, etc.)

**Day 61+:** Collections action
- Send formal demand letter
- Offer payment plan if client has cash flow issues
- Consider referral to collections if large balance
- Some ethics rules allow attorney liens on disputed funds

### Handling Payment Disputes

If client disputes invoice:

1. **Listen to the concern**
   - "I don't think the hours are accurate"
   - "I didn't authorize that work"
   - "Other counsel does this cheaper"

2. **Review the invoice**
   - Verify time entries are accurate
   - Confirm work was authorized
   - Check if work was reasonable for the matter

3. **Discuss with client**
   - Explain what work was done and why it was necessary
   - If billing was excessive, consider adjustment
   - If client dispute is frivolous, hold firm

4. **Offer adjustments if warranted**
   - "I can reduce this by $500 for the research work since the case settled earlier than expected"
   - "Let's reduce the estimate to $X and I'll absorb the overage"
   - Maintain professionalism; client satisfaction matters long-term

5. **Escalate if needed**
   - Consider mediation/arbitration per ethics rules
   - Some bar associations have fee dispute resolution programs
   - Law of contracts applies (client can dispute if work not done)

### Managing Client Funds (Trust Accounting)

**Critical ethics requirement:** Client funds must be kept separate from your operating account.

**Trust account requirements vary by state, but generally:**

1. **Separate account:** Maintain one or more trust accounts with client funds only
2. **Identification:** Account must be clearly labeled as trust/escrow account
3. **Record-keeping:** Maintain detailed records showing:
   - Client name
   - Date received
   - Amount received
   - Matter description
   - How funds are used (applied to fees, expenses, returned)
4. **Deposits:** Deposit client funds within short timeframe (rules vary; often 1-5 business days)
5. **No mixing:** Do NOT deposit your own money or firm operating funds in trust account
6. **Interest:** Some jurisdictions require interest-bearing accounts; interest paid to bar charitable funds
7. **Withdrawals:** Only withdraw funds that are "earned" (earned retainers) or properly documented expenses
8. **Reconciliation:** Monthly or quarterly reconciliation of trust account balance

**Common trust account problems that trigger ethics violations:**

- **Unearned retainers:** Client paid $5,000 retainer; you spent only $1,000; cannot withdraw remaining $4,000 until work done
- **Commingling:** Depositing trust funds with operating account
- **Excessive withdrawals:** Withdrawing funds before work is completed
- **Improper tracking:** No documentation of trust account activity

**Best practice for solos:**
1. Open trust account at bank (often requires trust account verification from bar)
2. Keep detailed log: Date | Client | Matter | Received | Spent | Balance
3. Monthly reconciliation: Trust account balance should equal sum of all client funds held
4. Never use trust funds for business expenses
5. If disciplined, most common complaint is improper trust accounting

### Cash Flow Management

Poor cash flow is the #1 reason solo practitioners fail. Improve cash flow through billing:

**Strategies:**

1. **Bill frequently** — Monthly invoicing is standard. Some practices bill biweekly.
2. **Require retainers** — Upfront payment improves cash flow; $2,000-$5,000 typical for litigation
3. **Deposit quickly** — Don't let checks sit on desk; deposit within 1-2 days
4. **Offer payment plans** — Client strapped for cash? Offer $500/month for 6 months instead of one $3,000 invoice
5. **Set payment terms** — Specify NET 30 (due 30 days after invoice), not "when convenient"
6. **Track receivables** — Know how much clients owe you
7. **Follow up aggressively** — First follow-up should be day 30; most collections are collected within 60 days or not at all
8. **Stop work if overdue** — If client is 60+ days overdue, stop work until payment received (with notice to client)

## Key Takeaways

- **Track time daily** — waiting until week-end leads to lost time and disputes
- **Bill detailed descriptions** — helps clients understand value and reduces disputes
- **Offer multiple billing models** — hourly works for some clients, flat fee for others
- **Follow up aggressively** — 70% of late-paid invoices collect within 60 days
- **Maintain trust accounts properly** — this is non-negotiable; ethics violations carry serious consequences
- **Monitor profitability** — track which practice areas and tasks are profitable; double rates on profitable work
- **Use billing software** — as practice grows, software saves time and reduces errors

Your billing practices directly impact your income. Invest time in setting up systems now; the return will be substantial.
