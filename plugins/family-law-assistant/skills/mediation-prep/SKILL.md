# Mediation Preparation Skill

Mediation is the most common path to resolution in family law cases. Approximately 90% of family law matters settle, and many settle in mediation. Attorneys who prepare effectively for mediation often achieve better outcomes, lower client costs, and faster resolution.

Mediating effectively requires understanding the mediation process, analyzing each case issue, developing settlement ranges based on legal standards and comparable cases, and preparing clients psychologically and strategically for negotiation.

## Understanding the Mediation Process

Mediation is a facilitated negotiation where a neutral third party helps disputants reach resolution. The mediator does not make decisions—the parties do. The mediator's role is to facilitate communication, help parties understand each other's positions, suggest creative solutions, and move parties toward agreement.

**Stages of Mediation**:

**Opening**: Mediator explains the process, confidentiality rules, and his/her role. Attorneys present overview of case. Parties present their perspective.

**Private Caucuses**: Mediator meets privately with each side. This is where real negotiations happen. Parties can speak candidly without the other side hearing.

**Shuttle Negotiation**: Mediator carries offers and counteroffers between the sides, gradually narrowing positions.

**Resolution**: When parties reach agreement, mediator summarizes terms and may help memorialize in writing.

**Impasse**: If parties cannot reach agreement, mediation ends. Case may proceed to trial or parties may negotiate further outside mediation.

## Analyzing Case Issues

Before mediation, conduct thorough analysis of each contested issue.

### Issue Identification

List every matter requiring resolution:

**Custody/Parenting**: Which parent has primary custody? How is decision-making allocated? What is visitation schedule? How are holidays handled? What about relocation?

**Property Division**: Which assets are martial vs. separate? How should martial property be divided? Are any assets contested? Are business interests involved? Any hidden assets?

**Spousal Support**: Is spousal support appropriate? For how long? At what amount? Are there factors supporting increased or decreased support?

**Child Support**: Is child support appropriate? What is guideline amount? Are there deviation factors? Any shared custody adjustments?

**Attorney Fees**: Should one party pay attorney fees? Under what standard? Is this likely to be resolved or will require judicial determination?

**Debts**: Which debts are martial? How should marital debt be allocated?

**Other Issues**: Anything else requiring resolution (college funding, insurance, relocation provisions, etc.)?

### Issue Prioritization

Not all issues are equal. Some are more important to clients, more contentious, or more legally complex.

**Primary Issues** (essential for client):
- Custody of children (if contested)
- Primary residence (if contested)
- Major assets (family home, business, retirement accounts)
- Child support or spousal support amounts

**Secondary Issues** (important but negotiable):
- Holiday schedules
- Specific asset division
- Attorney fees
- Insurance provisions

**Tertiary Issues** (less important):
- Specific transition dates
- Communication protocols
- Minor technical matters

Understanding priority helps in negotiations. Your most important issues are trading chips for concessions on less important matters.

### Issue Analysis Framework

For each significant issue, analyze:

**Legal Standard**: What does law require? Is this discretionary or mandatory? Are there guidelines?

**Comparable Outcomes**: What do similar cases yield? What's typical? What's unusual?

**Case Strengths**: What facts support your client's position?

**Case Weaknesses**: What facts undermine your client's position?

**Uncertainty**: How predictable is the judicial outcome? Are there gray areas?

**Risk**: What's the worst-case scenario? What's best case?

**Cost of Litigation**: How much will it cost in attorney fees to litigate this issue? How much time?

## Settlement Range Development

For each issue, develop a realistic settlement range. This range becomes your negotiation framework.

### Creating the Framework

**Ideal Outcome**: What's the best realistic result your client could get? This should be aggressive but not fantasy. It's the opening position.

**Acceptable Outcome**: What result would your client accept? This is the "walkaway point"—if you get less than this, litigation is better.

**Expected Outcome**: What do you realistically expect a judge would award? This is often somewhere in middle.

**Other Party's Likely Range**: What does the other party probably want? What would they accept?

### Example: Spousal Support

**Facts**: 20-year marriage, one parent stayed home with kids, earner has $150,000 annual income, non-earner has no current income

**Legal Standard**: State law provides guidelines based on marriage length and income differential

**Comparable Cases**: Similar 20-year marriages typically result in 8-10 years of support at 25-35% of income differential

**Your Client's Position**: Non-earner needs support to retrain and rebuild. 10 years at 35% ($52,500 annually) = $525,000 total

**Likely Judicial Award**: Judge probably awards 8-9 years at 30% ($45,000 annually) = $360,000-$405,000

**Acceptable Outcome**: Non-earner needs enough to survive and retrain. Minimum 7 years at 25% ($37,500 annually) = $262,500

**Settlement Range**: $262,500 to $525,000 with expected outcome around $380,000

Your negotiation will move from $525,000 down toward $380,000 (if you're seeking support) or from $150,000 up toward $380,000 (if you're resisting support).

### Developing Ranges for Multiple Issues

When multiple issues are in dispute, develop ranges for each:

**Issue A**: Range $X to $Y, Expected $Z
**Issue B**: Range $X to $Y, Expected $Z
**Issue C**: Range $X to $Y, Expected $Z

Then consider package deals: Can you trade off on Issue A if other party concedes on Issue B?

## Negotiation Strategy Development

### Opening Positions

Your opening position sets the negotiation framework. It anchors the discussion.

**Anchoring Principle**: Psychologically, the first number offered influences where settlement ultimately lands. A stronger opening anchor pulls settlement in your direction.

**Opening Position Recommendations**:
- Start with your "ideal outcome" rather than your "acceptable outcome"
- Make the opening position credible (not fantasy)
- Be prepared to explain and defend your opening position
- Understand that other party will have equally strong opening position

**In Private Caucus**:
When mediator asks what you want, state your opening position clearly and confidently. But also tell the mediator (in private):
- What you actually expect
- What you'd accept
- Your rationale for the opening position
- This helps mediator understand what's realistic movement

### Negotiation Sequencing

The order in which you address issues matters.

**Start with Easy Issues**: Build momentum by resolving issues where parties are close. This creates positive energy and demonstrates good faith.

**Address Hard Issues When Momentum Exists**: After resolving easier issues, tackle the most difficult ones. Parties are in better mindset.

**Leave Most Difficult for Last**: Sometimes the most difficult issue is easier to resolve after others are settled because parties are invested in completing deal.

**Create Trade-offs**: "If you move on Issue A, we'll move on Issue B." This creates mutual concession.

### Interest-Based Negotiation

Move beyond positions to underlying interests.

**Position vs. Interest**:
- **Position**: "I want 60% of the family home equity"
- **Interest**: "I need housing stability and funds to purchase a home"

Underlying interests are often less rigid than stated positions. If you can meet underlying interests in different way, you create settlement opportunities.

**Identifying Interests**:
- Why does client want what they want?
- What underlying need or concern drives the position?
- Are there multiple ways to meet that interest?
- What does the other party's interests likely are?

**Example**:
Client's position is "I need the house." Underlying interests might be:
- Housing stability for children
- Avoiding need to move children from school
- Equity to purchase new home
- Familiar home while adjusting to divorce

Other party's interests in keeping house might be:
- Same housing stability for their time with children
- Not having to move as well
- Equity they see as rightfully theirs

Could there be creative solutions? Buy-out of other party's interest? Delayed sale? Rent to other party? Refinance and pay out equity? These creative solutions address interests rather than positions.

### Package Deals and Contingent Agreements

Rather than resolving each issue in isolation, consider packages combining multiple issues.

**Package Example**:
- "You keep the house, we get the business"
- "You get primary custody, we get increased support to manage expenses"
- "You keep investment accounts, we keep retirement accounts"

**Contingent Agreements**:
- "If IRA is worth more than $200,000, X gets $100,000 from it"
- "If house sells for more than $500,000, we split proceeds differently"
- "If your income increases by more than 10%, support increases by half the increase"

These structures create value and address legitimate concerns about unknowns.

### Managing High-Conflict Dynamics

Some cases involve high-conflict dynamics that complicate negotiation.

**High-Conflict Signs**:
- Intense anger and blame
- Desire to punish other party
- Difficulty listening to other party's perspective
- Rigid positions with no flexibility
- Emotional dysregulation
- Ongoing litigation over minor matters
- Poor communication between parties

**Strategies for High-Conflict Cases**:
- Use private caucuses extensively (shuttle negotiation)
- Keep parties separated during mediation
- Focus on future and forward movement (not past blame)
- Frame resolution as "protecting self/children" not "winning"
- Use objective standards (guidelines, comparable cases) to depersonalize decisions
- Consider parallel parenting or rigid custody schedules if coparenting is contentious
- Involve mental health professional if substance abuse, domestic violence, or mental health issues
- Use written communication rather than direct communication

### Dealing with Impasse

Sometimes negotiations reach deadlock. Mediator's role is to help parties move past impasse.

**Common Impasse Situations**:
- Parties are far apart on major issue
- Party is emotionally activated and not thinking clearly
- Party feels disrespected or unheard
- Issue is tied to principle or ego
- Both parties have dug in on positions

**Strategies for Breaking Impasse**:
- Take break to allow emotions to settle
- Refocus on interests rather than positions
- Introduce objective standard or expert opinion
- Mediator may offer suggestion or proposal
- Reframe issue from different perspective
- Link issue to other issues for package deal
- Discuss costs of continued litigation
- Sometimes impasse is real and mediation ends

## Preparing Clients for Mediation

### Pre-Mediation Client Meeting

Before mediation, meet with client to prepare.

**Explain the Process**:
- Mediator is neutral, not favoring either side
- Mediator is not a judge and makes no decisions
- Confidentiality: what's said in mediation stays confidential and can't be used in court
- Private caucuses: you'll meet with mediator privately
- Goal is agreement, not winning

**Explain Your Strategy**:
- What your objectives are
- What you hope to achieve on each issue
- What you're willing to give on
- Where you might compromise
- What your "walkaway point" is

**Set Realistic Expectations**:
- Settlement will require concessions on both sides
- You probably won't get everything you want
- Other party won't either
- Middle ground is typical
- Some issues may not be resolved in mediation

**Prepare Emotionally**:
- You will see the other party; prepare yourself emotionally
- Other party may say harsh things; don't react
- Hearing other party's perspective may be difficult
- You need to stay focused on agreement, not blame
- Emotions are normal; manage them professionally

**Discuss Communication**:
- Listen without interrupting
- Speak respectfully even if angry
- Use "I" statements not accusations
- Don't bring up past grievances unless relevant
- Focus on future not past
- Tell mediator if you need a break

**Discuss Non-Negotiables**:
- What matters most to client?
- What is absolutely essential?
- What is client willing to give up?
- Be realistic about what's negotiable

### Client Communication Guidelines

Teach clients how to communicate effectively in mediation.

**Effective Communication Principles**:
- **Listen actively**: Understand other party's concerns even if you disagree
- **Speak respectfully**: Disagree without being disagreeable
- **Focus on interests**: Explain why you need what you need
- **Use facts**: Base positions on objective information
- **Avoid blame**: Don't rehash past grievances
- **Stay future-focused**: Talk about what you need going forward
- **Take responsibility**: Acknowledge your own contributions to disputes
- **Ask questions**: Understand other party's perspective
- **Propose solutions**: Offer creative suggestions, not just demands

**What to Avoid**:
- Bringing up past infidelities, failures, or grievances
- Name-calling or character attacks
- Threatening language
- Walking out or showing contempt
- Saying you don't care about the other party's perspective
- Demanding unreasonable concessions
- Making unilateral ultimatums
- Raising new issues in mediation
- Lying or being deceptive

### Managing Client Emotions During Mediation

Some clients struggle with emotions during mediation.

**Anxiety**: Client is worried about the outcome
- Remind them of your strategy
- Use private caucuses to regroup
- Take breaks to manage anxiety
- Focus on progress made

**Anger**: Client becomes angry at other party
- Acknowledge their anger
- Remind them of their goals
- Use break if necessary
- Refocus on what serves their interests

**Grief**: Client becomes tearful
- Allow the emotion
- Provide tissues and water
- Take a break if needed
- Acknowledge loss while staying focused on path forward

**Distrust**: Client doesn't believe other party's statements
- Focus on verification (documents, experts)
- Don't require trust in other party's word
- Use objective standards
- Let mediator help verify information

## Post-Mediation Issues

### Evaluating Tentative Agreement

When parties reach tentative agreement, take time to evaluate before finalizing.

**Review Agreement for**:
- Completeness: Are all issues resolved?
- Clarity: Is language clear and unambiguous?
- Enforceability: Can terms be enforced if breached?
- Fairness: Does this still reflect client's interests?
- Tax implications: Are there tax consequences?
- Long-term implications: How will this work over time?

**Ask Client**:
- Does this meet your goals?
- Are you comfortable with the terms?
- Do you feel this is fair?
- Are you willing to sign and be bound?

### Documenting Agreement

When agreement is reached, document it promptly.

**Memorandum of Understanding**: Mediator often provides written summary of agreement reached

**Follow-up Agreement**: Attorney should follow up with formal settlement agreement incorporating all terms

**Timeline**: Get agreement in final form promptly; don't delay while memories fade

### When No Agreement is Reached

If mediation doesn't result in full agreement:

**Partial Agreements**: Even if not complete, document what was agreed
- This reduces disputed issues for trial
- Represents progress
- Saves litigation time and cost

**Continue Negotiations**: Parties may continue negotiating outside mediation
- With attorneys
- With new mediator
- At later date

**Prepare for Trial**: If full resolution not reached
- Use partial agreements to streamline trial
- Focus trial on remaining disputed issues
- Use mediation discussions to understand other party's likely trial positions

## Mediator Selection and Working with Mediators

### Choosing a Mediator

Consider mediator characteristics:

**Experience**:
- How much family law experience?
- How many mediations has mediator conducted?
- What outcomes does mediator typically achieve?

**Style**:
- **Evaluative mediator**: Provides opinions about likely outcomes
- **Facilitative mediator**: Helps parties communicate without giving opinions
- **Transformative mediator**: Focuses on empowerment and recognition

**Reputation**:
- What do attorneys say about this mediator?
- What do clients say?
- What is mediator's settlement rate?

**Specific Issues**:
- For custody cases: choose mediator with child psychology background?
- For financial cases: choose mediator with accounting/business background?
- For high-conflict: choose mediator with training in high-conflict dynamics?

### Working Effectively with Mediator

**Communication**:
- Provide mediator with case summary in advance
- Be honest in private caucuses
- Tell mediator your realistic expectations
- Listen to mediator's perspective

**Advocating for Client**:
- In joint sessions, present your position clearly
- In private caucuses, explain why you need what you need
- Respond to mediator's suggestions seriously
- Push back if mediator's direction doesn't serve client

**Feedback**:
- If mediation isn't working, discuss with mediator
- Request different approach if current isn't productive
- Consider switching mediators if fundamental mismatch
- Use mediator's experience to improve strategy

## Key Takeaways for Effective Mediation

1. **Prepare Thoroughly**: Analyze each issue, develop realistic ranges, understand law and comparable cases

2. **Develop Strategy**: Know your opening, know your walkaway point, develop package deals

3. **Manage Emotions**: Both your own and client's; recognize emotions are normal but manage them professionally

4. **Focus on Interests**: Move beyond stated positions to underlying interests; find creative solutions

5. **Communicate Clearly**: Help client prepare to communicate effectively and listen

6. **Stay Flexible**: Be willing to move from opening position based on new information and other party's position

7. **Think Long-term**: Focus on what works going forward, not punishing past behavior

8. **Value Settlement**: Most cases settle; mediation is usually the path to resolution

Effective mediation preparation and execution often results in faster, less expensive, and more satisfying resolutions than litigation.
